### Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only

A "1-loop graph" means a connected graph with exactly one cycle.
"Labeled $n$-vertex" means the vertices are distinguishable.
"1-valent and 3-valent vertices only" means all vertices must have degree 1 or 3.

Let $n_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $n_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
The total number of vertices is $n = n_1 + n_3$.
The sum of degrees in any graph must be twice the number of edges ($2E$):
$$ n_1 \cdot 1 + n_3 \cdot 3 = 2E $$
For this sum to be even, $n_1 + 3n_3$ must be even. Since $3n_3 \equiv n_3 \pmod 2$, this implies $n_1 + n_3$ must be even. Therefore, the total number of vertices $n$ must be even. If $n$ is odd, there are no such graphs.

For a connected graph with $V$ vertices and $E$ edges, the number of loops $L = E - V + 1$.
Here $V=n$ and $L=1$, so $E = n$.
Substituting $E=n$ into the sum of degrees equation:
$$ n_1 + 3n_3 = 2n $$
We also have $n_1 = n - n_3$. Substitute this into the equation:
$$ (n - n_3) + 3n_3 = 2n $$
$$ n + 2n_3 = 2n $$
$$ 2n_3 = n \implies n_3 = n/2 $$
And then $n_1 = n - n_3 = n - n/2 = n/2$.
So, for such a graph to exist, it must have exactly $n/2$ 1-valent vertices and $n/2$ 3-valent vertices. This reinforces that $n$ must be even.

Now consider the structure of a 1-loop graph with only 1-valent and 3-valent vertices:
- 1-valent vertices must be leaves (endpoints of edges). They cannot be part of a cycle.
- Therefore, the cycle must be formed entirely by 3-valent vertices.
- Let $k$ be the number of vertices in the cycle. These $k$ vertices are all 3-valent.
- Each of these $k$ cycle vertices has two edges belonging to the cycle. The third edge of each cycle vertex must connect to a 1-valent vertex (a leaf), as there are no other 3-valent vertices not on the cycle.
- So, there are $k$ 3-valent vertices (on the cycle) and $k$ 1-valent vertices (leaves, each connected to one cycle vertex).
- This means $n_3 = k$ and $n_1 = k$. Thus, $n = n_1 + n_3 = 2k$.
- A cycle must have at least 3 vertices, so $k \ge 3$. This implies $n \ge 6$.
- If $n < 6$ (i.e., $n=2$ or $n=4$), or if $n$ is odd, the number of such graphs is 0.

Now we count the number of labeled graphs for $n \ge 6$ and $n$ even:
1.  Choose $k = n/2$ vertices out of $n$ available labeled vertices to form the cycle. There are $\binom{n}{k}$ ways.
2.  Form a labeled cycle with these $k$ chosen vertices. The number of distinct labeled $k$-cycles is $\frac{(k-1)!}{2}$.
3.  The remaining $n-k = k$ vertices are the 1-valent leaves. Each of the $k$ cycle vertices must connect to a unique leaf. This is a perfect matching between two sets of $k$ labeled vertices. There are $k!$ ways to do this.

Multiplying these factors:
$$ N_n = \binom{n}{k} \cdot \frac{(k-1)!}{2} \cdot k! $$
Substitute $k = n/2$:
$$ N_n = \binom{n}{n/2} \cdot \frac{((n/2)-1)!}{2} \cdot (n/2)! $$
$$ N_n = \frac{n!}{(n/2)!(n/2)!} \cdot \frac{((n/2)-1)!}{2} \cdot (n/2)! $$
$$ N_n = \frac{n!}{(n/2)!} \cdot \frac{((n/2)-1)!}{2} $$
Since $(n/2)! = (n/2) \cdot ((n/2)-1)!$:
$$ N_n = \frac{n!}{(n/2) \cdot ((n/2)-1)!} \cdot \frac{((n/2)-1)!}{2} = \frac{n!}{(n/2) \cdot 2} = \frac{n!}{n} = (n-1)! $$

**Check for small numbers of vertices:**
