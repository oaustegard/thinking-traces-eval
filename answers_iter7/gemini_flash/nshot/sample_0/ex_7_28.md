## Reasoning trace

The system is a quantum particle with potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$.
The Euclidean action is given by $S[q] = \int dt \bigl(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\bigr)$.
The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = \int dt \left( -\tfrac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.
Feynman rules:
*   Free propagator: $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
*   Vertex: Each $q^3$ vertex carries a weight $g$ (from $-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar$, where the $1/3!$ in the action cancels the $3!$ leg permutations, leaving just $g$ as the vertex factor).
*   Diagram amplitude: For a given diagram, multiply vertex factors and propagators, integrate over internal vertex times, and divide by the diagram's symmetry factor $S$. External legs are distinct.

### (i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$

The 1-point function is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg. We need to find the leading (lowest order in $g$) non-zero term.

*   **Order $g^0$ (free theory):** There are no vertices, so no diagrams with an external leg can be formed. Thus, $G_1^{(0)}(t) = 0$.

*   **Order $g^1$ (one vertex):**
    *   The diagram must contain one $q^3$ vertex. This vertex has three half-edges.
    *   One half-edge connects to the external leg at time $t$.
    *   The remaining two half-edges must connect to each other to form a closed loop (a "tadpole").
    *   **Diagram:** A single vertex with one external leg and one self-loop.
    *   **Vertex factor:** $g$.
    *   **Propagator:** The self-loop is $G_0(t_v, t_v)$, where $t_v$ is the time of the vertex.
    *   **Symmetry factor $S$:** The self-loop has a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (swapping the two ends of the loop edge leaves the diagram unchanged). So, $S=2$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{S} \int dt_v \, \delta(t - t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v)$$
        $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \, G_0(t,t)$$
    *   Using $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
        $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m}.$$

Since $G_1^{(0)}(t) = 0$, the $g^1$ term is the leading non-zero contribution.

### (ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$

The connected 2-point function is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs. We need to calculate this up to order $g^2$.

*   **Order $g^0$ (free theory):**
    *   The free propagator itself is a connected 2-point function.
    *   $$G_2^{c(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

*   **Order $g^1$ (one vertex):**
    *   A single $q^3$ vertex has three half-edges. If two half-edges are external (to $t_1$ and $t_2$), only one half-edge remains. This single remaining half-edge cannot form a closed loop (which requires two half-edges) nor connect to another vertex (as we are at order $g^1$).
    *   Therefore, there are no connected 2-point function diagrams at order $g^1$ for a cubic interaction.
    *   $$G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) = 0.$$

*   **Order $g^2$ (two vertices):**
    *   We need diagrams with two $q^3$ vertices (at times $t_a, t_b$) and two external legs (at $t_1, t_2$).
    *   Each vertex has 3 half-edges, so there are a total of 6 half-edges. Two are external, so 4 internal half-edges remain.
    *   These 4 internal half-edges must form internal propagators connecting the two vertices.
    *   **Diagram:** Each vertex has one external leg and two internal legs. The two internal legs from $V_a$ connect to the two internal legs from $V_b$. This forms an "eye" or "double-bubble" diagram.
    *   **Vertex factors:** $g$ for $V_a$ and $g$ for $V_b$, so $g^2$.
    *   **Propagators:** Two propagators connect $V_a$ and $V_b$, so $[G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2$.
    *   **Symmetry factor $S$:** The two internal propagators connecting $V_a$ and $V_b$ are indistinguishable. Swapping them leaves the diagram unchanged. So, $S=2$. (The two vertices are effectively distinguished by their external connections to $t_1$ and $t_2$, so there is no $2!$ symmetry for swapping vertices).
    *   **Contribution:**
        $$G_2^{c(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{S} \int dt_a dt_b \, \delta(t_1 - t_a) \, \delta(t_2 - t_b) \, [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2$$
        $$G_2^{c(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \, [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2$$
    *   Substituting $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$:
        $$G_2^{c(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} \right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{2} \frac{1}{4m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Total connected 2-point function up to $g^2$:**
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^{c(0)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c(2)}(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3)$$
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + 0 + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} + O(g^3).$$

## Final answer

(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ is:
$$\boxed{\;G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} + O(g^2).\;}$$

(ii) The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ is:
$$\boxed{\;G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} + O(g^3).\;}$$
