## Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$

**Problem.** For $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{6})$, compute the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$, where $Z = \int e^{-S(x)/\hbar} dx$ and $Z_0$ is the free ($g=0$) partition function.

## Reasoning trace (Part 1)

The action is $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - gx^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
We can rewrite this as $S(x) = \tfrac{1-2g}{2}x^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
The free action (for $g=0$) is $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$. Its partition function is $Z_0 = \int e^{-x^2/(2\hbar)} dx = \sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$.

For a general action $S(x)$, the 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ can be computed by expanding $S(x)$ around its classical minimum. However, for $\log(Z/Z_0)$, a direct formula is available from the expansion of the path integral.
Let $S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}S_0''(0)x^2 + S_{\text{int}}(x)$. The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is:
$$ \log(Z/Z_0)|_{1\text{-loop}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\log\left(\frac{S_0''(0)}{S''(0)}\right) - \frac{1}{\hbar}S_{\text{int}}(0) - \frac{1}{\hbar}\frac{S_{\text{int}}'(0)^2}{2S_0''(0)} + O(\hbar^2) $$
In our case, the full action is $S(x) = \tfrac{1-2g}{2}x^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
The free action is $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$. So $S_0''(0) = 1$.
The full second derivative is $S''(x) = (1-2g) - gx$. Thus $S''(0) = 1-2g$.
The interaction part relative to $S_0(x)$ is $S_{\text{int}}(x) = -gx^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
We evaluate its derivatives at $x=0$:
$S_{\text{int}}(0) = 0$.
$S_{\text{int}}'(x) = -2gx - \tfrac{g}{2}x^2$, so $S_{\text{int}}'(0) = 0$.
$S_{\text{int}}''(x) = -2g - gx$, so $S_{\text{int}}''(0) = -2g$.

Plugging these into the formula:
$$ \log(Z/Z_0)|_{1\text{-loop}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\log\left(\frac{1}{1-2g}\right) - 0 - 0 = -\tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-2g). $$
This result can be expanded as a series:
$$ -\tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-2g) = -\tfrac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{-(2g)^k}{k} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(2g)^k}{2k}. $$
Each term $\frac{(2g)^k}{2k}$ corresponds to a 1-loop vacuum Feynman diagram: a cycle of $k$ 2-valent vertices. Each 2-valent vertex comes from the $-gx^2$ term in the action, contributing a factor of $2g$ (from $e^{gx^2/\hbar}$ in the exponent, $1/2!$ for $x^2$ is cancelled by $2!$ permutations, so $g$ for $x^2/2$, but the $x^2$ term is $-gx^2$, so $2g$). The factor $1/(2k)$ is the symmetry factor for a $k$-cycle.

## Final answer (Part 1)

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is
$$\boxed{\log(Z/Z_0)|_{1\text{-loop}} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g).}$$

## Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices

**Problem.** Compute the number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only. Check your answer by directly enumerating such graphs with small numbers of vertices.

## Reasoning trace (Part 2)

Let $a_n$ be the number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with only 1-valent and 3-valent vertices.
Let $V_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $V_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
Total vertices $n = V_1 + V_3$.
For a connected graph with $L$ loops, Euler's formula is $V - E + L = 1$. Here $L=1$, so $V - E = 0 \implies V = E$.
The sum of valences is $1 \cdot V_1 + 3 \cdot V_3 = 2E$.
Substituting $E=V=V_1+V_3$:
$V_1 + 3V_3 = 2(V_1 + V_3)$
$V_1 + 3V_3 = 2V_1 + 2V_3$
$V_3 = V_1$.
So, any such graph must have an equal number of 1-valent and 3-valent vertices. Let this number be $m = V_1 = V_3$.
Then $n = V_1 + V_3 = 2m$. This implies $n$ must be an even number. If $n$ is odd, $a_n=0$.

These graphs consist of a cycle of $m$ 3-valent vertices, with each 3-valent vertex connected to a distinct 1-valent vertex. The 1-valent vertices are "leaves" attached to the cycle.

Let's enumerate for small values of $n$:

*   **$n=2$ ($m=1$):** $V_1=1, V_3=1$.
    Let the vertices be labeled 1 and 2.
    If 1 is 3-valent and 2 is 1-valent: vertex 1 must connect to 2 (1 edge). To be 3-valent, it needs two more edges. These must form a self-loop on 1. So edges are $(1,2)$ and $(1,1)$. This is a 1-loop graph ($V=2, E=2 \implies L=1$).
    If 2 is 3-valent and 1 is 1-valent: edges are $(2,1)$ and $(2,2)$.
    These are two distinct labeled graphs. So $a_2 = 2$.

*   **$n=4$ ($m=2$):** $V_1=2, V_3=2$.
    Choose 2 vertices to be 3-valent out of 4: $\binom{4}{2}=6$ ways. Let them be $v_1, v_2$. The other two $l_1, l_2$ are 1-valent.
    The 1-valent vertices must connect to the 3-valent vertices. There are $2!$ ways to pair them (e.g., $v_1 \leftrightarrow l_1$ and $v_2 \leftrightarrow l_2$, or $v_1 \leftrightarrow l_2$ and $v_2 \leftrightarrow l_1$).
    The two 3-valent vertices $v_1, v_2$ have two remaining half-edges each. These must connect to each other to form a cycle. For $m=2$, this means two edges between $v_1$ and $v_2$ (a "double edge"). There is 1 way to form this structure.
    Total: $\binom{4}{2} \times 2! \times 1 = 6 \times 2 \times 1 = 12$. So $a_4 = 12$.

*   **$n=6$ ($m=3$):** $V_1=3, V_3=3$.
    Choose 3 vertices to be 3-valent out of 6: $\binom{6}{3}=20$ ways. Let them be $v_1, v_2, v_3$. The other three $l_1, l_2, l_3$ are 1-valent.
    Pair the 1-valent vertices with the 3-valent vertices: $3! = 6$ ways.
    The three 3-valent vertices $v_1, v_2, v_3$ have two remaining half-edges each. These must form a cycle of length 3 (a triangle). The number of ways to form a labeled 3-cycle on 3 vertices is $(3-1)!/2 = 1$.
    Total: $\binom{6}{3} \times 3! \times 1 = 20 \times 6 \times 1 = 120$. So $a_6 = 120$.
    This matches $(6-1)! = 5! = 120$.

General formula for $n=2m$ ($m \ge 3$):
$a_{2m} = \binom{2m}{m} \times m! \times \frac{(m-1)!}{2}$.
The term $\binom{2m}{m}$ chooses the $m$ 3-valent vertices.
The term $m!$ pairs the $m$ 1-valent vertices with the $m$ 3-valent vertices.
The term $\frac{(m-1)!}{2}$ counts the number of ways to form a simple labeled $m$-cycle on the $m$ 3-valent vertices.
$a_{2m} = \frac{(2m)!}{m!(2m-m)!} \times m! \times \frac{(m-1)!}{2} = \frac{(2m)!}{m!} \times \frac{(m-1)!}{2} = \frac{(2m)!}{2m} = (2m-1)!$.
This formula holds for $m \ge 3$.

Summary of $a_n$:
*   $a_n = 0$ if $n$ is odd.
*   $a_2 = 2$.
*   $a_4 = 12$.
*   $a_n = (n-1)!$ for $n \ge 6$ and $n$ is even.

Let $A(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n \frac{z^n}{n!}$ be the exponential generating function (EGF) for these graphs.
$A(z) = \frac{a_2 z^2}{2!} + \frac{a_4 z^4}{4!} + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{a_{2m} z^{2m}}{(2m)!}$
$A(z) = \frac{2 z^2}{2!} + \frac{12 z^4}{4!} + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{(2m-1)! z^{2m}}{(2m)!}$
$A(z) = z^2 + \frac{12}{24}z^4 + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$
$A(z) = z^2 + \frac{z^4}{2} + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$.

We know that $\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{x^k}{k} = -\log(1-x)$.
So $\sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2)$.
This sum can be written as $\frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^4}{4} + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$.
Substituting this into $A(z)$:
$A(z) = z^2 + \frac{z^4}{2} + \left( -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2) - \frac{z^2}{2} - \frac{z^4}{4} \right)$
$A(z) = \left(z^2 - \frac{z^2}{2}\right) + \left(\frac{z^4}{2} - \frac{z^4}{4}\right) - \frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2)$
$$A(z) = \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^4}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2).$$

**Using the QFT result to compute:**
The QFT result from Part 1 is $\log(Z/Z_0)|_{1\text{-loop}} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)$.
If we make the substitution $2g = z^2$, then the QFT result becomes $-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2)$.
This is the logarithmic part of $A(z)$.
The QFT formula counts cycles of "effective 2-valent vertices" where each vertex carries a factor $2g$.
The combinatorial graphs are cycles of "units", where each unit consists of a 3-valent vertex connected to a 1-valent vertex. Each unit contributes $z^2$ to the EGF (one $z$ for the 3-valent vertex, one for the 1-valent vertex).
So the substitution $2g=z^2$ maps the QFT formula to the combinatorial EGF for $m \ge 3$ terms:
$\sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$.
The QFT result (with $2g=z^2$) is $\sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$.
The combinatorial EGF is $A(z) = \sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{a_{2m}}{(2m)!} z^{2m}$.
We found $a_2/2! = 1$, $a_4/4! = 1/2$, and $a_{2m}/(2m)! = 1/(2m)$ for $m \ge 3$.
So $A(z) = z^2 + \frac{z^4}{2} + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$.
Comparing $A(z)$ with $-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2) = \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^4}{4} + \sum_{m=3}^\infty \frac{z^{2m}}{2m}$:
$$A(z) = \left(-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2)\right) + \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^4}{4}.$$
This means the combinatorial enumeration $A(z)$ is equal to the QFT result (with $2g=z^2$) plus correction terms for the $m=1$ and $m=2$ cases ($z^2/2$ and $z^4/4$ respectively), which arise because the cycle definitions for small $m$ (self-loop, double edge) differ from the generic "simple cycle" factor $(m-1)!/2$.

## Final answer (Part 2)

The number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only, denoted by $a_n$, is:
*   $a_n = 0$ if $n$ is odd.
*   $a_2 = 2$.
*   $a_4 = 12$.
*   $a_n = (n-1)!$ if $n \ge 6$ and $n$ is even.

The exponential generating function (EGF) for these graphs is $A(z) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n \frac{z^n}{n!}$:
$$\boxed{A(z) = \frac{z^2}{2} + \frac{z^4}{4} - \frac{1}{2}\log(1-z^2).}$$
