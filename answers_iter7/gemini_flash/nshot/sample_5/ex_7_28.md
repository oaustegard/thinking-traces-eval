## Reasoning trace

**Setup.** The Euclidean action for the quantum particle is given by
$$S[q] = \int dt \left(\frac{1}{2} \dot{q}(t)^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q(t)^2 - \frac{g}{6} q(t)^3\right).$$
We assume $\hbar=1$.
The free part of the action is $S_0[q] = \int dt \left(\frac{1}{2} \dot{q}(t)^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q(t)^2\right)$.
The interaction part of the action is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\frac{g}{6} \int dt\, q(t)^3$.

**Feynman rules for the cubic interaction.**
The vertex factor for a $q^3$ interaction, from the expansion $e^{-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar} = e^{\frac{g}{6}\int q^3 dt}$, is $g$. (The $1/6$ in the action cancels the $3!$ permutations of legs at the vertex up to symmetry factors).
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

**Useful integrals/Fourier transforms.**
The Fourier transform of $G_0(t)$ is $G_0(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2}$.
The inverse Fourier transform $\int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{-i\omega x}}{(\omega^2+m^2)^n}$ is given by:
For $n=1$: $\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|x|}$ (this is $G_0(x)$).
For $n=2$: $\frac{1}{4m^3} (1 + m|x|) e^{-m|x|}$.
For $n=3$: $\frac{1}{64m^5} (12 + 12m|x| + 4m^2x^2) e^{-m|x|}$.

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$.**

The 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg.

**Order $g^0$ (free theory).**
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(0)} = 0$, as there are no sources or interactions in the free theory.

**Order $g^1$.**
The lowest order non-zero contribution comes from a single vertex. A 3-valent vertex has three half-edges. For a 1-point function, one half-edge is external (connected to $t$). The remaining two half-edges must form a closed loop (a "tadpole").
Diagram: A single 3-valent vertex at time $t_v$, with one leg to $t$ and one internal loop.
Feynman factors:
- Vertex factor: $g$.
- External propagator: $G_0(t, t_v)$.
- Internal loop propagator: $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
- Symmetry factor: $S=2$ (due to the indistinguishability of the two ends of the loop).

So, $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{1}{S} \int dt_v \, g \, G_0(t, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
We know $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, \frac{1}{2m} \, G_0(t, t_v) = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v)$.
The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t_v|} dt_v = \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
Therefore,
$$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{4m} \cdot \frac{1}{m^2} = \frac{g}{4m^3}.$$
This is the leading (order $g$) term. Higher order terms involve more vertices and loops.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is related to the 1-particle irreducible (1PI) 2-point function (self-energy) $\Sigma(t_1, t_2)$ via Dyson's equation:
$$G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$$
We need to calculate this up to order $g^2$. This means we need $\Sigma$ up to order $g^2$.

**1PI 2-point function $\Sigma(t_1, t_2)$.**
$\Sigma$ is the sum of all 1PI diagrams with two amputated external legs.

**Order $g^0$.** $\Sigma^{(0)} = 0$.

**Order $g^1$.**
Diagram: One vertex, with two external legs (amputated) and one internal loop.
Vertex factor: $g$. Internal loop: $G_0(t_v, t_v)$. External legs are just points $t_1, t_2$.
The diagram is local: the vertex is effectively at $t_1$ and $t_2$ simultaneously.
$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{2} G_0(t_1, t_1) \delta(t_1-t_2) = \frac{g}{2} \frac{1}{2m} \delta(t_1-t_2) = \frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_1-t_2)$.
This represents a mass renormalization.

**Order $g^2$.**
Diagram: Two vertices, with two external legs (amputated). Must be 1PI (cannot be disconnected by cutting a single internal line).
The only 1PI diagram is two vertices connected by two propagators (a "double-line" or "bubble" diagram).
$t_1 \stackrel{\text{2 lines}}{\longleftrightarrow} t_2$.
Feynman factors:
- Vertex factors: $g^2$.
- Two internal propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_2)^2$.
- Symmetry factor: $S=2$ (the two propagators are indistinguishable).
So,
$$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2 = \frac{g^2}{2} \left(\frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{2m}\right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

**Calculating $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ using Dyson's equation.**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2)}$.

**Order $g^0$.**
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$.**
This term comes from $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \int dt_x dt_y G_0(t_1, t_x) \Sigma^{(1)}(t_x, t_y) G_0(t_y, t_2).$$
Substitute $\Sigma^{(1)}$:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \int dt_x dt_y G_0(t_1, t_x) \left(\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_x-t_y)\right) G_0(t_y, t_2).$$
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_x G_0(t_1, t_x) G_0(t_x, t_2).$$
This is the convolution of two free propagators. Using the Fourier transform integral for $n=2$:
$\int dt_x G_0(t_1, t_x) G_0(t_x, t_2) = \frac{1}{4m^3} (1 + m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
So,
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \frac{g}{4m} \cdot \frac{1}{4m^3} (1 + m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{g}{16m^4} (1 + m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^2$.**
There are two contributions to order $g^2$:
1. $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0$:
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2a)} = \int dt_x dt_y G_0(t_1, t_x) \Sigma^{(2)}(t_x, t_y) G_0(t_y, t_2).$$
   Substitute $\Sigma^{(2)}$:
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2a)} = \int dt_x dt_y G_0(t_1, t_x) \left(\frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|}\right) G_0(t_y, t_2).$$
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2a)} = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \int dt_x dt_y \frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_x|}}{2m} e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|} \frac{e^{-m|t_y-t_2|}}{2m}.$$
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2a)} = \frac{g^2}{32m^4} \int dt_x dt_y e^{-m|t_1-t_x|} e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|} e^{-m|t_y-t_2|}.$$
   This integral is equivalent to $\int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} e^{-i\omega(t_1-t_2)} G_0(\omega) G_0(2\omega) G_0(\omega)$ - wait, $e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|}$ is not $G_0(2\omega)$. It is $\frac{1}{2m} e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|}$ is $G_0(t_x,t_y)$ with mass $2m$.
   So the middle term is $G_0(t_x,t_y; 2m)$.
   $\int dt_x dt_y G_0(t_1, t_x; m) G_0(t_x, t_y; 2m) G_0(t_y, t_2; m)$.
   This is $\frac{1}{2m} \int dt_x e^{-m|t_1-t_x|} \frac{1}{2(2m)} \int dt_y e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_y-t_2|}$.
   The Fourier transform is $\int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{1}{\omega^2+m^2} \frac{1}{\omega^2+(2m)^2} \frac{1}{\omega^2+m^2}$.
   This integral can be evaluated, but it is simpler to use the form calculated in the thought process:
   $\int dt_x e^{-m|t_a-t_x|} e^{-2m|t_x-t_b|} = \frac{1}{m} \left( \frac{5}{3} e^{-m|t_a-t_b|} - e^{-2m|t_a-t_b|} \right)$.
   So, $\int dt_x dt_y e^{-m|t_1-t_x|} e^{-2m|t_x-t_y|} e^{-m|t_y-t_2|} = \frac{1}{m} \left( \frac{5}{3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right)$.
   Thus,
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2a)} = \frac{g^2}{32m^4} \cdot \frac{1}{m} \left( \frac{5}{3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right) = \frac{g^2}{32m^5} \left( \frac{5}{3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right).$$

2. $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$:
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2b)} = \int dt_x dt_y dt_z dt_w G_0(t_1, t_x) \Sigma^{(1)}(t_x, t_y) G_0(t_y, t_z) \Sigma^{(1)}(t_z, t_w) G_0(t_w, t_2).$$
   Substitute $\Sigma^{(1)}$:
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2b)} = \int dt_x dt_y dt_z dt_w G_0(t_1, t_x) \left(\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_x-t_y)\right) G_0(t_y, t_z) \left(\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_z-t_w)\right) G_0(t_w, t_2).$$
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2b)} = \left(\frac{g}{4m}\right)^2 \int dt_x dt_z G_0(t_1, t_x) G_0(t_x, t_z) G_0(t_z, t_2).$$
   This is the convolution of three free propagators. Using the Fourier transform integral for $n=3$:
   $\int dt_x dt_z G_0(t_1, t_x) G_0(t_x, t_z) G_0(t_z, t_2) = \frac{1}{(2m)^3} \cdot \frac{1}{64m^5} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
   $= \frac{1}{8m^3} \cdot \frac{1}{64m^5} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
   $= \frac{1}{512m^8} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
   So,
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2b)} = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \cdot \frac{1}{512m^8} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2b)} = \frac{g^2}{8192m^{10}} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

**Total connected 2-point function up to $g^2$.**
Summing all contributions:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2a)} + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^2b)}.$$

Group terms by the exponential factors:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \left[ \frac{1}{2m} + \frac{g}{16m^4} (1 + m|t_1-t_2|) + \frac{5g^2}{96m^5} + \frac{g^2}{8192m^{10}} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) \right] e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$$
$$- \frac{g^2}{32m^5} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).$$

The final expressions are quite lengthy due to the explicit time dependence and many powers of $m$.

## Final answer

**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$:**
The leading non-zero term is of order $g^1$. It arises from a single 3-valent vertex where one leg is external and the other two form a closed loop (a tadpole).
$$\boxed{\;\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2).\;}$$

**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$:**
The connected 2-point function is calculated using Dyson's equation $G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$, where $\Sigma$ is the 1-particle irreducible (1PI) 2-point function.

The 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma(t_1, t_2)$ up to order $g^2$ is:
$$\Sigma(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_1-t_2) + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).$$

Substituting this into Dyson's equation and collecting terms up to $O(g^2)$:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 + O(g^3).$$

Explicitly, with $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$:
$$\boxed{\;G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \left[ \frac{1}{2m} + \frac{g}{16m^4} (1 + m|t_1-t_2|) + \frac{5g^2}{96m^5} + \frac{g^2}{8192m^{10}} (12 + 12m|t_1-t_2| + 4m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) \right] e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - \frac{g^2}{32m^5} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).\;}$$
