### Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for $S(x) = x^2/2 - g(x^2 + x^3/6)$

The action is given by $S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})$.
We can rearrange this as $S(x) = \frac{1}{2}(1-g)x^2 - \frac{g}{6}x^3$.
The partition function is $Z = \int e^{-S(x)/\hbar} dx$. The free partition function $Z_0$ corresponds to $g=0$, so $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
In zero-dimensional quantum field theory, for an action of the form $S(x) = \frac{1}{2} K x^2 + O(x^3)$, the 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is $-\frac{1}{2}\log K$.
In our case, the coefficient of $x^2/2$ is $K = (1-g)$.
Therefore, $\log Z = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-g) + \text{const}$.
For the free theory ($g=0$), $K=1$, so $\log Z_0 = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1) + \text{const} = \text{const}$.
Thus, the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is $\boxed{-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-g)}$.

**Verification using Feynman diagrams:**
The interaction part of the action is $S_{\text{int}}(x) = -g x^2 - \frac{g}{6}x^3$. In the path integral $e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$, the interaction terms appear as $e^{(gx^2 + gx^3/6)/\hbar}$. This implies two types of vertices:
1.  A 2-valent vertex with Feynman weight $g$.
2.  A 3-valent vertex with Feynman weight $g$.
The propagator (from the $x^2/2$ term) is 1.

We are looking for 1-loop vacuum diagrams. These are connected diagrams with no external legs and exactly one loop. Let $N_2$ be the number of 2-valent vertices and $N_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices. The total number of vertices is $N = N_2 + N_3$. For a 1-loop graph, the number of edges $E$ must equal $N$.
The sum of valences is $2N_2 + 3N_3$. This must equal $2E = 2N = 2(N_2 + N_3)$.
$2N_2 + 3N_3 = 2N_2 + 2N_3 \implies N_3 = 0$.
This means that any 1-loop vacuum diagram can only consist of 2-valent vertices. Such a diagram is a cycle (a $k$-gon) of $k$ 2-valent vertices.
For a $k$-gon:
*   Each vertex contributes a factor $g$. So the total vertex factor is $g^k$.
*   The symmetry factor (automorphism group order) for a $k$-gon is $2k$ (dihedral group $D_k$).
The contribution of such a $k$-gon to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is $g^k / (2k)$.
Summing over all possible $k \ge 1$:
$$ \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{g^k}{2k} = \frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{g^k}{k} = -\frac{1}{2} \log(1-g). $$
This diagrammatic sum confirms the previous result.

### Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only

Let $a_n$ be the number of such graphs.
A "1-loop graph" is defined as a connected graph with $n$ vertices and $n$ edges.
Let $N_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $N_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices. The total number of vertices is $n = N_1 + N_3$.
The sum of valences is $1 \cdot N_1 + 3 \cdot N_3$. This must equal $2E = 2n$.
So $N_1 + 3N_3 = 2(N_1 + N_3)$.
This simplifies to $N_3 = N_1$.
Thus, $n = N_1 + N_3 = 2N_1$. This implies that $n$ must be an **even** number. If $n$ is odd, $a_n = 0$.
Let $n = 2m$, so $N_1 = N_3 = m$.

The graph structure: We have $m$ 3-valent vertices and $m$ 1-valent vertices. The graph has $n=2m$ edges.
Each of the $m$ 1-valent vertices must be an endpoint of exactly one edge. This accounts for $m$ edges.
The remaining $2m - m = m$ edges must connect the $m$ 3-valent vertices.
Since each 3-valent vertex has 3 incident edges, and one edge must connect to a 1-valent vertex (otherwise it would be 2-valent or 0-valent), the remaining two edges for each 3-valent vertex must connect to other 3-valent vertices.
This implies that the $m$ 3-valent vertices form a cycle of length $m$. Each vertex in this cycle is also connected to a unique 1-valent vertex.

We count these labeled graphs in three steps:
1.  **Choose which $m$ vertices out of $n$ are 3-valent.** The remaining $m$ vertices will be 1-valent. There are $\binom{n}{m} = \binom{2m}{m}$ ways to do this.
2.  **Form a cycle with the $m$ chosen 3-valent vertices.** The number of ways to form a cycle on $m$ *labeled* vertices depends on $m$:
    *   If $m=1$ ($n=2$): The cycle is a self-loop on the single 3-valent vertex. There is $1$ way to form this.
    *   If $m=2$ ($n=4$): The cycle is a double edge between the two 3-valent vertices. There is $1$ way to form this.
    *   If $m \ge 3$ ($n \ge 6$): The cycle is a simple cycle of $m$ vertices. There are $\frac{(m-1)!}{2}$ ways to form this.
3.  **Connect the remaining $m$ half-edges from the 3-valent vertices to the $m$ 1-valent vertices.** Each of the $m$ 3-valent vertices has one half-edge remaining (after forming the cycle), and each of the $m$ 1-valent vertices needs one connection. There are $m!$ ways to pair them up.

Now we compute $a_n$ for $n=2m$:

*   **If $n$ is odd:** $a_n = 0$.

*   **If $n=2$ ($m=1$):**
    $a_2 = \binom{2}{1} \times (\text{cycle factor for } m=1) \times 1! = 2 \times 1 \times 1 = 2$.
    *Check:* Two labeled vertices $\{1,2\}$. Either $1$ is 1-valent and $2$ is 3-valent (edge $(1,2)$ and self-loop on $2$), or $2$ is 1-valent and $1$ is 3-valent (edge $(1,2)$ and self-loop on $1$). These are 2 distinct graphs.

*   **If $n=4$ ($m=2$):**
    $a_4 = \binom{4}{2} \times (\text{cycle factor for } m=2) \times 2! = 6 \times 1 \times 2 = 12$.
    *Check:* Four labeled vertices $\{1,2,3,4\}$. Choose two 3-valent (e.g., $\{1,2\}$) in $\binom{4}{2}=6$ ways. The remaining two $(\{3,4\})$ are 1-valent. The 3-valent vertices $\{1,2\}$ form a double edge. The remaining half-edges of $1$ and $2$ must connect to $3$ and $4$. There are $2!$ ways: $(1,3)-(2,4)$ or $(1,4)-(2,3)$. So $6 \times 2 = 12$ graphs.

*   **If $n \ge 6$ ($m \ge 3$):**
    $a_n = \binom{n}{m} \times \frac{(m-1)!}{2} \times m!$.
    $a_n = \frac{n!}{m!m!} \times \frac{(m-1)!}{2} \times m! = \frac{n! (m-1)!}{2 m!} = \frac{n!}{2m}$.
    Since $n=2m$, we can substitute $m=n/2$: $a_n = \frac{n!}{n} = (n-1)!$.
    *Check for $n=6$ ($m=3$):* $a_6 = (6-1)! = 5! = 120$.
    Six labeled vertices $\{1,\dots,6\}$. Choose three 3-valent (e.g., $\{1,2,3\}$) in $\binom{6}{3}=20$ ways. The remaining three $(\{4,5,6\})$ are 1-valent. The 3-valent vertices $\{1,2,3\}$ form a triangle (cycle $1-2-3-1$). There is $\frac{(3-1)!}{2}=1$ way. The remaining half-edges of $1,2,3$ must connect to $4,5,6$. There are $3!=6$ ways to pair them. So $20 \times 1 \times 6 = 120$ graphs.
