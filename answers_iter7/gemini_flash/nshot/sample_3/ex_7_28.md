## Reasoning trace

**Setup.** The Euclidean action for the cubic anharmonic oscillator is
$$S[q] = \int dt \left(\frac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3\right).$$
The interaction part is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\int dt \frac{g}{6} q^3$.
The free propagator (for mass $m$) is
$$G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Feynman rules:**
*   Each $q^3$ vertex carries a weight of $+g$ (from $-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar$ expansion, with the $1/3!$ in the action canceling the $3!$ leg-permutation count up to symmetry factors).
*   Each internal line is a free propagator $G_0(t_a, t_b)$.
*   For a diagram with a symmetry factor $S$ (number of automorphisms), divide by $S$.

---

### Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$

The 1-point function is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg.

**Order $g^0$ (free theory).** For $g=0$, the potential $U(q) = m^2 q^2 / 2$ is symmetric about $q=0$. Thus, $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

**Order $g^1$.** We need one vertex. The $q^3$ vertex has 3 half-edges. One half-edge connects to the external leg at time $t$. The remaining two half-edges must contract with each other to form a loop.
*   **Diagram:** A single 3-valent vertex at time $t_v$, with one leg connected to $t$ and the other two forming a loop.
*   **Vertex factor:** $g$.
*   **Propagator:** The internal loop contributes $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
*   **Symmetry factor:** The diagram consists of a vertex with one external leg and one internal loop. There are 3 ways to choose which leg connects to $t$. The remaining 2 legs form a loop. The loop itself has a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (interchanging its ends). So the symmetry factor for the diagram is $S=2$.
*   **Contribution:**
    $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \int dt_v \, g \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t_v, t_v) \, \delta(t - t_v).$$
    Performing the $\delta$-integration:
    $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \, G_0(t, t).$$
    Substituting $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
    $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m}.$$
This is the leading term of $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$.

---

### Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$

The connected 2-point function is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs.

**Order $g^0$.** This is the free propagator:
$$G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$.** We need one vertex. A 3-valent vertex has 3 half-edges. For two external legs ($q(t_1), q(t_2)$), only one half-edge remains. This single half-edge cannot form a loop or connect to another external leg (as there are only two). Therefore, no connected diagram with one $q^3$ vertex contributes to $G_2^c$.
$$G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = 0.$$

**Order $g^2$.** We need two vertices. The relevant diagrams for the connected 2-point function are described by the Dyson equation: $G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \ldots$, where $\Sigma$ is the 1-particle irreducible (1PI) 2-point function (self-energy). We need to calculate $\Sigma^{(2)}$.
*   **1PI Self-Energy $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_v, t_{v'})$:** This is the sum of all 1PI diagrams with two amputated external legs.
    *   **Diagram:** Two 3-valent vertices, at times $t_v$ and $t_{v'}$, connected by two internal propagators. Each vertex uses 2 half-edges for these internal propagators, leaving 1 half-edge free to connect to an external leg (which is amputated for $\Sigma$).
    *   **Vertex factors:** $g \cdot g = g^2$.
    *   **Propagators:** Two propagators between $t_v$ and $t_{v'}$, so $G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^2$.
    *   **Symmetry factor:** The two propagators connecting the two vertices are indistinguishable. So the symmetry factor for this diagram is $S=2$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        $$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_v, t_{v'}) = g^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^2.$$
        Substituting $G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v - t_{v'}|}$:
        $$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_v, t_{v'}) = \frac{g^2}{2} \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v - t_{v'}|}\right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_v - t_{v'}|}.$$

*   **Dressed Propagator (order $g^2$).** The $O(g^2)$ contribution to $G_2^c$ is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0$:
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) \, \Sigma^{(2)}(t_v, t_{v'}) \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_2).$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_v dt_{v'} \left(\frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_v|}}{2m}\right) \left(\frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_v-t_{v'}|}\right) \left(\frac{e^{-m|t_{v'}-t_2|}}{2m}\right).$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{32m^4} \int dt_v dt_{v'} e^{-m|t_1-t_v|} e^{-2m|t_v-t_{v'}|} e^{-m|t_{v'}-t_2|}.$$
    This integral is a standard convolution. Let $T = |t_1 - t_2|$. We can evaluate it using Fourier transforms. The Fourier transform of $e^{-A|t|}$ is $\frac{2A}{\omega^2+A^2}$. The integral is the inverse Fourier transform of the product of the Fourier transforms:
    $$I(T) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[\frac{2m}{\omega^2+m^2} \cdot \frac{4m}{\omega^2+(2m)^2} \cdot \frac{2m}{\omega^2+m^2}\right](T) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[\frac{16m^3}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+4m^2)}\right](T).$$
    Let $J(T) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int d\omega \frac{e^{-i\omega T}}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+4m^2)}$.
    Using partial fraction decomposition:
    $$\frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+4m^2)} = -\frac{1}{9m^4(\omega^2+m^2)} + \frac{1}{3m^2(\omega^2+m^2)^2} + \frac{1}{9m^4(\omega^2+4m^2)}.$$
    Using inverse Fourier transforms $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[\frac{1}{\omega^2+A^2}\right] = \frac{1}{2A}e^{-A|T|}$ and $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[\frac{1}{(\omega^2+A^2)^2}\right] = \frac{e^{-A|T|}}{4A^3}(1+A|T|)$:
    $$J(T) = -\frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{e^{-m|T|}}{2m} + \frac{1}{3m^2} \frac{e^{-m|T|}}{4m^3}(1+m|T|) + \frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{e^{-2m|T|}}{2(2m)}.$$
    $$J(T) = \frac{e^{-m|T|}}{m^5} \left(-\frac{1}{18} + \frac{1+m|T|}{12}\right) + \frac{e^{-2m|T|}}{18m^5} = \frac{e^{-m|T|}}{m^5} \left(\frac{1+3m|T|}{36}\right) + \frac{e^{-2m|T|}}{18m^5}.$$
    Substituting $I(T) = 16m^3 J(T)$ back into $G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2)$:
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{32m^4} \cdot 16m^3 J(|t_1-t_2|) = \frac{g^2}{2m} J(|t_1-t_2|).$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2m} \left[ \frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{m^5} \left(\frac{1+3m|t_1-t_2|}{36}\right) + \frac{e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}}{18m^5} \right].$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{72m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) + \frac{g^2}{36m^6} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

The total connected 2-point function up to order $g^2$ is the sum of the $O(g^0)$ and $O(g^2)$ terms:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3).$$

---

## Final answer

**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$:**
The leading term is of order $g^1$.
$$\boxed{\;G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} + O(g^2).\;}$$

**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$:**
The connected 2-point function is
$$\boxed{\;G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{72m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) + \frac{g^2}{36m^6} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).\;}$$
