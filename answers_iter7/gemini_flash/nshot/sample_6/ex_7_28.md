## Reasoning trace

The Euclidean action for the quantum particle is given by $S[q] = \int dt \bigl(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\bigr)$.
We split this into a free part $S_0[q] = \int dt \bigl(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2\bigr)$ and an interaction part $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\int dt \tfrac{g}{6} q^3$.
The Feynman rules for this theory are:
1. Free propagator: $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
   A special case is the coincident-time propagator: $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
2. Vertex factor: Each $q^3$ vertex from $-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar$ contributes a factor of $+g$. (The $1/3!$ from the action's $q^3/3!$ cancels the $3!$ permutations of legs at the vertex in the Wick expansion, up to symmetry factors of the diagram).

### Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$

The 1-point function is given by the path integral:
$$G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q\, q(t) e^{-S[q]}}{\int \mathcal{D}q\, e^{-S[q]}} = \frac{\langle q(t) e^{-S_{\text{int}}} \rangle_0}{\langle e^{-S_{\text{int}}} \rangle_0}.$$
We expand this expression in powers of $g$.

**Numerator expansion:**
$\langle q(t) e^{-S_{\text{int}}} \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \left\langle q(t) \left(\frac{g}{6} \int dt_v q(t_v)^3\right) \right\rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.
- **Order $g^0$:** $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$, as the free theory is Gaussian with zero mean.
- **Order $g^1$:**
  $\left\langle q(t) \left(\frac{g}{6} \int dt_v q(t_v)^3\right) \right\rangle_0 = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \langle q(t) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$.
  By Wick's theorem, there are 3 ways to contract $q(t)$ with one of the $q(t_v)$ fields, leaving the remaining two $q(t_v)$ fields to contract with each other, forming a loop (a tadpole).
  So, $\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = 3 \cdot G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
  Substituting this into the expression:
  $\frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, 3 G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
  Using $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$ and $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) = \int dt_v \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t_v|} = \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{1}{m^2}$:
  The $O(g^1)$ numerator term is $\frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{m^2} = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.
  Thus, $\langle q(t) e^{-S_{\text{int}}} \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^3)$. (Higher order terms would involve $g^3$, e.g., three vertices, two external legs, etc.)

**Denominator expansion:**
$\langle e^{-S_{\text{int}}} \rangle_0 = 1 + \left\langle \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v q(t_v)^3 \right\rangle_0 + \frac{1}{2!} \left\langle \left(\frac{g}{6} \int dt_v q(t_v)^3\right)^2 \right\rangle_0 + O(g^3)$.
- **Order $g^0$:** $1$.
- **Order $g^1$:** $\left\langle \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v q(t_v)^3 \right\rangle_0 = 0$, as there's an odd number of fields ($q(t_v)^3$) which cannot be fully contracted by Wick's theorem.
- **Order $g^2$:**
  $\frac{1}{2} \left(\frac{g}{6}\right)^2 \int dt_v dt_{v'} \langle q(t_v)^3 q(t_{v'})^3 \rangle_0$.
  By Wick's theorem, to contract these 6 fields, each $q(t_v)$ must connect to a $q(t_{v'})$. There are $3! = 6$ ways to pair them up. So, $\langle q(t_v)^3 q(t_{v'})^3 \rangle_0 = 6 \cdot G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^3$.
  Substituting this: $\frac{1}{2} \frac{g^2}{36} \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, 6 G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^3 = \frac{g^2}{12} \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^3$.
  Let $\tau = t_v - t_{v'}$. The integral is $\int d\tau \left(\frac{e^{-m|\tau|}}{2m}\right)^3 = \frac{1}{8m^3} \int d\tau e^{-3m|\tau|} = \frac{1}{8m^3} \cdot \frac{2}{3m} = \frac{1}{12m^4}$.
  The $O(g^2)$ denominator term is $\frac{g^2}{12} \cdot \frac{1}{12m^4} = \frac{g^2}{144m^4}$.
  Thus, $\langle e^{-S_{\text{int}}} \rangle_0 = 1 + \frac{g^2}{144m^4} + O(g^4)$. (Only even powers of $g$ contribute to vacuum diagrams for a time-translation invariant system.)

**Combining numerator and denominator:**
$G_1(t) = \frac{\frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^3)}{1 + \frac{g^2}{144m^4} + O(g^4)} = \left(\frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^3)\right) \left(1 - \frac{g^2}{144m^4} + O(g^4)\right) = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^3)$.
The leading term for $G_1(t)$ is $O(g^1)$.

### Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs.

- **Order $g^0$:** The only diagram is the free propagator: $G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
- **Order $g^1$:** Diagrams with one vertex. A $q^3$ vertex has 3 legs. If two are external (to $t_1, t_2$), one leg remains. This remaining leg cannot form a loop and cannot connect to another external leg (as there are only two). Thus, there are no connected diagrams with two external legs at $O(g^1)$. The contribution is $0$.
- **Order $g^2$:** Diagrams with two vertices. Each vertex is $q^3$. Total of 6 half-edges. Two are external (to $t_1, t_2$). So 4 internal half-edges remain. These must form internal propagators between the two vertices.
  The only connected diagram is the "double bubble" or "figure-eight" diagram: two vertices (at $t_a, t_b$) connected by two internal propagators, with one external leg going to $t_a$ and the other to $t_b$.

  The contribution from this diagram:
  Vertex factors: $(+g) \cdot (+g) = g^2$.
  Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_a)$, $G_0(t_b, t_2)$, and two $G_0(t_a, t_b)$ between the vertices.
  Combinatorial factor: We are evaluating $\frac{1}{2!} \left(\frac{g}{6}\right)^2 \int dt_a dt_b \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_a)^3 q(t_b)^3 \rangle_0^c$.
  For $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_a)^3 q(t_b)^3 \rangle_0^c$:
  - $q(t_1)$ connects to one of $q(t_a)$ (3 ways).
  - $q(t_2)$ connects to one of $q(t_b)$ (3 ways).
  - The remaining two $q(t_a)$ fields connect to the remaining two $q(t_b)$ fields (2 ways, i.e., $G_0(t_a,t_b)G_0(t_a,t_b)$).
  Total combinatorial factor for this specific contraction is $3 \times 3 \times 2 = 18$.
  The $O(g^2)$ term is $\frac{1}{2!} \left(\frac{g}{6}\right)^2 \cdot 18 \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2 G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
  $= \frac{g^2}{72} \cdot 18 \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2 G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
  $= \frac{g^2}{4} \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2 G_0(t_b, t_2)$.

  Hold on, the factor $1/2!$ for the vertices is only if the vertices are identical (e.g., in a vacuum diagram). Here the vertices are distinct due to external legs.
  Let's use the explicit Feynman rules: each vertex contributes $g$. The symmetry factor for the diagram is $S$.
  The diagram is two vertices, $V_a$ and $V_b$. One external leg $t_1$ to $V_a$, one external leg $t_2$ to $V_b$. Two propagators connect $V_a$ and $V_b$.
  For $V_a$: 1 leg to $t_1$, 2 legs to $V_b$. Number of ways to choose these: $\binom{3}{1}\binom{2}{2} = 3$.
  For $V_b$: 1 leg to $t_2$, 2 legs to $V_a$. Number of ways to choose these: $\binom{3}{1}\binom{2}{2} = 3$.
  The two internal propagators connecting $V_a$ and $V_b$ are indistinguishable. So we divide by $2!$.
  The overall factor is $g^2 \cdot \frac{3 \cdot 3}{2!} = \frac{9g^2}{2}$.
  So the $O(g^2)$ contribution is $\frac{9g^2}{2} \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2 G_0(t_b, t_2)$.

  Substituting $G_0(t,t') = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t'|}$:
  $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{9g^2}{2} \int dt_a dt_b \, \frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_a|}}{2m} \left(\frac{e^{-m|t_a-t_b|}}{2m}\right)^2 \frac{e^{-m|t_b-t_2|}}{2m} + O(g^3)$.
  $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{9g^2}{16m^4} \int dt_a dt_b \, e^{-m|t_1-t_a|} e^{-2m|t_a-t_b|} e^{-m|t_b-t_2|} + O(g^3)$.

## Final answer

(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ is:
$$\boxed{\;G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^3).\;}$$

(ii) The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ is:
$$\boxed{\;G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{9g^2}{16m^4} \int dt_a dt_b \, e^{-m|t_1-t_a|} e^{-2m|t_a-t_b|} e^{-m|t_b-t_2|} + O(g^3).\;}$$
