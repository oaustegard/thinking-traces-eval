# Solution: Etingof Exercise 7.28 (1-point and connected 2-point functions)

## Reasoning

For the Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$, I compute the leading term in $G_1(t) = \langle q(t)\rangle$ and the connected 2-point $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1)q(t_2)\rangle_c$ through order $g^2$.

**Step 1: Free propagator.** The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \bigl[\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2}q^2 + \tfrac{g}{6}q^3\bigr]$. The free propagator (mass $m$, Euclidean) is
$$G_0(t_1,t_2) = \frac{1}{2m}e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$
At coincident times: $G_0(t,t) = \tfrac{1}{2m}$.

**Step 2: 1-point function $G_1(t)$.** By $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry of the potential $U(q) = U(-q)$ (the cubic term $-gq^3/6$ breaks this), actually no — the cubic breaks $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry explicitly. So at zeroth order (free theory), $G_1^{(0)} = 0$ is not automatic.

Reconsider: the action is $S = \int dt[\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2}q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}q^3]$ (note the minus sign on the cubic vertex). Expanding:
$$e^{-S/\hbar} = e^{-S_0/\hbar} e^{g/(6\hbar) \int q^3 dt},$$
where $S_0 = \int[\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2}q^2]dt$. The perturbation is $+\tfrac{g}{6}q^3/\hbar$, giving a 3-valent vertex with weight $+g$ in the Feynman rule.

Order $g^0$: $G_1^{(0)}(t) = \langle q(t)\rangle_0 = 0$ by Wick's theorem (free 1-point is zero, or equivalently the Gaussian has no linear term).

Order $g^1$: The first correction comes from the 1-vertex 1-point diagram (tadpole with one external leg):
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = g \cdot \langle q(t) \int dt_v (q(t_v))^2 \rangle_c.$$
By Wick's theorem,
$$\langle q(t) (q(t_v))^2\rangle = 2 G_0(t, t_v)^2.$$
Thus,
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = 2g \int dt_v G_0(t,t_v)^2 = 2g \int dt_v \frac{1}{4m^2}e^{-2m|t-t_v|} = \frac{g}{2m^2}\int_0^\infty e^{-2m\tau}d\tau = \frac{g}{2m^2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m^3}.$$

Wait, let me recompute more carefully. The Feynman rule for a 3-valent vertex with weight $+g$ contracted with two internal legs and one external leg gives:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = (+g) \cdot \frac{1}{2!}\cdot\langle q(t) \int dt_v q(t_v)q(t_v) \rangle_{\text{2-loop contraction}}.$$
Actually, the standard formula from the trace is: at order $g$, the tadpole contributes
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot G_0(t,t) \cdot \int dt' G_0(t,t') = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{m} = \frac{g}{4m^2}.$$
(The integral $\int dt' e^{-m|t-t'|} = \int_0^\infty e^{-m\tau}d\tau = 1/m$.)

Order $g^2$: Corrections are $O(g^2)$. The leading 1-point correction at order $g^2$ comes from vertex diagrams with two 3-valent vertices. However, by $\mathbb{Z}_3$ symmetry considerations and Feynman-rule counting, many diagrams vanish due to symmetry. The explicit form requires summing reducible and 1PI contributions and is typically of order $g^2$ or higher.

Leading result: $\boxed{G_1(t) = \tfrac{g}{4m^2} + O(g^2)}$ (time-independent by translation invariance).

**Step 3: Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$.** Order $g^0$: $G_2^c(t_1,t_2) = G_0(t_1,t_2) = \tfrac{1}{2m}e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$ (the free propagator is tree-level).

Order $g^1$: The 2-point 1PI diagrams at order $g$ are one 3-valent vertex with two external legs and one self-loop (tadpole on the 2-point). By the trace's analysis for quartic, this contributes
$$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1,t_2) = (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t_1,t_1)\cdot\delta(t_1-t_2) = -\frac{g}{4m}\delta(t_1-t_2).$$
However, this is a 1PI self-energy, which enters via Dyson's equation. For the *connected* 2-point:
$$G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 * \Sigma^{(1)} * G_0 + \cdots$$
At order $g$ (to leading order in $g$), $G_2^c(t_1,t_2) \approx G_0 + (\text{contact term})$, which is nonlocal. By the symmetry of the cubic (odd parity), there is **no $g^1$ contribution to $G_2^c$** (all odd-order perturbations to even correlators vanish).

Order $g^2$: The leading non-zero connected 2-point correction at $g^2$ comes from 2-loop 1PI diagrams (e.g., sunset, or other topologies). The sunset diagram with two 3-valent vertices and three parallel edges between them contributes
$$\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1,t_2) = \frac{g^2}{6} [G_0(t_1,t_2)]^3 = \frac{g^2}{6 \cdot 8m^3} e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{g^2}{48m^3}e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|}.$$
This dominates the $g^2$ connected 2-point.

## Final Answer

**(i) 1-point function:**
$$\boxed{G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^2} + O(g^2).}$$
Time-independent, arising from the tadpole diagram with a self-loop.

**(ii) Connected 2-point function (modulo $g^3$):**
$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1,t_2) = \frac{1}{2m}e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{48m^3}e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).}$$
No $g^1$ contribution (odd-order perturbations vanish for even correlators). The $g^2$ term is the sunset 2-loop self-energy correction.
