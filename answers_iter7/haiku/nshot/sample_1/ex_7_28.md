# n-shot solution: ex_7_28

Below are three worked examples. Study them, then solve the new problem in the same style.

## Example 1: ex_7_27_quartic_1pi

For a quartic oscillator $U(q) = m^2q^2/2 + gq^4/24$, the 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma(t_1, t_2)$ is computed via Feynman diagrams. At order $g^1$, the tadpole diagram (one 4-valent vertex with a self-loop and two external legs) has symmetry factor 2 and contributes $-g\delta(t_1-t_2)/(4m)$. The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}/(2m)$. At order $g^2$, the sunset diagram (two vertices connected by three parallel internal propagators) is 1PI and yields $g^2[G_0(t_1,t_2)]^3/6$, which expands to $g^2 e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|}/(48m^3)$ — a non-local contribution that reflects genuine momentum dependence.

## Example 2: ex_3_26_effective_action

The 1-loop effective action for $S(x) = x^2/2 - gx^3/6$ is $S_1(x_c) = (1/2)\log(1 - gx_c)$, obtained from the second-derivative formula $S_1 = (1/2)\log S''(x_c)$ with $S''(x) = 1 - gx$. The contributing diagrams are 1PI necklaces: a k-cycle of 3-valent vertices each with one external leg, with symmetry factor $1/(2k)$. Summing over all k yields the logarithm. Expanding: $\log(1 - gx_c) = -\sum_{k \geq 1}(gx_c)^k/k$, giving the power series.

## Example 3: ex_2_20_morse

For a smooth function $f(x,y) = a(y) + b(y)x + c(y)x^2 + d(y)x^3$ with specific vanishing and boundary conditions, explicit local Morse coordinates can be constructed by factorizing: $u(x,y) = x\sqrt{c(y) + d(y)x}$ captures the x-quadratic and cubic, while $v(x,y) = y\sqrt{a(y)/y^2 + b(y)x/y^2}$ captures the residual y-dependence (which vanishes to second order). The key is recognizing that under the given conditions, both terms under the square roots remain positive near the origin, so the coordinates are well-defined and smooth.

## Now solve this problem:

Let $U(q) := m^2q^2/2 - gq^3/6$ (cubic anharmonic oscillator). (i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) := \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to g. (ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ (up to and including order $g^2$).

## Solution

**Part (i): 1-point function**

At order $g^0$, the action is free (quadratic), so the potential is even in $q$: $U_0 = m^2q^2/2$. By $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry, $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

At order $g^1$, we compute the leading 1-point via the tadpole diagram. The interaction is $-S_{\text{int}} = gq^3/6$. In the perturbative expansion, the 1-point function gets a contribution from a diagram with one cubic vertex and a self-loop (tadpole). The vertex has 3 legs: one external (carrying $q(t)$), the other two contracting to form an internal loop with the free propagator.

The amplitude is:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{6} \cdot 2 \cdot \langle q(t) [q(t')]^2 \rangle_0^{\text{loop}}.$$

Wait — be careful with the combinatorics. The interaction term is $gq^3/6$. A tadpole diagram has one vertex with three half-edges: one external (at time $t$), two internal. The internal edges contract, giving $G_0(t, t)$ (a loop). The prefactor is the vertex weight $g$ divided by the symmetry factor of contracting two identical legs: $2!$ ways to pair them... actually, there's only one way to pair two legs, but the symmetry of the diagram (the two internal legs are identical) gives a factor. Standard: the diagram weight is:
$$\text{weight} = \frac{g}{2} \cdot G_0(t, t).$$

But we also need the external-leg contraction structure. Actually, the correct statement: the 1-point correlator receives the one-loop tadpole contribution. The free correlator of $q(t)q(t') q(t')$ (two legs at $t'$, one at $t$) sums over Wick contractions: $\langle q(t) q(t')^2 \rangle = 2 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$ (the factor 2 counts the two ways to pair the two legs at $t'$ with... wait, that doesn't make sense).

Let me redo this carefully. At order $g^1$, the path integral is:
$$Z(0) = \int e^{-S_0/\hbar} \left[1 - \frac{g}{6\hbar}\int dt' q(t')^3 + O(g^2)\right] dx.$$

So $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{6\hbar} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 / Z_0$.

The free 4-point correlator $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$ is the sum of Wick contractions: pair the external $q(t)$ with one of the three $q(t')$'s (3 ways), and contract the remaining two $q(t')$'s:
$$\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t').$$

Thus:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{6\hbar} \int dt' \cdot 3 \cdot G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') \cdot \hbar = -\frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_0(t, t')^2.$$

Hmm, still not matching the tadpole picture. Actually, the tadpole is a one-particle-irreducible diagram contributing to the effective action (or 1PI correlator), not directly to the 1-point function via Wick contractions. Let me use the Feynman rule approach. At 1-loop order, the 1-point function is:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} G_0(t, t)^2$$

by standard Feynman rules for the cubic interaction. More carefully: the tadpole diagram has a 3-valent vertex with legs at $t$, $t'$, $t'$ (two internal, one external). The amplitude is $g \cdot G_0(t', t') \cdot$ (vertex factor) / (symmetry). Integrating over the internal time $t'$ and using standard Feynman rules:

$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_0(t, t) G_0(t', t') = \frac{g}{2} G_0(t, t) \int dt' G_0(t', t').$$

For Euclidean kinematics with mass $m$, $G_0(t, t) = 1/(2m)$ and $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} G_0(t', t') dt' = \int e^{-m|t'-t'|} /(2m) dt' = 1/m$ (since $\int_0^{\infty} e^{-ms} ds = 1/m$).

Therefore:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{m} = \frac{g}{4m^2}.$$

This is **time-independent** (translation invariance), and the leading 1-point contribution is:
$$\boxed{G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^2}.}$$

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function**

At order $g^0$ (free theory): $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}/(2m)$.

At order $g^1$: The interaction is $gq^3/6$. A diagram contributing to $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^c$ at 1-loop with one cubic vertex has 2 external legs at $t_1, t_2$ and one internal leg. But the cubic vertex has 3 half-edges total. With 2 external, there's 1 internal, which cannot form a loop (needs 2+ legs). So there's **no 1-loop, 1-vertex diagram with 2 external legs for the cubic**. Alternatively, the $\mathbb{Z}_3$ symmetry of the cubic action says: diagrams with odd numbers of external legs vanish for even correlators. The 2-point function is even; one external vertex gives an odd number of external legs, so it vanishes.

At order $g^2$: Two-loop diagrams with two cubic vertices contribute. One class is the sunset-like diagram (similar to the quartic case): two vertices connected by internal propagators, with 2 external legs (one at each vertex). The amplitude is $g^2 [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^3 / (2 \cdot 3!) = g^2 e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|} / (12m^3)$ (accounting for vertex and edge symmetries). Another class would be nested or other topologies.

The leading contribution at $g^2$ is:
$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g^2}{12m^3} e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).}$$
