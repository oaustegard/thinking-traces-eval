# Solution: ex_7_28 — 1-point and 2-point functions for cubic anharmonic oscillator

The trace carefully sets up the Euclidean path integral, identifies external vs. internal legs, applies symmetry factors, and systematically evaluates tadpole ($g^1$) and sunset ($g^2$) diagrams. **Key differences:** (i) the problem here uses a *cubic* potential $U(q) = m^2q^2/2 - gq^3/6$ (not quartic), so vertices are 3-valent (not 4-valent); (ii) the problem asks for the 1-point function *and* the connected 2-point (not the self-energy / 1PI 2-point), requiring understanding of Z₂ symmetry and reducible-diagram cancellation; (iii) the cubic sign is negative ($-g$ not $+g$), affecting sign tracking in the expansion.

## Solution

**Setup and Z₂ symmetry.** The Euclidean action is
$$S[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2}q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}q^3\right).$$

The potential $U(q) = m^2q^2/2 - gq^3/6$ is **not** even in $q$ (due to the $-gq^3/6$ term). However, at zeroth order in $g$ (free theory), $U_0(q) = m^2q^2/2$ is even, and the free correlators satisfy $\langle q(t_1) \cdots q(t_k) \rangle_0 = 0$ whenever $k$ is odd (by Z₂ symmetry of the free action). This symmetry is broken at order $g$ by the cubic term, but the perturbation structure is constrained: odd-$k$ correlators receive contributions only at odd orders in $g$, and even-$k$ correlators at even orders.

**Free propagator.** In Euclidean quantum mechanics with mass $m$ and no external source, the free 2-point function is
$$G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$
At coincident times: $G_0(t, t) = 1/(2m)$.

## (i) 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$

**Order $g^0$ (free theory):** By the Z₂ symmetry of the free action, $G_1^{(0)}(t) = 0$.

**Order $g^1$ (first correction):** The 1-point function at order $g$ is generated by a single cubic vertex. The diagram is a "tadpole": one 3-valent vertex (from $-gq^3/6$) with one external leg (carrying $q(t)$) and two internal half-edges contracting to form a self-loop.

Feynman-rule evaluation:
- The 3-valent vertex weight is $-g$ (from the $-gq^3/6$ term in the action, with the $1/3!$ canceling the permutation count modulo symmetry).
- The external leg carries the time variable $t$ and contributes $q(t)$ to the correlator.
- The two internal half-edges form a single propagator connecting the vertex to itself (a self-loop), contributing $G_0(t, t) = 1/(2m)$.

**Symmetry factor.** The self-loop has two ends at the same vertex; there is a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry from interchanging the two ends, so the symmetry factor is $S = 2$. 

**Diagram value:**
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{S} \cdot G_0(t,t) = (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = -\frac{g}{4m}.$$

Wait — this is time-independent, as expected from translation invariance in the vacuum. However, re-checking the sign: the $-g$ vertex weight is correct (from $-gq^3/6$ with $1/3!$ factored out); the self-loop propagator is $G_0(t,t) = 1/(2m)$; and the symmetry factor for an unordered pair of legs forming a closed loop is 2 (the two legs are indistinguishable but the loop has no orientation, so the only symmetry is swapping them). Thus:
$$\boxed{G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{4m}.}$$

At order $g^2$ and beyond, contributions arise from 2-loop and higher diagrams (e.g., two vertices each with self-loops), giving corrections in powers of $g^2$. For the leading term, we report $G_1^{(1)} = -g/(4m)$ as the answer to leading order in $g$.

## (ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$

The connected 2-point function (or "cumulant") is related to the full 2-point function by removing disconnected pieces. For even-degree correlators, the Z₂ symmetry implies that the $g^1$ (odd-order) correction vanishes:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^1} = 0.$$

**Order $g^0$ (free theory):**
$$G_2^{c(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^2$ (1-loop correction):** The 1-loop 1PI diagrams with two external legs are:
1. **Tadpole on one leg:** A 3-valent vertex with one external leg (say, to $t_1$), one leg to the external point $t_2$, and a self-loop. But this vertex has only 3 legs total: 1 (external to $t_1$) + 1 (external to $t_2$) + 2 (self-loop) = 4, which exceeds 3. This configuration is impossible for a 3-valent vertex.

   More carefully: a 3-valent vertex can have at most 1 external leg and 2 other half-edges (either forming a self-loop or connecting to another vertex). So a single vertex cannot attach to two distinct external points and have a self-loop. Thus this diagram does not occur.

2. **Two-vertex diagram (analogue of "eye"):** Two 3-valent vertices, each with one external leg. The remaining 2 half-edges at each vertex must connect to the other vertex. Two vertices, each with 2 internal half-edges, can be connected by 1, 2, ... propagators. With 2 internal half-edges per vertex, we have a total of 4 internal half-edges, which pair into 2 propagators between the two vertices. This is the analogue of the quartic "eye" diagram from Trace 1.

   Feynman rule:
   - Vertex 1 at time $t_1$: weight $-g$, one external leg ($q(t_1)$), two internal half-edges to vertex 2.
   - Vertex 2 at time $t_2$: weight $-g$, one external leg ($q(t_2)$), two internal half-edges to vertex 1.
   - Two parallel propagators between $t_1$ and $t_2$: $(G_0(t_1, t_2))^2$.

   Combined: $(-g) \cdot (-g) \cdot (G_0(t_1, t_2))^2 / S$, where $S$ is the symmetry factor.

   **Symmetry factor:** The two parallel internal propagators are indistinguishable, so swapping them is a symmetry → factor $2!$. The two vertices are distinguishable (external legs at different times), so no vertex-swap symmetry. Thus $S = 2$.

   **Diagram value:**
   $$G_2^{c(2)}_{\text{eye}}(t_1, t_2) = g^2 \cdot \frac{(G_0(t_1, t_2))^2}{2} = \frac{g^2}{2 \cdot (2m)^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

Actually, hold on — the 1PI self-energy $\Sigma$ in Trace 1 is distinct from the connected 2-point. The *full* 2-point function $G_2(t_1, t_2)$ relates to the 1PI self-energy by the Dyson equation: $G_2 = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \cdots$. But the *connected* 2-point is related by $G_2^c = G_2 - G_0^2 = \text{terms with at least one vertex}$. For a 1-loop 1PI diagram with two external legs, it already represents a connected piece. But to be careful: is the "eye" diagram a 1PI 2-point (self-energy) or not? It connects two external points via two internal propagators — cutting any one propagator leaves it connected (the other propagator maintains connection) — so it IS 1PI. However, the self-energy $\Sigma$ is conventionally defined with external legs amputated, whereas here we have external legs at $t_1, t_2$. So the 1PI 2-point value is the self-energy times the two external legs ($q(t_1) q(t_2)$). The diagram value I computed above is the contribution to the full 2-point, not the self-energy.

To clarify using Trace 1's framework: the 1-loop self-energy for the cubic is
$$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = [\text{sum of 1PI 1-loop diagrams with two external legs, no external-leg factors}].$$

For the eye diagram (two vertices, two internal propagators), the value is 
$$\Sigma^{(1)}_{\text{eye}} = g^2 \cdot \frac{[G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2}{2}.$$

Then the connected 2-point is
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 + O(g^3) = G_0 + G_0 \star (g^2 \cdot [G_0]^2 / 2) \star G_0 + O(g^3).$$

But wait — $\Sigma^{(1)}$ depends on $t_1, t_2$; convolution would involve integrals over intermediate times. Actually, the eye diagram is **non-local** in time (it has a time-dependent form $[G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2$), so the relationship is more complex. 

Actually, re-reading the problem: it asks for $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$, the connected 2-point *function*, not the self-energy. The connected cumulant is the sum of all connected diagrams (including trees and loops). At order $g^2$, the connected 2-point includes:
- The "eye" diagram (two vertices, two propagators, no self-loops) — this is 1PI 1-loop.
- Potentially reducible 2-loop diagrams where tadpoles attach to the free propagator, but those are already $O(g^2)$ and are captured in the Dyson equation.

For our purposes (modulo $g^3$), the eye diagram dominates. So:

$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).$$

Actually, let me reconsider the sign. The two vertices each have weight $-g$ (from $-gq^3/6$), so $(-g)^2 = +g^2$. Then the eye diagram contributes $+g^2 [G_0]^2 / 2$. Since both contributions are positive relative to the expansion parameter, the leading correction is indeed positive.

$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).}$$

Alternatively, if we interpret the problem as asking for the correction *from* $G_0$, then the answer emphasizes the $O(g^2)$ term: the leading non-trivial correction is the eye diagram, proportional to $g^2 \cdot e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}$.
