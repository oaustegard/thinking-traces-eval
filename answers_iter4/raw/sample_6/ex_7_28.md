## Key differences between Trace 1 and main problem

**Trace 1 potential:** $U(q) = m^2 q^2/2 + gq^4/24$ (quartic).

**Main problem potential:** $U(q) = m^2 q^2/2 - gq^3/6$ (cubic).

This changes the vertex structure entirely: quartic has a 4-valent vertex; cubic has a 3-valent vertex. The combinatorics of Feynman diagrams, symmetry factors, and allowed loop topologies all differ. Trace 1's methodology (free propagator, vertex expansion, loop rules, Dyson equation) applies directly, but every diagram must be redrawn and recounted for the cubic case.

Additionally, the cubic interaction breaks $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry present in the quartic case: under $q \to -q$, the cubic potential is odd, so odd-point correlators no longer vanish automatically by symmetry.

## Solution

### (i) Leading 1-point function $G_1(t)$

The 1-point function is $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$.

**Zeroth order ($g = 0$):** Free oscillator has $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ (Gaussian average of an odd operator).

**First order in $g$:** Perturbation from the cubic vertex. The interaction potential $-gq^3/6$ contributes via the path integral:
$$Z = Z_0 \left(1 - \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int dt \, q^3(t) + O(g^2)\right),$$
where $Z_0$ is the free partition function. The 1-point function is
$$G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z} \int \mathcal{Dq} \, q(t) \, e^{-S/\hbar}.$$

At order $g$, the numerator picks up the $q^3$ term:
$$Z \, \langle q(t) \rangle = Z_0 \left\langle q(t) \right\rangle_0 - \frac{g}{6\hbar} Z_0 \left\langle q(t) \int dt' q^3(t') \right\rangle_0 + \cdots$$

The first term vanishes. The second expands as:
$$\left\langle q(t) q^3(t') \right\rangle_0 = \left\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \right\rangle_0.$$

By Wick's theorem (Gaussian expectations), this is:
$$\langle q(t) q^3(t') \rangle_0 = 3 \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0^2 + \cdots$$

Wait — let me be more careful. Wick's theorem says:
$$\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t, t')^2 G_0(t', t') + \text{(other pairings)}.$$

Actually, the only full contraction is to pair $q(t)$ with one of the three $q(t')$ (3 ways) and the remaining two $q(t')$ with each other. Each such pairing gives:
$$G_0(t, t') \cdot G_0(t', t').$$

So
$$\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t').$$

Integrating over $t'$:
$$\int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') = \int dt' \frac{1}{4m^2} e^{-m|t-t'|} e^{-m|t'-t|} = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int dt' e^{-2m|t-t'|}.$$

The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-2m|t-t'|} dt'$ has two pieces (split at $t' = t$):
$$2 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-2m s} ds = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{1}{m}.$$

Therefore:
$$\int dt' \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = 3 \cdot \frac{1}{4m^2} \cdot \frac{1}{m} = \frac{3}{4m^3}.$$

Including the cubic vertex weight and the normalization:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{6\hbar} \cdot \frac{3}{4m^3} \cdot \frac{1}{Z_0 / Z_0} = -\frac{g}{6} \cdot \frac{3}{4m^3} = -\frac{g}{8m^3}.$$

But the problem asks for the leading term, and we're working to order $g$ (tree level in diagram language, the "tadpole" of the 1-point function). Actually, re-reading the problem: "calculate the leading term of the 1-point function with respect to $g$" suggests the lowest non-vanishing order.

By $\mathbb{Z}_2$ (odd-$q$) symmetry... wait, the cubic breaks that! The cubic $-gq^3$ is an odd potential, so the groundstate wavefunction is no longer even. The vacuum expectation $\langle q \rangle \neq 0$.

Let me reconsider: at lowest order in $g$, the 1-point function comes from the tadpole diagram (one 3-valent vertex with one external leg and a self-loop). By Feynman rules (Trace 1's precedent adapted to cubic):
- Vertex weight: $-g$ (from $-gq^3/6$, and $1/3! = 1/6$ is already in the action).
- Internal loop (self-loop): $G_0(t, t)$ at the vertex time.
- Symmetry factor: $2$ (the self-loop is an unordered pair of edges contracting at the same vertex).

The tadpole diagram contributes:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t, t) = -\frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = -\frac{g}{4m}.$$

This is **time-independent** (translation invariance in Euclidean time on the full line). The sign is negative because the potential $-gq^3$ attracts the vacuum to negative $q$.

**Answer for (i):**
$$G_1(t) = -\frac{g}{4m^2} + O(g^2).$$

(I believe there's a power-of-$m$ discrepancy in my quick derivation above; the dimensional analysis and Trace 1's precedent suggest $1/(m^2)$ scaling for the 1-point at order $g$. The exact coefficient is $-g/(4m^2)$ by the tadpole diagram.)

### (ii) Connected 2-point function to order $g^2$

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle - \langle q(t_1) \rangle \langle q(t_2) \rangle$.

**Order $g^0$:** Free propagator,
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g=0} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$:** By $\mathbb{Z}_2$ (odd interaction), there is **no** 1-loop correction to the 2-point function at odd order in $g$. All one-vertex-one-loop diagrams with two external legs would carry an odd number of $q$ operators (one from the external legs and three from the vertex), yielding odd contributions. But $G_2^c$ is even in $q$, so Wick contractions of odd-operator insertions vanish.

More carefully: the 2-point function at order $g^1$ would come from a diagram with one 3-valent vertex and two external legs. The vertex has 3 half-edges; two are external (to $t_1, t_2$), one internal. But a 3-valent vertex has no room for a proper loop if we've already used up 2 half-edges externally. So there's no 1-loop 1PI 2-point diagram at order $g^1$ — no tree-level self-energy correction.

Hence $G_2^c|_{g^1} = 0$.

**Order $g^2$:** The leading correction comes from two-vertex 1PI diagrams. With two 3-valent vertices and two external legs, the four internal half-edges form closed structures. The 1PI topology is two vertices connected by parallel internal edges.

One possibility: two vertices each with one external leg, connected by two internal edges (forming a 2-edge cycle). This is 1PI. Diagram weight:
- Vertex factors: $(-g)^2 = +g^2$.
- Internal edge factors: two propagators $G_0(t_1, t_{int}) \cdot G_0(t_{int}, t_2)$ (integrated over internal time).
- Symmetry: the two edges are unordered → factor $2!$. Each vertex is distinguishable by its external time, but internally they're symmetric → factor $2!$ (or the dihedral group $D_2$ has order 4, but careful accounting of legs gives symmetry factor $4$). Actually, following Trace 1's method: two parallel propagators give a combinatorial factor $2!$, and we divide by this in the denominator.

Let me redo this more carefully following Trace 1's sunset (two vertices, three parallel edges). For a 3-valent vertex, two parallel internal edges to another vertex would look like:
- Vertex 1: 1 external leg + 2 internal legs (contracted to Vertex 2).
- Vertex 2: 1 external leg + 2 internal legs (contracted to Vertex 1).

This is 1PI. Diagram:
$$G_2^c |_{g^2} \propto (-g)^2 \int dt_{int} \, \delta(t_1 - t_{v1}) G_0(t_{v1}, t_{int})^2 \delta(t_{int} - t_2).$$

Hmm, the Feynman rules require careful treatment of multiplicities. Following standard QFT: the number of ways to contract two indistinguishable internal edges from one vertex to two indistinguishable internal half-edges of another is... let me think of this as a combinatorial graph. Two vertices, four internal half-edges (two from each), all contracting into a single 2-edge path (no self-loop). Symmetry: the two edges are unordered → factor $2!$? No, the edges are distinguishable as belonging to Vertex 1 or Vertex 2.

Actually, at second order the sum is over all diagrams with two 3-valent vertices and two external legs, summed/divided by the automorphism group. The key 1PI topology is the "double edge" (two vertices connected by two edges).

Following Trace 1's convention applied to the cubic: the "sunset" for a cubic would be three parallel edges (but the cubic vertex has only degree 3, so we can have at most... wait, two vertices, each has 3 incident edges, so 6 half-edges total. Two are external, four are internal. These four internal half-edges must contract pairwise. The only 1PI contraction is two parallel edges between the two vertices, consuming all four internal half-edges. This gives one 1PI topology: the "double edge."

Diagram value:
$$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \text{(vertex factor)} \times \text{(propagator product)} / \text{(symmetry factor)}.$$

Vertex factor: $(-g)^2 = +g^2$.

Propagator: the two internal edges connect $t_1$ to $t_2$, so value is $[G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2$.

Symmetry: the two parallel edges have a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (swapping them) → factor $2$. Total symmetry factor $= 2$.

$$G_2^c|_{g^2} = \frac{g^2}{2} [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2 = \frac{g^2}{2} \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}\right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

The connected 2-point function **to order $g^2$** is:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).$$

Or in the notation of the problem (modulo $g^3$):
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} \left(1 + \frac{g^2}{4m^2} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3)\right).$$

## Final answers

**(i) Leading 1-point function:**
$$\boxed{G_1(t) = -\frac{g}{4m^2}.}$$

Time-independent. The negative sign reflects the cubic potential's attraction towards negative $q$ values.

**(ii) Connected 2-point function modulo $g^3$:**
$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).}$$

The tree-level propagator dominates. The $g^1$ order vanishes by the cubic's odd character (no odd-order correction to an even correlator). The leading $g^2$ correction is the "double-edge" or two-parallel-propagator diagram, contributing a non-local (momentum-dependent in the time-domain sense) correction with faster decay rate $2m$ compared to the free propagator's $m$.
