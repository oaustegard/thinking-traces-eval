### Key Differences from ex_7_27

- **Potential:** Our problem uses $U(q) = m^2 q^2/2 - gq^3/6$ (cubic interaction), not quartic. This affects the Feynman rule (cubic vertex weight $-g$, not quartic $-g$), and changes the diagram topology and symmetry factors.
- **1-point function:** ex_7_27 does not compute the 1-point function; it focuses on the 2-point (self-energy). We must handle the 1-point separately, paying attention to $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry.
- **Cubic vs. quartic vertices:** One vertex has 3 half-edges (not 4). This reduces the number of possible internal contractions and changes diagram shapes (e.g., no "eye" diagram at 1-loop for 1-point).

## Solution

### Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$

**Free theory ($g=0$):** The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt (\dot q^2/2 + m^2 q^2/2)$. By $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry ($q \to -q$), all odd-point correlation functions vanish. Thus
$$G_1^{(0)}(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0.$$

**Order $g^1$ correction:** The interaction is $-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar = (g/6\hbar) q^3$. To first order in $g$, the 1-point function receives contributions from diagrams with one insertion of $-gq^3/(6\hbar)$ and any number of free-theory contractions.

A 1-point function with a cubic vertex has 3 legs. For the diagram to be connected and non-vanishing, we need to contract all 3 legs via propagators. This requires at least one external leg (to probe $\langle q(t) \rangle$) and internal contractions of the remaining legs. However, with only 3 legs total from the vertex and one external point $t$ (via $\delta(t - t_v)$ from the vertex insertion), we cannot form a topologically consistent diagram at order $g^1$ that preserves the one-point structure without violating odd-parity expectations.

**More carefully:** The 1-point function at order $g$ comes from the Feynman rule:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \int dt_v \, (-g) \times (\text{leg contractions}).$$

A cubic vertex has 3 half-edges. For a 1-point function (one external leg), we have 3 legs with 1 external (to $t$) and 2 internal legs to contract. But a $\mathbb{Z}_2$-odd insertion (the $q^3$ vertex) contracted with an even-point free correlator gives an **odd result**, violating $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry. Therefore,
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = 0.$$

**This extends to all odd orders by $\mathbb{Z}_2$.**

**Order $g^2$ correction:** At two-loop, we have diagrams with two cubic vertices (6 half-edges total). With one external leg, 5 half-edges must pair into propagators. An odd number of unpaired legs is not possible, so the diagram is topologically possible. However, the $\mathbb{Z}_2$ parity of the diagram is: two $q^3$ insertions (even parity) contracted with free propagators gives an **even result**. This can be non-zero.

The simplest diagram: **two cubic vertices, one external leg to $t$, and the remaining 5 legs forming a connected graph**. One configuration: a single propagator from one vertex to the other, with each vertex also having a self-loop. This gives:
$$G_1^{(2)}(t) = \int dt_1 dt_2 \, (-g)^2 \times (\text{propagator structure}),$$
accounting for symmetry factors.

After detailed diagrammatic analysis, the leading non-vanishing 1-point is at order $g^2$ (but the problem asks for the "leading term," which may mean the first non-zero order). If the problem intends the result to leading order in perturbation theory:

$$\boxed{G_1^{(1)}(t) = 0 \quad \text{(by $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry).}}$$

The **first non-vanishing** term is at $O(g^2)$, with explicit value requiring computation of all $g^2$ diagrams. However, based on standard QFT results for cubic interactions, the leading order is:

$$\boxed{G_1(t) = 0 + O(g^2).}$$

Alternatively, if the problem implicitly asks for the **first-order-in-$g$ result treating the effective coupling or renormalization**, the answer depends on the specific interpretation. The most standard result: **$G_1(t) = 0$ to all orders** by $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry unless the potential is deformed to break this symmetry (e.g., adding a linear term).

### Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $O(g^2)$

**Order $g^0$ (free theory):**
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$:** The cubic vertex has 3 half-edges. With 2 external legs to $(t_1, t_2)$, the remaining 1 half-edge has no contracting partner — it cannot close a loop. Thus **no 1-loop diagram at $g^1$ connects only the 2 external legs**. The first non-zero correction is at $g^2$.

$$G_2^c \big|_{g^1} = 0.$$

**Order $g^2$:** Two cubic vertices. A connected 1PI 2-point diagram with 2 external legs requires internal pairing of the remaining 4 half-edges. Topologies:

1. **Vertex-to-vertex with 2 internal propagators, each external leg on one vertex:** Vertex A has one external leg to $t_1$ and two internal legs (forming a double edge to vertex B). Vertex B has one external leg to $t_2$ and two internal legs (forming the double edge to A). This is **connected and 1PI**.

   Symmetry factor: 2 parallel internal edges have $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (swapping the two propagators), and the two vertices are distinguishable by their external leg times, so $S = 2$.

   Diagram value:
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) \bigg|_{g^2} = \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, (-g)^2 \, \frac{1}{S} \, \delta(t_1 - t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^2 \, \delta(t_2 - t_{v'}) = \frac{g^2}{2} G_0(t_1, t_2)^2.$$

   Substituting $G_0(t_1, t_2) = e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}/(2m)$:
   $$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) \bigg|_{g^2} = \frac{g^2}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{4m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

2. **Other topologies** (e.g., triple edge, self-loops on external legs) either reduce to the above or are non-1PI (disconnected by cutting a single propagator).

**Total result through $O(g^2)$:**
$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} + O(g^3).}$$

## Final Answer

**(i)** The leading term of $G_1(t)$ is **zero**: $G_1(t) = 0 + O(g^2)$ by $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry of the cubic interaction around $q=0$.

**(ii)** The connected 2-point function modulo $g^3$ is:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

The $g^0$ term is the free propagator. The $g^2$ term comes from a single 1PI diagram: two cubic vertices connected by a double propagator, one external leg on each vertex. No $g^1$ correction exists due to the odd valence of the cubic vertex and the requirement of 1PI topology.
