## Solution

Euclidean action: $S[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2}q^2 + \tfrac{g}{6}q^3\right)$ (note: $U = \tfrac{m^2q^2}{2} - \tfrac{gq^3}{6}$, so in the action we have $+\tfrac{gq^3}{6}$ from $-U$).

Free propagator: $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \tfrac{1}{2m}e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

Feynman rules: each cubic vertex carries weight $+g$ (from $e^{(g/6) \int q^3 dt}$ expansion in the path integral). Vertices have valence 3. External legs are not amputated in the definition of $G_1$ and $G_2^c$ (they are full correlation functions, not 1PI).

The action is $S[q] = \int \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2}q^2 + \tfrac{g}{6}q^3\right) dt$. Under $q \to -q$, the quadratic term is unchanged but $q^3 \to -q^3$, so the action changes by $\int -\tfrac{g}{6}q^3 dt = -\int \tfrac{g}{6}q^3 dt$. Thus the action is **not** Z₂-invariant, and there is no symmetry suppressing the 1-point function at tree level.

At tree level ($g=0$), there are no interaction vertices, so the 1-point function is just the saddle-point value of $q$ in the free theory, which is $\langle q \rangle_0 = 0$ (translation invariance of the free action).

### Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$

**Order $g^0$ (free theory):** 
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \tfrac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$:** Diagrams with one cubic vertex and two external legs. A 3-valent vertex with 2 external legs and 1 internal leg (self-loop or connection to another vertex):
- Single vertex, two external legs, one self-loop: contributes a tadpole correction to the 2-point. But wait, one vertex with 3 legs, two external, one internal... the internal leg closes on itself (self-loop). This gives a local $\delta(t_1 - t_2)$ contribution (both external legs are at the vertex).
- Alternatively, if the two external legs don't both connect to the same vertex, we'd need at least two vertices—but that's order $g^2$.

For one vertex with two external legs at times $t_1, t_2$ and one self-loop at the vertex time $t_v$:
$$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_v \; \delta(t_1 - t_v) \delta(t_2 - t_v) \cdot (-g) \cdot G_0(t_v, t_v).$$

The $\delta$ functions force $t_1 = t_2 = t_v$, so this is proportional to $\delta(t_1 - t_2)$. But this type of diagram is **not 1-particle-irreducible** (1PI) — it's a disconnected contribution (two external legs attached to the same vertex cannot be 1PI by definition, as the connection is via a single vertex, not two independent paths).

Actually, "connected" means the diagram remains connected if we remove any single external leg. For a single-vertex diagram with two external legs and one self-loop, removing either external leg disconnects the other. So this **is not connected** in the standard definition. Thus there are **no connected 1-loop contributions to the 2-point function from one cubic vertex**—they're all disconnected (essentially $1 \otimes \text{tadpole}$).

**Order $g^2$:** Two cubic vertices with two external legs (one per vertex, typically) and internal propagators forming a loop.

By the same logic as Trace 1: the connected 2-point 1PI contributions at order $g^2$ come from diagrams where the two external legs are attached to two different vertices, and the vertices are connected by internal propagators.

With two 3-valent vertices, each has 3 legs. If one vertex has 1 external leg, it has 2 internal legs; similarly for the other. So we have $2 + 2 = 4$ internal half-edges, which must pair to form $2$ propagators connecting the two vertices.

Diagram: two vertices $v_1, v_2$ at times $t_v, t_v'$; vertex $v_1$ has external leg to $t_1$, vertex $v_2$ has external leg to $t_2$; the two internal legs of $v_1$ and two of $v_2$ form two propagators connecting $v_1$ to $v_2$.

Combinatorial weight: vertices can be swapped ($2!$ symmetry), and the two internal propagators connecting them can be swapped ($2!$ symmetry). So $|\text{Aut}| = 2 \cdot 2 = 4$. Vertex weights: $(-g)^2 = +g^2$.

$$G_2^{(2),\text{int}}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_v \, dt_{v'} \; \delta(t_1 - t_v) \delta(t_2 - t_{v'}) \cdot \tfrac{g^2}{4} \cdot [G_0(t_v, t_{v'})]^2.$$

Integrating over $t_v, t_{v'}$:
$$G_2^{(2),\text{int}}(t_1, t_2) = \tfrac{g^2}{4} \cdot [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2.$$

Substituting $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \tfrac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$:
$$G_2^{(2),\text{int}}(t_1, t_2) = \tfrac{g^2}{4} \cdot \tfrac{1}{4m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} = \tfrac{g^2}{16m^2} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

This is the connected 1-loop 2-point contribution at order $g^2$.

## Final answer

$$\boxed{G_1(t) = -\tfrac{g}{2m} + O(g^2).}$$

$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \tfrac{1}{2m}e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \tfrac{g^2}{16m^2}e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).}$$

The 1-point function is nonzero at order $g$ due to the cubic term breaking Z₂ symmetry. The connected 2-point function receives its first correction at order $g^2$ from a pair of cubic vertices connected by two internal propagators; no $g^1$ contribution arises (single-vertex diagrams with 2 external legs are disconnected).
