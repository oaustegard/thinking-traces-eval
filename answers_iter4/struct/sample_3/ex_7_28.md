## Solution

**Part (i): Leading 1-Point Function $G_1(t)$**

The potential is $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$ (note: the problem states $U(q) := m^2 q^2 / 2 - g q^3 / 6$, which I interpret as a **negative cubic** for consistency with standard physics conventions).

The path integral is $Z = \int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S[q]/\hbar}$ with action $S[q] = \int_0^\beta d\tau \left( \tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + U(q) \right)$. The 1-point function is $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z} \int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]/\hbar}$.

**Zeroth order in $g$ (free theory):**
The free action is $S_0[q] = \int d\tau (\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2})$, which is quadratic. The path integral is Gaussian, so $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ by $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (the action is invariant under $q \to -q$).

**First order in $g$:**
We expand $e^{-S[q]/\hbar} = e^{-S_0[q]/\hbar} \left(1 - \frac{1}{\hbar} \int d\tau \left(-\frac{g q^3(\tau)}{6}\right) + O(g^2) \right) = e^{-S_0[q]/\hbar} \left(1 + \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int d\tau \, q^3(\tau) + O(g^2)\right)$.

Then,
$$G_1(t) = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) \left[1 + \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int d\tau \, q^3(\tau) + O(g^2)\right] e^{-S_0[q]/\hbar}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, \left[1 + \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int d\tau \, q^3(\tau) + O(g^2)\right] e^{-S_0[q]/\hbar}}.$$

To order $g$:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) \cdot \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int d\tau \, q^3(\tau) \, e^{-S_0[q]/\hbar}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]/\hbar}} = \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int d\tau \, \langle q(t) q^3(\tau) \rangle_0.$$

By Wick's theorem in Gaussian path integrals, $\langle q(t) q^3(\tau) \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(\tau) \rangle_0 \langle q(\tau)^2 \rangle_0 + 2 \langle q(t) q(\tau) \rangle_0^2 \langle q(\tau) \rangle_0 + \ldots$. But $\langle q(\tau) \rangle_0 = 0$, so only the first term (and Wick contractions involving $\langle q(t) q(\tau) \rangle_0$) contribute:

$$\langle q(t) q^3(\tau) \rangle_0 = 3 \langle q(t) q(\tau) \rangle_0 \langle q(\tau)^2 \rangle_0.$$

The free propagator in Euclidean time is $G_0(t, \tau) = \langle q(t) q(\tau) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - \tau|}$ (from the harmonic oscillator at inverse temperature $\beta$, or in continuous Euclidean QM with mass $m$).

Also, $\langle q(\tau)^2 \rangle_0 = G_0(\tau, \tau) = \frac{1}{2m}$ (divergent in infinite-volume Euclidean field theory, but here we interpret it as a local contact term; in regularized path integrals, this is a finite constant).

Thus,
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{6\hbar} \int d\tau \, 3 G_0(t, \tau) \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m\hbar} \int d\tau \, \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - \tau|} = \frac{g}{4m\hbar} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{g}{4m^2 \hbar}.$$

Setting $\hbar \to 1$ (natural units), 

$$\boxed{G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{4m^2} \quad (\text{time-independent, tadpole diagram})}$$

**Part (ii): Connected 2-Point Function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ to Order $g^2$**

The connected 2-point function (amputated) is defined by 
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle - \langle q(t_1) \rangle \langle q(t_2) \rangle.$$

**Order $g^0$ (free theory):**
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g=0} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$:**
By $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (the cubic interaction $-\frac{g q^3}{6}$ is odd under $q \to -q$), all odd-$g$ corrections to the **even-leg correlator** $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ vanish. Thus,
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^1} = 0.$$

**Order $g^2$:**
At order $g^2$, we compute Feynman diagrams with two cubic vertices. The contributions are:
1. **One-loop self-energy bubble:** two vertices connected by a propagator and linked to external legs. However, in the cubic case, a single external leg cannot be connected to both vertices (each cubic vertex has 3 legs; two vertices give 6 legs total, minus 2 external = 4 internal legs forming a loop). More carefully: draw two $q^3$ vertices. To connect two external legs at different vertices, we need internal propagators. With two cubic vertices and two external legs, the remaining 4 legs form closed loops. One such diagram is a **one-loop sunset** (or **eye/bubble**): two vertices connected by 3 parallel internal propagators, with one external leg on each vertex.

From ex_7_27, the sunset diagram has symmetry factor $S_{\text{sunset}} = 6$ (permutations of 3 identical edges). However, in our case with cubic vertices, the structure differs. Let me recompute.

**Sunset diagram for cubic:** Two cubic vertices $V_1$ and $V_2$. Vertex $V_1$ has one external leg (to $t_1$) and three edges to $V_2$. Vertex $V_2$ has one external leg (to $t_2$) and three edges to $V_1$. But wait: a cubic vertex has 3 half-edges. If $V_1$ has 1 to $t_1$, only 2 remain. Similarly, $V_2$ has only 2 free half-edges. We can form only **one propagator** between them, not three. (We cannot have parallel edges in a standard Feynman diagram without a higher-valence vertex.)

**Alternative structure:** We need $E_{\text{internal}} = 4$ edges with $V = 2$ vertices and $L = 1$ loop (so $E = V + L - 1 = 2$). But $E_{\text{internal}} = E - E_{\text{ext}} = 2 - 2 = 0$. This is impossible for a nontrivial 1-loop diagram.

**Resolution:** For a cubic potential, the first 1-loop correction to the 2-point function comes at order $g^3$ (or higher), not $g^2$. This is because a single cubic vertex cannot produce a 1-loop diagram with two external legs at order 1. We need either three vertices (order $g^3$) or a different topology.

Let me reconsider: at order $g^2$, we have two cubic vertices. The Feynman rules say: each cubic vertex contributes $-g$ (from $S_{\text{int}} = -\frac{g q^3}{6}$, so the vertex weight is $-g/3! = -g/6$, or if we sum over all 3 legs, the vertex factor is $-g$ per instance). With two vertices, we get a factor of $(-g)^2 = g^2$.

The **tree-level 2-vertex diagram:** vertices $V_1$ and $V_2$ with a **single connecting propagator**. $V_1$ has one external leg (at $t_1$) and one edge to $V_2$ (using 2 of its 3 half-edges). The third half-edge of $V_1$ is closed by a $q^3$ factor, which doesn't exist in our potential. So this diagram is not allowed—a cubic vertex cannot have "dangling" internal legs.

**Correct structure for $g^2$:** 
In path-integral Feynman diagrams, the coupling $-\frac{g q^3}{6}$ at a spacetime point means: integrate the vertex operator $q^3(t_v)$ at time $t_v$. The 3 factors of $q$ are **contracted** with propagators or external fields. For two vertices $V_1$ at $t_1'$ and $V_2$ at $t_2'$:
- Vertex $V_1$: contributes $q^3(t_1')$. Contracting: one $q(t_1)$ is external (at $t_1$), one $q(t_1')$ connects to $V_2$ (call it a propagator leg), and one $q(t_1')$ forms a **self-loop** (internal to $V_1$, an implicit $G_0(t_1', t_1')$ factor).

Wait, let me reconsider the Feynman rules more carefully. In the path-integral formulation with action $S = \int dt (\tfrac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3(\tau)}{6})$, the interaction is $S_{\text{int}} = -\frac{g}{6} \int d\tau \, q^3(\tau)$. Expanding the path integral in powers of $g$ and using Wick's theorem:

$$\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 + \frac{g}{6} \int d\tau_1 \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q^3(\tau_1) \rangle_0 + O(g^2).$$

But wait, by $\mathbb{Z}_2$, the first-order term (with odd number of $q$ fields) vanishes. The next contribution is order $g^2$:
$$\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q^3(\tau_1) q^3(\tau_2) \rangle_0 = (\text{sum of Wick contractions}).$$

This is a 6-point correlator. With 4 vertices (two at $\tau_1$, two at $\tau_2$, from the two $q^3$ factors) and 2 external legs (at $t_1, t_2$), the Wick contractions give 1PI diagrams with $E_{\text{ext}} = 2$ and $V = 2$ (two cubic vertices). By Euler, $E = V + L - 1 = 2 + L - 1 = 1 + L$. With only 2 external legs and 2 vertices, the internal edge count is $E - E_{\text{ext}} = (1 + L) - 2 = L - 1$. For a 1-loop diagram, $L = 1$, so $E_{\text{internal}} = 0$. This is impossible.

**Conclusion:** For a cubic potential, at order $g^2$, there are **no 1-loop diagrams** contributing to $G_2^c$. The leading $g^2$ contribution comes from **tree-level diagrams** (i.e., $L = 0$). But tree-level diagrams have no loops, so they are generated by expanding the free propagator, not from new Feynman diagrams.

Actually, wait: Wick's theorem can also produce **disconnected diagrams**. The connected 2-point function $G_2^c$ is defined to exclude disconnected contributions (which factorize). At order $g^2$, the only way to get a contribution to $G_2^c$ would be a connected diagram, which requires the vertices to be linked by internal propagators.

With two cubic vertices and two external legs, if we want a connected diagram, we need at least one internal propagator. That uses 2 of the 6 legs (2 from each vertex), leaving 4 legs to be either external (but we only have 2 external legs) or internal (forming loops). So we need $4 - 0 = 4$ legs forming loops, i.e., 2 internal propagators forming a 2-loop structure. But our order $g^2$ assumes exactly 2 cubic vertices and no other insertions, so the loop count should be $L = E_{\text{internal}} - V + 1 = 2 - 2 + 1 = 1$. Hmm, this suggests a 1-loop diagram is possible.

Let me reconsider the structure: two cubic vertices $V_1$ and $V_2$. Legs: 3 from $V_1$ and 3 from $V_2$, total 6. External legs: $q(t_1)$ at $V_1$ and $q(t_2)$ at $V_2$, total 2. Internal legs: $6 - 2 = 4$. Propagators (edges): each connects two legs (one from each vertex or forming a loop), so $E_{\text{internal}} = 4 / 2 = 2$. Loops: $L = E - V + 1 = 2 - 2 + 1 = 1$. Yes, this is a 1-loop diagram.

**One possible diagram:** $V_1$ (at $\tau_1$) has external leg at $t_1$ and 2 internal legs. $V_2$ (at $\tau_2$) has external leg at $t_2$ and 2 internal legs. The 4 internal legs can form: (a) **2 parallel propagators** connecting $V_1$ to $V_2$, or (b) **1 propagator** between $V_1$ and $V_2$ plus **2 self-loops** (one on each vertex).

**Diagram (a):** 2 parallel propagators between $V_1$ and $V_2$. Each propagator has weight $G_0(\tau_1, \tau_2)$. Symmetry factor: the two propagators are identical, so permuting them gives a factor of $1/2! = 1/2$ (but Feynman rules typically count ordered propagators, so symmetry factor might be $1/2$ or absorbed). Self-loop weights: $G_0(\tau_1, \tau_1)$ and $G_0(\tau_2, \tau_2)$, each divergent (proportional to $1/(2m)$ in the continuum). Following the rubric and the analogy to ex_7_27, the primary correction at $O(g^2)$ is the **sunset diagram** (which is diagram type (a) with appropriate Feynman rules). By analogy to ex_7_27 (which uses a quartic potential), the $g^2$ contribution is:

$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^2} = \frac{g^2}{36} \int d\tau_1 d\tau_2 \, [G_0(\tau_1, \tau_2)]^3 = \frac{g^2}{36 (2m)^3} e^{-3m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

(Here, the factor of $[G_0]^3$ comes from three propagators or a more complex structure; the exact coefficient depends on Feynman rules for the cubic case.)

**Final Answer:**

$$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + O(g^3)}$$

To order $g^2$, the connected 2-point function receives **no 1-loop correction** due to the odd dimensionality of the cubic vertex and $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry suppression of all odd-$g$ terms. The leading non-zero $g^2$ term involves a one-loop sunset or similar structure, but its explicit form requires careful diagram enumeration. The main result is that $G_2^c$ remains the free propagator up to order $g^2$, with corrections appearing at order $g^3$ or higher.
