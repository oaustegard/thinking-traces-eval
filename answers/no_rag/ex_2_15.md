# Solution to Problem ex_2_15: Asymptotic Expansion of Bessel Function I_0(a)

## Problem Setup

We are given the Bessel function of the first kind:
$$I_0(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{a\cos\theta} d\theta$$

and we must find the asymptotic expansion as $a \to +\infty$ and as $a \to i\infty$ using steepest descent/stationary phase methods.

## General Asymptotic Strategy

For an integral of the form $I(a) = \int_0^{2\pi} e^{aS(\theta)} d\theta$ where $S(\theta) = \cos\theta$, as $a \to \infty$ (in some direction in the complex plane), the asymptotic behavior is dominated by the **stationary points** where $S'(\theta) = 0$. We apply the **saddle-point method** (Laplace method).

## (i) Asymptotic Behavior as $a \to +\infty$ (Real)

### Finding Stationary Points

For $S(\theta) = \cos\theta$:
$$\frac{dS}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta = 0$$

In $[0, 2\pi]$, this gives $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$.

### Evaluating at Stationary Points

- At $\theta = 0$: $\cos 0 = 1$ (maximum since $S'' = -\cos\theta|_0 = -1 < 0$)
- At $\theta = \pi$: $\cos\pi = -1$ (minimum since $S'' = -\cos\pi = 1 > 0$)

As $a \to +\infty$ with $a > 0$, the maximum at $\theta = 0$ dominates exponentially. The contribution from $\theta = \pi$ is exponentially suppressed by factor $e^{-2a}$.

### Steepest Descent Calculation at $\theta = 0$

Expand $S(\theta)$ near $\theta = 0$:
$$S(\theta) = 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2} + O(\theta^4)$$

The integral near this point becomes:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a\theta^2/2} d\theta = \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}}$$

Thus:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}}$$

### Second-Order Correction

Including the next term in the expansion of $S(\theta) = 1 - \theta^2/2 + \theta^4/24 + \ldots$, and refining the Gaussian integral:

$$I_0(a) = \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}} \left(1 - \frac{1}{8a} + O(a^{-2})\right)$$

## (ii) Asymptotic Behavior as $a \to i\infty$ (Purely Imaginary)

Let $a = ia'$ where $a' \to +\infty$ with $a' > 0$. Then:
$$I_0(ia') = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{ia'\cos\theta} d\theta$$

This is now a **stationary phase** integral (oscillatory).

### Stationary Points Remain the Same

The phase is $\phi(\theta) = \cos\theta$, with stationary points at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$.

### Phase and Contributions

- At $\theta = 0$: $\phi = 1$, $\phi'' = -1$
- At $\theta = \pi$: $\phi = -1$, $\phi'' = 1$

For large $a' > 0$, by stationary phase both endpoints contribute with comparable amplitudes (oscillations), but different phases.

### Stationary Phase Formula

Near $\theta = 0$:
$$\int e^{ia' \cos\theta} d\theta \approx e^{ia'} \int e^{-ia'\theta^2/2} d\theta = e^{ia'} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi i}{a'}} = e^{ia'} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a'}} e^{i\pi/4}$$

Near $\theta = \pi$, setting $\theta = \pi - \phi$:
$$\int e^{-ia'\cos\phi} d\phi \approx e^{-ia'} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a'}} e^{i\pi/4}$$

### Final Result for $a \to i\infty$

Combining both contributions:
$$I_0(ia') \approx \frac{1}{2\pi} \left(e^{ia'} + e^{-ia'}\right) \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a'}} e^{i\pi/4}$$

$$I_0(ia') \approx \frac{\cos a'}{\sqrt{\pi a'}} e^{i\pi/4} \left(1 + O(a'^{-1})\right)$$

More explicitly, using real notation (recognizing this connects to modified Bessel functions):
$$I_0(ia') = J_0(a') = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi a'}} \cos\left(a' - \frac{\pi}{4}\right) + O(a'^{-3/2})$$

## Summary of Answers

$$\boxed{\text{(i) As } a \to +\infty: \quad I_0(a) \sim \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}}\left(1 - \frac{1}{8a} + \ldots\right)}$$

$$\boxed{\text{(ii) As } a \to i\infty: \quad I_0(ia) \sim \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi a}} \cos\left(a - \frac{\pi}{4}\right)\left(1 + O(a^{-1})\right)}$$
