# Solution: Asymptotic Expansion of Bessel Function I_0(a)

**Strategy:** Using steepest descent / stationary phase methods from the Wallis formula example. The integral I_0(a) = (1/(2π)) · ∫_0^{2π} e^{a cos θ} dθ is converted to exponential form, the phase function cos θ is analyzed for its critical points, and we expand around them to extract asymptotic behavior.

## Setup

We write the integral in exponential form:
$$I_0(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{a\cos\theta}\,d\theta$$

For asymptotic analysis with large parameter a, the key is to identify where the phase function $f(\theta) = \cos\theta$ is stationary (has extrema).

## Phase Function Analysis

The phase function $f(\theta) = \cos\theta$ has:
- **Maximum:** $f(\theta) = 1$ at $\theta = 0$ (and by periodicity, at $\theta = 2\pi$)
- **Minimum:** $f(\theta) = -1$ at $\theta = \pi$

For **large positive a**, the integral is dominated by the maximum at $\theta = 0$.
For **large imaginary a = ia** (with a real), the phase becomes $\cos\theta = \text{Re}(e^{i\theta})$, and the integral's behavior depends on both real and imaginary parts.

## (i) Asymptotic expansion for a → +∞

Following the steepest descent method (as in Wallis), expand $\cos\theta$ near $\theta = 0$:
$$\cos\theta = 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2} + \frac{\theta^4}{24} + O(\theta^6)$$

The integral becomes approximately:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{a(1 - \theta^2/2 + \theta^4/24 + \cdots)}\,d\theta$$

$$= \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{-a\theta^2/2 + a\theta^4/24 + \cdots}\,d\theta$$

By steepest descent, the integral is dominated by the region near $\theta = 0$. Extend the upper limit to infinity (exponential decay makes boundary negligible):

$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-a\theta^2/2}\left(1 + \frac{a\theta^4}{24} + \cdots\right)\,d\theta$$

The leading Gaussian integral is:
$$\int_0^{\infty} e^{-a\theta^2/2}\,d\theta = \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2a}}$$

**First term:**
$$I_0^{(0)}(a) = \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2a}} = \sqrt{\frac{e^a}{4\pi a}}$$

**Second term (from the $\theta^4/24$ correction):**
$$\int_0^{\infty} \theta^4 e^{-a\theta^2/2}\,d\theta = \frac{3}{8} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2a^5}}$$

This contributes:
$$I_0^{(1)}(a) = \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{a}{24} \cdot \frac{3}{8} \sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2a^5}} = \frac{e^a}{128\pi a^{5/2}} \cdot 3\sqrt{2\pi} \sim \frac{e^a}{128a^{5/2}}$$

**Result for a → +∞:**
$$\boxed{I_0(a) \sim \sqrt{\frac{e^a}{4\pi a}}\left(1 + \frac{1}{8a} + O(a^{-2})\right)}$$

## (ii) Asymptotic expansion for a → i∞

For $a = ia_0$ with $a_0 \to +\infty$ (purely imaginary):
$$I_0(ia_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{ia_0\cos\theta}\,d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{ia_0\cos\theta}\,d\theta$$

This is an oscillatory integral with phase $\phi(\theta) = a_0\cos\theta$. The critical point is at $\theta = 0$ where $\phi'(0) = 0$ and $\phi''(0) = -a_0 < 0$.

The stationary phase method gives (for large $a_0$):
$$I_0(ia_0) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{ia_0(1 - \theta^2/2 + \cdots)}\,d\theta$$

Substituting $u = \sqrt{a_0}\theta$ and evaluating the stationary phase integral:
$$I_0(ia_0) \approx \frac{1}{2\pi} e^{ia_0} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-ia_0 u^2/2}\,du = \frac{e^{ia_0}}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{ia_0}} = \frac{e^{ia_0}}{\sqrt{2\pi a_0}} \cdot e^{i\pi/4}$$

Including the second-order correction from $\cos\theta \approx 1 - \theta^2/2 + \theta^4/24$:

**Result for a → i∞:**
$$\boxed{I_0(ia_0) \sim e^{i\pi/4}\sqrt{\frac{e^{ia_0}}{2\pi a_0}}\left(1 - \frac{i}{8a_0} + O(a_0^{-2})\right)}$$

Note: In the purely imaginary case, the dominant exponential is $e^{ia_0}$ (oscillatory), and the envelope decays as $a_0^{-1/2}$.
