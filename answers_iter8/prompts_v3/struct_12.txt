Example 1: **Approach:** Using Calculus and Symmetry

**Problem:** Find the maximum product of the areas of the four regions formed when two lines intersect inside a unit square.

**Step 1 — Define Areas and Product:**
Let the areas of the four regions be $A_1, A_2, A_3, A_4$, and the product be $P = A_1 A_2 A_3 A_4$.  The sum of the areas is $A_1 + A_2 + A_3 + A_4 = 1$.

**Step 2 — Identify a Symmetric Configuration:**
Conjecture that the maximum occurs when the intersection point is at the center of the square, and the lines are parallel to the sides.

**Step 3 — Compute Areas for the Symmetric Configuration:**
If lines are $x = 1/2$ and $y = 1/2$, the four areas are $A_1 = A_2 = A_3 = A_4 = 1/4$.

**Step 4 — Calculate the Product:**
Compute $P = (1/4) \cdot (1/4) \cdot (1/4) \cdot (1/4) = 1/256$.

**Step 5 — Apply AM-GM inequality:**
Recognize that by AM-GM, $\sqrt[4]{A_1 A_2 A_3 A_4} \le \frac{A_1 + A_2 + A_3 + A_4}{4}$.

**Step 6 — Substitute and Simplify:**
Substitute $A_1 + A_2 + A_3 + A_4 = 1$ to get $\sqrt[4]{P} \le 1/4$, and therefore $P \le (1/4)^4 = 1/256$.

**Step 7 — State the Solution:**
Since the product $1/256$ is achieved with lines parallel to the sides and intersecting at the center, and no larger product can be obtained (by AM-GM), the maximum product is $1/256$.

**Answer:** $\boxed{1/256}$
Example 2: **Approach:** Geometric Argument and Counting Regions

**Problem:** Find the maximum number of different permutations of the indices of 8 points obtained by projecting them onto a directed line in the plane.

**Step 1 — Recognize Critical Directions:**
Identify that the order of the projections of the points changes when the projection line's direction is perpendicular to the line segment connecting any two points.

**Step 2 — Determine Number of Critical Directions:**
Since there are 8 points, there are $\binom{8}{2} = 28$ pairs of points. Each pair yields a critical direction.

**Step 3 — Visualize the Space of Directions:**
Think of the space of directions as a circle (unit vectors). Each critical direction corresponds to two antipodal points on this circle.

**Step 4 — Partition the Space of Directions:**
The 28 pairs of antipodal critical directions divide the circle into intervals, where each interval has the same permutation.

**Step 5 — Estimate the Maximum Number of Permutations:**
The maximum number of distinct permutations is determined by the maximum number of intervals formed.

**Step 6 — Relate to Arrangement of Lines:**
In the space of directions, the perpendiculars can be considered like lines on a circle. These lines will intersect and will produce distinct sections.

**Step 7 — Compute Maximum number of regions:**
$N = m^2-m+2$, where m is number of lines and N is number of regions in the space of directions. Substitute $m=28$. Therefore number of regions is  $28^2-28+2=758$.

**Answer:** $\boxed{758}$
Example 3: **Approach:** Volume Calculation via Integration

**Problem:** Find the volume of the solid formed by all points $P=(x,y,z)$ in the cuboid $K$ defined by $|x| \le \sqrt{3}$, $|y| \le \sqrt{3}$, and $0 \le z \le 2$, such that $\overline{OP} \le \overline{PH}$, where $O$ is the origin and $H$ is the perpendicular projection of $P$ onto the plane $z=2$.

**Step 1 — Define the Region:** Define the region in terms of inequalities. The given conditions transform to $x^2 + y^2 \le 4 - 4z$, along with $-\sqrt{3} \le x \le \sqrt{3}$, $-\sqrt{3} \le y \le \sqrt{3}$, and $0 \le z \le 1$.

**Step 2 — Identify Cross-Sections:** Observe that the cross-sections are formed by the intersection of the disk $x^2 + y^2 \le 4 - 4z$ and the square defined by the inequalities for $x$ and $y$.

**Step 3 — Determine the Integration Limits:** Since $x^2 + y^2 \le 4-4z$ implies $4-4z \ge 0$, and also $z$ is bounded by 2 from the cuboid, the limits of integration for $z$ are $0 \le z \le 1$.

**Step 4 — Divide the z-Interval:** Divide the interval of $z$ into two subintervals based on the relative size of the disk and the square: Case 1: $0 \le z \le 1/4$, where the disk completely contains the square. Case 2: $1/4 < z \le 1$, where the disk is inside the square.

**Step 5 — Integrate over Case 1:** For $0 \le z \le 1/4$, the area of the cross-section is the area of the square, $12$. Integrate the area with respect to z: $\int_0^{1/4} 12 \, dz = 12(1/4) = 3$.

**Step 6 — Integrate over Case 2:** For $1/4 < z \le 1$, the area of the cross-section is $\pi(4-4z)$. Integrate the area with respect to z: $\int_{1/4}^{1} \pi(4-4z) \, dz = \pi[4z - 2z^2]_{1/4}^{1} = \pi[(4-2) - (1-1/8)] = \frac{9\pi}{8}$.

**Step 7 — Sum the Volumes:** Add the volumes calculated from the two cases: $3 + \frac{9\pi}{8}$.

**Answer:** $\boxed{3 + \frac{9\pi}{8}}$
