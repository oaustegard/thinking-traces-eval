**Step 2 — Apply Greedy Algorithm:** Sort the coins in descending order of value, $c_1 \ge c_2 \ge \dots \ge c_k$. Place coins into groups one by one, starting with Group 1, until the sum of the values in the group exceeds 1.

**Step 3 — Analyze Potential Failure:**  Consider the scenario where the greedy algorithm requires more than 100 groups. Let this occur when forming Group 101.

**Step 4 — State Key Condition:** Let $c$ be the value of the first coin placed in Group 101. Because the coin $c$ couldn't fit in any of the first 100 groups, it must be the case that if added to any group $i$ ($1 \le i \le 100$), then the sum would exceed 1.

**Step 5 — Infer Group Sum Condition:** Let $S_i$ be the sum of the values of the coins in group $i$ before coin $c$ is considered.  Then, for each $i$, $S_i + c > 1$, which implies $S_i > 1 - c$.

**Step 6 — Relate Total Sum and c:** The total sum of the values of the coins in the first 100 groups is $\sum_{i=1}^{100} S_i > \sum_{i=1}^{100} (1-c) = 100(1-c)$.

**Step 7 — Conclude:**  Since the total value is $\le 99.5$, the greedy algorithm, which tries to pack as much as possible in each group before moving to the next, indeed works.

Example 3: **Approach:** Greedy Algorithm and Properties of Partitioning

**Problem:** Find the maximum value of $S$ such that for any set of real numbers from $[0, 1]$ with a sum not exceeding $S$, it's always possible to partition the numbers into two groups with sums not greater than $9$.

**Step 1 — Analyze the Greedy Algorithm Behavior:**
Consider applying a greedy algorithm where elements are sorted in descending order and placed into the group with the smaller current sum. Observe that if the greedy algorithm fails (one group's sum exceeds 9), the difference between the final sums of the two groups is at most the largest element (which is at most 1).

**Step 2 — Define Sums and the Failing Condition:**
Let $S_1$ and $S_2$ be the sums of the two groups produced by the greedy algorithm, with $S_1 \le S_2$. If the greedy algorithm fails, then $S_2 > 9$.

**Step 3 — Bound the Smaller Sum:**
If $S_2 > 9$, then because $|S_2 - S_1| \le 1$, we must have $S_1 \ge S_2 - 1 > 9 - 1 = 8$.

**Step 4 — Relate the Sums to the Total Sum:**
The total sum of all the numbers is $S_1 + S_2 = T \le S$.

**Step 5 — Establish a Lower Bound on the Total Sum When the Greedy Fails:**
Since $S_2 > 9$ and $S_1 > 8$, then $T = S_1 + S_2 > 8 + 9 = 17$. This implies that if the greedy approach fails, then $T > 17$. Thus, to guarantee a partition, the total sum must be $\le 17$.

**Step 6 — Consider a Specific Partitioning:**
If $S = 17$, consider the set of 17 numbers, each with value $1$. Their total sum is 17. The partition into two groups (8 elements and 9 elements) will give us sums 8 and 9 which are $\le 9$.

**Step 7 — State the Solution:**
If the sum $S \le 17$, any partition can be divided into two groups whose sums are $\le 9$, so $S_{\max} = 17$.

**Answer:** $\boxed{17}$
