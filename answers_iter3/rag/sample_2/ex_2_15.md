# Solution: ex_2_15 — Bessel Function I_0(a) Asymptotic Expansion

## Relevance of Examples

**Retrieved Example 1 (ex_2_14_wallis):** Relevant for the steepest descent / stationary phase methodology. The Wallis formula solution demonstrates how to (i) recognize an integral with an exponential integrand, (ii) identify a phase function and its extremum, (iii) substitute to move the extremum to a convenient location, (iv) expand near the extremum, and (v) extract the leading asymptotic behavior from a Gaussian integral. This technique transfers directly.

**Retrieved Example 2 (ex_9_13_amitsur_levitzki) and Example 3 (ex_2_20_morse):** Not relevant to asymptotic analysis of Bessel functions; these address algebraic identities and Morse coordinate transformations, respectively.

## Key Differences from ex_2_14_wallis

The Wallis example uses $W_n = \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin^n x\, dx$ with phase $f(x) = \log\sin x$ where the maximum occurs *at the boundary* $x = \pi/2$. In the Bessel problem, we have an exponential integrand $e^{a\cos\theta}$ over a closed interval $[0, 2\pi]$, and the phase $\phi(\theta) = \cos\theta$ has *interior* maxima at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = 2\pi$ (identified modulo $2\pi$). Additionally, we must analyze *two different regimes*: $a \to +\infty$ and $a \to i\infty$, whereas Wallis analyzed only one regime.

---

## Solution

### Part 1: Asymptotic Expansion for $a \to +\infty$

Start with the integral representation:
$$I_0(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{a\cos\theta}\, d\theta$$

**Identify the phase.** The exponent is $a\cos\theta$. As $a \to +\infty$, the integrand concentrates near the maximum of $\cos\theta$, which occurs at $\theta = 0$ (and $\theta = 2\pi$, which is the same point modulo $2\pi$).

**Substitute to bring the maximum to the origin.** Set $\theta = \epsilon$ with $\epsilon$ small:
$$\cos\epsilon \approx 1 - \frac{\epsilon^2}{2} + \frac{\epsilon^4}{24} - \cdots$$

The integral becomes:
$$I_0(a) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{a(1 - \epsilon^2/2 + \epsilon^4/24 - \cdots)}\, d\epsilon = \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{-a\epsilon^2/2 + a\epsilon^4/24 - \cdots}\, d\epsilon$$

Near $\theta = 0$, the exponential $e^{-a\epsilon^2/2}$ decays rapidly for $a\epsilon^2 \gtrsim 1$, i.e., $\epsilon \gtrsim \sqrt{1/a}$. For large $a$, contributions from $|\epsilon| > O(1/\sqrt{a})$ are negligible.

**Apply the Gaussian approximation.** For the leading term, approximate:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a\epsilon^2/2}\, d\epsilon = \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}} = \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}}$$

**Compute the next-order correction.** Include the $+a\epsilon^4/24$ term from the expansion:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a\epsilon^2/2 + a\epsilon^4/24}\, d\epsilon$$

For large $a$, the fourth-order term perturbs the Gaussian. Expand $e^{a\epsilon^4/24} \approx 1 + \frac{a\epsilon^4}{24} + \cdots$:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \left[\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}} + \frac{a}{24}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \epsilon^4 e^{-a\epsilon^2/2}\, d\epsilon\right]$$

The second integral evaluates to:
$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \epsilon^4 e^{-a\epsilon^2/2}\, d\epsilon = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{2}{\sqrt{a}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{a}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{a}} = \frac{3\sqrt{2\pi}}{2a^{5/2}}$$

Thus:
$$I_0(a) \approx \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}} + \frac{e^a}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{a}{24} \cdot \frac{3\sqrt{2\pi}}{2a^{5/2}} = \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}}\left(1 + \frac{1}{8a} + \cdots\right)$$

**Leading and second terms for $a \to +\infty$:**
$$I_0(a) \sim \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}} + \frac{e^a}{8a\sqrt{2\pi a}} = \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}}\left(1 + \frac{1}{8a}\right)$$

### Part 2: Asymptotic Expansion for $a \to i\infty$

Set $a = ib$ with $b > 0$ real, so $b \to +\infty$:
$$I_0(ib) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_0^{2\pi} e^{ib\cos\theta}\, d\theta$$

**Identify stationary points.** The phase is $\phi(\theta) = b\cos\theta$. Stationary points satisfy $\phi'(\theta) = -b\sin\theta = 0$, occurring at $\theta = 0$ and $\theta = \pi$.

**Near $\theta = 0$:**
$$\cos\theta \approx 1 - \frac{\theta^2}{2} + \cdots \Rightarrow e^{ib\cos\theta} \approx e^{ib} e^{-ib\theta^2/2}$$

**Near $\theta = \pi$:**
$$\cos(\pi + \eta) \approx -1 + \frac{\eta^2}{2} + \cdots \Rightarrow e^{ib\cos(\pi+\eta)} \approx e^{-ib} e^{ib\eta^2/2}$$

**Combine contributions.** Each stationary point contributes a Fresnel integral:
$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{ib\theta^2/2}\, d\theta = \sqrt{\frac{4\pi}{2b}} e^{i\pi/4} = \sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{b}} e^{i\pi/4}$$

The two stationary points contribute with the same phase in the exponent. Combining:
$$I_0(ib) \approx \frac{1}{2\pi}\left[e^{ib}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{b}} e^{-i\pi/4} + e^{-ib}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{b}} e^{i\pi/4}\right]$$
$$= \frac{1}{2\pi}\sqrt{\frac{2\pi}{b}}\left[e^{i(b - \pi/4)} + e^{-i(b - \pi/4)}\right] = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi b}}\cos(b - \pi/4)$$

This is the leading term.

---

## Final Answer

$$\boxed{a \to +\infty: \quad I_0(a) \sim \frac{e^a}{\sqrt{2\pi a}}\left(1 + \frac{1}{8a} + \cdots\right)}$$

$$\boxed{a \to i\infty \text{ (write } a = ib, b \to \infty): \quad I_0(ib) \sim \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi b}}\cos\left(b - \frac{\pi}{4}\right)}$$
