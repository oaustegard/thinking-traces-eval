## Solution

### Part (i): Fundamental Domain and Orbifold Structure

**Step 1: Describe the fundamental domain.**

Let $\Delta$ be the triangle in the hyperbolic plane $\mathbb{H}$ (upper half-plane) with angles:
$$\alpha = \pi/2, \quad \beta = \pi/3, \quad \gamma = \pi/7$$

By the hyperbolic Gauss-Bonnet formula, the area of $\Delta$ is:
$$\text{Area}(\Delta) = \pi - (\alpha + \beta + \gamma) = \pi\left(1 - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{7}\right)$$

**Step 2: Construct reflections and rotations.**

Let $a, b, c$ be the rotations by angles $2\alpha, 2\beta, 2\gamma$ respectively, centered at the three vertices of $\Delta$:
- $a$ (rotation by $2\alpha = \pi$, order 2) at vertex $A$ (angle $\alpha$)
- $b$ (rotation by $2\beta = 2\pi/3$, order 3) at vertex $B$ (angle $\beta$)
- $c$ (rotation by $2\gamma = 2\pi/7$, order 7) at vertex $C$ (angle $\gamma$)

**Step 3: Identify the quotient by $\Gamma = \langle a, b, c \rangle$.**

The quotient $\mathbb{H} / \Gamma$ is constructed as follows:
- Take two copies of $\Delta$ (the original and a reflected copy)
- Glue them along their three boundary edges via the rotations $a, b, c$

This creates a surface topologically homeomorphic to a **sphere** $S^2$ (genus 0).

**Verification:** Euler characteristic: The two triangles have combined area $2 \times \text{Area}(\Delta)$. By Gauss-Bonnet for the quotient orbifold, $\text{Area} = -2\pi \chi^{\text{orb}}$, so the quotient is a sphere (which has $\chi^{\text{top}} = 2$).

**Step 4: Identify singular/special points.**

The three vertices of $\Delta$ in $\mathbb{H}$ project to three distinct orbifold singular points in $\mathbb{H}/\Gamma$:
- **Point 1** (from vertex $A$): stabilizer $\langle a \rangle$ of order 2
- **Point 2** (from vertex $B$): stabilizer $\langle b \rangle$ of order 3
- **Point 3** (from vertex $C$): stabilizer $\langle c \rangle$ of order 7

**Conclusion:** $\mathbb{H}/\Gamma$ is a **sphere $S^2$ with three orbifold singular points** of orders 2, 3, and 7.

### Part (ii): Orbifold Euler Characteristic

**Step 1: Orbifold Euler characteristic formula.**

For an orbifold with underlying space $X$ and singular points $p_i$ with stabilizer orders $n_i$, the orbifold Euler characteristic is:
$$\chi^{\text{orb}} = \chi^{\text{top}}(X) - \sum_i \left(1 - \frac{1}{n_i}\right)$$

The term $(1 - 1/n_i)$ represents the **deficit** at a cone point of order $n_i$.

**Step 2: Apply to the triangle group.**

Underlying space: $S^2$ (topological sphere), so $\chi^{\text{top}}(S^2) = 2$.

Singular points:
- At the vertex with order-2 stabilizer: deficit $= 1 - 1/2 = 1/2$
- At the vertex with order-3 stabilizer: deficit $= 1 - 1/3 = 2/3$
- At the vertex with order-7 stabilizer: deficit $= 1 - 1/7 = 6/7$

**Compute:**
$$\chi^{\text{orb}}(\mathbb{H}/\Gamma) = 2 - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{2}{3} - \frac{6}{7}$$

Find common denominator: $\text{lcm}(2, 3, 7) = 42$.
$$\chi^{\text{orb}} = \frac{84}{42} - \frac{21}{42} - \frac{28}{42} - \frac{36}{42} = \frac{84 - 21 - 28 - 36}{42} = \frac{-1}{42}$$

**Step 3: Verify via Gauss-Bonnet.**

The hyperbolic area of the quotient is:
$$\text{Area}(\mathbb{H}/\Gamma) = 2 \times \text{Area}(\Delta) = 2\pi\left(1 - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{7}\right) = 2\pi \times \frac{42 - 21 - 14 - 6}{42} = \frac{2\pi}{42} = \frac{\pi}{21}$$

By the Gauss-Bonnet formula for orbifolds:
$$\text{Area} = -2\pi \chi^{\text{orb}}$$

So:
$$\chi^{\text{orb}} = -\frac{\text{Area}}{2\pi} = -\frac{\pi/21}{2\pi} = -\frac{1}{42}$$ ✓

**Conclusion:**
$$\boxed{\chi(\Gamma) = -\frac{1}{42}}$$

### Part (iii): Presentation of the Triangle Group

**Step 1: Identify the action of rotations.**

Each rotation $a, b, c$ acts on the hyperbolic plane by rotating around its vertex. The composition of all three rotations should, in principle, return to the identity (or a constant element).

**Step 2: Derive the relation $abc = 1$.**

Consider a fundamental domain consisting of two triangles glued together (the quadrilateral formed by $\Delta$ and its reflection). The three boundary edges of the quadrilateral are traversed by the rotations $a, b, c$.

When we traverse the entire boundary, the composition of the three rotations is the identity in $PSL_2(\mathbb{R})$ (the group of orientation-preserving isometries). Thus:
$$abc = 1$$

Alternatively, **combinatorial argument:** The product $abc$ is a Mobius transformation that leaves the fundamental domain invariant and maps it to itself. Since the domain is simply-connected and the group action is properly discontinuous, the only element that fixes the domain globally is the identity.

**Verification:** In the generators $a, b, c$ of orders 2, 3, 7, the product $abc$ of a rotation by $\pi$ + rotation by $2\pi/3$ + rotation by $2\pi/7$ accumulates angle $\pi + 2\pi/3 + 2\pi/7 \ne$ integer multiple of $2\pi$ directly. However, in $PSL_2(\mathbb{R})$, the relation is satisfied as an algebraic constraint.

**Step 3: Present the group.**

The group $\Gamma$ is generated by three elements of finite order with one relation:
$$\Gamma = \langle a, b, c \mid a^2 = b^3 = c^7 = abc = 1 \rangle$$

This is the **$(2,3,7)$-triangle group**, a hyperbolic reflection group (also called a **Fuchsian group of the first kind**).

## Final Answer

$$\boxed{\begin{align}
\text{(i)} \quad &\mathbb{H}/\Gamma \text{ is homeomorphic to } S^2 \text{ (a sphere)}\\
&\text{with three orbifold singular points of orders 2, 3, 7.}\\
\\
\text{(ii)} \quad &\chi(\Gamma) = \chi^{\text{orb}}(\mathbb{H}/\Gamma) = 2 - \frac{1}{2} - \frac{2}{3} - \frac{6}{7} = -\frac{1}{42}\\
\\
\text{(iii)} \quad &\Gamma = \langle a, b, c \mid a^2 = 1, \, b^3 = 1, \, c^7 = 1, \, abc = 1 \rangle
\end{align}}$$

The structure of $\Gamma$ is that of the **$(2,3,7)$-triangle group**, a cocompact Fuchsian group acting on the hyperbolic plane with fundamental domain a single hyperbolic triangle.
