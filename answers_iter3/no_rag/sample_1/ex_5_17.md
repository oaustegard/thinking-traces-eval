# Solution to ex_5_17: Structure and Generators of Congruence Subgroups Gamma(N)

## Part (a): Freeness of Gamma(N)

### For $N \geq 3$: Gamma(N) is Free

**Action on the upper half-plane:**
The group $\Gamma(N)$ acts on the hyperbolic upper half-plane $H = \{z = x + iy : y > 0\}$ by Möbius transformations. Every matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix} \in \Gamma(N)$ acts as:
$$z \mapsto \frac{az + b}{cz + d}$$

**Freeness of the action:**
A key property: if $A \in \Gamma(N)$ has $A$ congruent to the identity modulo $N$ and $A$ has order $> 1$, then $A$ must fix a point in $H$. However, for $N \geq 3$, the only torsion elements in $SL_2(\mathbb{Z})$ are $\pm I$ (of order 2) and finitely many conjugates. For $N \geq 3$, the congruence condition $A \equiv I \pmod{N}$ excludes these non-trivial torsion elements. Thus no non-identity element of $\Gamma(N)$ has a fixed point in $H$ — the action is **free**.

**Consequence via algebraic topology:**
A free action of a group $\Gamma$ on a contractible space (like $H$) means the quotient $H / \Gamma$ is an Eilenberg-MacLane space $K(\Gamma, 1)$. For $N \geq 3$, the quotient is an open Riemann surface (with finitely many punctures), which is homotopy equivalent to a graph. The fundamental group of a graph (one-dimensional CW complex) is a free group. Hence $\Gamma(N)$ is **free**.

### For $\Gamma(2)$: Direct Product Structure

The element $-I \in \Gamma(2)$ is congruent to $I$ modulo 2 (since $-I \equiv I \pmod{2}$ in $\text{Mat}_2(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})$ because $-1 \equiv 1 \pmod{2}$).

The subgroup $\Gamma_+(2) = \Gamma(2) / \{\pm I\}$ is the quotient of $\Gamma(2)$ by the central involution. Since $-I$ is central:
$$\Gamma(2) = \Gamma_+(2) \times \langle -I \rangle = \Gamma_+(2) \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z}$$

$\Gamma_+(2)$ acts freely on $H$ (the image of $-I$ is trivial in the quotient), and the quotient $H / \Gamma_+(2)$ is a genus-0 surface with punctures. Thus $\Gamma_+(2)$ is a free group on **2 generators**, e.g., $T^2$ and $ST^2S$ where $T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ and $S = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

## Part (b): Number of Free Generators of Gamma(N)

### Using Euler Characteristic

For $N \geq 3$, $\Gamma(N)$ is torsion-free, so $H / \Gamma(N) = K(\Gamma(N), 1)$. 

The Euler characteristic of $H / \Gamma(N)$ is:
$$\chi(\Gamma(N)) = \frac{\chi(H)}{\text{[index]}} = \frac{0}{[\text{index}]} \text{ (up to sign scaling)}$$

More precisely, using the hyperbolic area formula: the area of the fundamental domain is:
$$\text{Area}(H/\Gamma(N)) = -2\pi \chi(\Gamma(N))$$

By a multiplicativity property for congruence subgroups:
$$[\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) : \Gamma(N)] = N^3 \prod_{p | N} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right)$$

The Euler characteristic of $\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})$ (an orbifold with torsion) is $\chi(\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z})) = -1/12$.

For a free group on $r$ generators:
$$\chi(\text{free group on } r \text{ generators}) = 1 - r$$

Thus the number of generators is:
$$r(\Gamma(N)) = 1 - \chi(\Gamma(N))$$

By index scaling and the area formula:
$$r(\Gamma(N)) = 1 + \frac{N^3}{12} \prod_{p | N} \left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right) - 1$$

More directly, the **genus** of $H / \Gamma(N)$ (as a surface with punctures) gives the number of generators.

**Example: $N = 3$**

Index: $[\text{SL}_2(\mathbb{Z}) : \Gamma(3)] = 3^3 \cdot (1 - 1/9) = 27 \cdot 8/9 = 24$

Hyperbolic area of $H / \Gamma(3)$: $2\pi \cdot \text{(index)} / 12 = 2\pi \cdot 24 / 12 = 4\pi$

Genus of fundamental domain: $4\pi / 2\pi = 2$, giving **3 generators** (for a genus-0 surface with 3 punctures).

## Final Answer

$$\boxed{\text{(a) For } N \geq 3: \Gamma(N) \text{ is free (free action on } H \text{ gives fundamental group of a graph).}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{For } \Gamma(2): \Gamma(2) = \Gamma_+(2) \times \mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z} \text{ where } \Gamma_+(2) \text{ is free on 2 generators.}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{(b) Number of generators: } r_N = 1 + \frac{N^3}{12} \prod_{p|N}\left(1 - \frac{1}{p^2}\right).}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Example: } N = 3 \Rightarrow r_3 = 3 \text{ generators.}}$$
