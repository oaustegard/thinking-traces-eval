**Understanding the Problem**

We are given a potential energy function for a quantum mechanical system: $U(q) = \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$. This describes a cubic anharmonic oscillator, where the $q^2$ term is the harmonic oscillator part and the $q^3$ term is the anharmonic perturbation. We are asked to calculate two correlation functions: the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$, up to a certain order in the coupling constant $g$.

The expectation values are taken in the context of quantum field theory, usually in the ground state or a thermal state. For simplicity, and as is standard when calculating correlation functions perturbatively, we will assume we are working in the vacuum state of the free theory (before the anharmonic term is turned on).

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function is the expectation value of the field operator $q(t)$. In quantum field theory, correlation functions can be calculated using perturbation theory. The generating functional for correlation functions is given by:

$Z[J] = \int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S[q] + \int d^4x J(x)q(x)}$

where $S[q] = \int d^4x \left( \frac{1}{2} (\partial_\mu q)^2 + U(q) \right)$ is the action (assuming Euclidean spacetime for simplicity, though the problem statement doesn't explicitly specify, the notation $U(q)$ often implies this or a time-independent potential in a non-relativistic context. We'll proceed with a non-relativistic interpretation for $q(t)$).

The expectation value of $q(t)$ is given by:

$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z[0]} \frac{\delta Z[J]}{\delta J(t)} \bigg|_{J=0}$

We can expand the exponential of the interaction term in the generating functional:

$Z[J] = \int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-\int d t \left( \frac{1}{2} (\partial_t q)^2 + \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} \right)} e^{\int d t J(t)q(t)} e^{\int d t \frac{g q^3}{6}}$

Let $S_0 = \int d t \left( \frac{1}{2} (\partial_t q)^2 + \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} \right)$ be the action of the free harmonic oscillator. The partition function for the free theory is $Z_0 = \int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0}$.

The expectation value in the presence of the interaction can be written as:

$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S_0} e^{\int d t' J(t')q(t')} e^{\int d t' \frac{g (q(t'))^3}{6}}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0} e^{\int d t' J(t')q(t')} e^{\int d t' \frac{g (q(t'))^3}{6}}}$

For $J=0$, we have:

$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\langle q(t) e^{\int d t' \frac{g (q(t'))^3}{6}} \rangle_0}{\langle e^{\int d t' \frac{g (q(t'))^3}{6}} \rangle_0}$

where $\langle \dots \rangle_0$ denotes expectation values in the free theory.

Expanding the exponentials in powers of $g$:

$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\langle q(t) (1 + \frac{g}{6} \int d t' q^3(t') + \dots) \rangle_0}{\langle 1 + \frac{g}{6} \int d t' q^3(t') + \dots \rangle_0}$

The leading term with respect to $g$ will come from the lowest order term in the numerator that contributes. In the denominator, the leading term is 1.

So, $\langle q(t) \rangle \approx \langle q(t) (1 + \frac{g}{6} \int d t' q^3(t')) \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \frac{g}{6} \int d t' \langle q(t) q^3(t') \rangle_0$.

The 1-point function in the free theory, $\langle q(t) \rangle_0$, is zero due to symmetry ($q$ is an odd operator and the vacuum state is symmetric).

The next term is $\frac{g}{6} \int d t' \langle q(t) q^3(t') \rangle_0$. This is a 4-point function in the free theory. Using Wick's theorem, we can express this in terms of 2-point functions (propagators). The propagator for the free harmonic oscillator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0$.

$\langle q(t) q^3(t') \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$

Applying Wick's theorem:
$= \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$
$= 3 G_0(t, t') \langle q(t')^2 \rangle_0$

The term $\langle q(t')^2 \rangle_0$ is the variance of the field in the free theory, which is related to the propagator evaluated at the same time: $\langle q(t')^2 \rangle_0 = G_0(t', t')$.

So, the leading term of $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is:
$\frac{g}{6} \int d t' (3 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')) = \frac{g}{2} \int d t' (G_0(t, t'))^2$

This term is non-zero. However, let's re-examine the expansion. We are looking for the *leading term with respect to g*.

$\langle q(t) \rangle = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \frac{g}{6} \int d t' \langle q(t) q^3(t') \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$

Since $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$, the leading term in $g$ is indeed the term proportional to $g$.

The propagator for a free non-relativistic quantum mechanical harmonic oscillator with mass $m$ and frequency $\omega = m$ (from $U(q) = \frac{1}{2}m\omega^2 q^2$, so $\omega^2 = m^2$) is:
$G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m\omega} e^{-\omega |t_1 - t_2|}$
In our case, $\omega = m$. So, $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m |t_1 - t_2|}$.

Then, the leading term is:
$\frac{g}{2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d t' \left( \frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m |t - t'|} \right)^2 = \frac{g}{8m^4} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d t' e^{-2m |t - t'|}$

Let $u = t - t'$. Then $dt' = -du$.
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-2m |u|} du = 2 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-2mu} du = 2 \left[ \frac{e^{-2mu}}{-2m} \right]_0^{\infty} = 2 \left( 0 - \frac{1}{-2m} \right) = \frac{1}{m}$.

So, the leading term is $\frac{g}{8m^4} \cdot \frac{1}{m} = \frac{g}{8m^5}$.

This result is time-independent, which is expected for the vacuum expectation value of an odd operator in a theory with a symmetric potential.
