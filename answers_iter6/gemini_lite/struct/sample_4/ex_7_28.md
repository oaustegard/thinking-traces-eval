**Reasoning through the solution:**

The Euclidean action for the system is given by:
$S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$

The free action is $S_0[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 \right)$.
The free propagator, derived from the equation of motion $\ddot{q} - m^2 q = 0$ (for Euclidean time, this is $\ddot{q} + m^2 q = 0$), is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The interaction term is $V[q] = - \frac{g}{6} q^3$. The Feynman rule for the interaction vertex is $-g$ times the powers of $q$ in the vertex, so each $q^3$ vertex carries a weight of $-g$.

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) := \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function is given by $\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z} \int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]}$.
Using perturbation theory, $Z = \int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]} (1 + S_{\text{int}}[q] + \dots) = Z_0 (1 + \langle S_{\text{int}} \rangle_0 + \dots)$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z_0} \int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S_0[q]} e^{-S_{\text{int}}[q]} = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) S_{\text{int}} \rangle_0 + \dots$

The leading term in $g$ for $\langle q(t) \rangle$ comes from the first-order correction, which involves one interaction vertex.
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \langle q(t) \left( - \int dt' \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 \right) \rangle_0$

We need to evaluate $\langle q(t) q(t'_1) q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0$. Using Wick's theorem, this can be expanded into a sum of products of propagators.
The relevant contractions for $\langle q(t) q(t'_1) q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0$ are:
1.  $q(t) q(t'_1) \cdot q(t'_2) q(t'_3)$
2.  $q(t) q(t'_2) \cdot q(t'_1) q(t'_3)$
3.  $q(t) q(t'_3) \cdot q(t'_1) q(t'_2)$

Each of these contractions gives $G_0(t, t'_i) G_0(t'_j, t'_k)$. Since all free propagators are identical in this 1D case, these three terms are equal.
So, $\langle q(t) q(t'_1) q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t, t'_1) G_0(t'_2, t'_3) + \text{permutations}$.
For the specific contractions needed:
$\langle q(t) q(t'_1) q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0 = G_0(t, t'_1) G_0(t'_2, t'_3) + G_0(t, t'_2) G_0(t'_1, t'_3) + G_0(t, t'_3) G_0(t'_1, t'_2)$.
Since $G_0(t_i, t_j)$ depends only on $|t_i - t_j|$, and the vertex is at $t'$, all three terms are $G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$. This is incorrect.
The Wick contraction is $\langle q(t) q(t'_1) q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0 = G_0(t,t'_1)G_0(t'_2,t'_3) + G_0(t,t'_2)G_0(t'_1,t'_3) + G_0(t,t'_3)G_0(t'_1,t'_2)$.
Since $G_0(t_i, t_j)$ is symmetric in its arguments and depends only on the time difference, and the vertex is integrated over $t'$, we can write the integral as:
$\int dt' \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$.
This is not right. The expression is $\langle q(t) \cdot (-\int dt' \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3) \rangle_0$.
This expands to:
$-\frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$
Using Wick's theorem for $\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$:
$= G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') G_0(t', t') + G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') G_0(t', t') + G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') G_0(t', t')$ (This is wrong, it should be 3 terms of the form $G_0(a,b)G_0(c,d)$)

Let's draw the diagram for $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)}$. It's a single vertex $q^3$ connected to the external point $q(t)$.
This diagram is:
$q(t)$ --- Vertex($-g$) --- $q(t')^3$
The vertex is at $t'$. The interaction is $-g q(t')^3$.
The term contributing to $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)}$ is:
$\langle q(t) \left( - \int dt' \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 \right) \rangle_0$
$= - \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$

$\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0$ (This is also wrong, the structure is $q \cdot qqq$)

The diagram is a line from $t$ connected to a vertex at $t'$. The vertex has three lines originating from it. For $\langle q(t) \rangle$, we contract $q(t)$ with one of the lines from the vertex. The other two lines from the vertex must be contracted with each other.
So, the diagram is:
$q(t)$ --- $q(t')$ (contracted)
          |
          $q(t')$ --- $q(t')$ (contracted)

This means:
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = - \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \left( \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 \right)$
The term $\langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0$ represents the vacuum expectation value of $q^2$ at a point, which is a loop.
$\langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t', t') = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{1}{p^2 + m^2}$. This is a divergent integral if we consider it as a point-like propagator. However, in the context of correlation functions, $G_0(t', t')$ is not a standard object.

Let's re-evaluate the diagrammatic expansion.
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) S_{\text{int}} \rangle_0 + \dots$
$\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ because $G_0(t_1, t_2)$ is a symmetric function of $t_1-t_2$, and $q$ is a single field. The definition of the vacuum state implies $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

The first non-zero contribution to $\langle q(t) \rangle$ will come from terms involving the interaction.
The interaction term is $- \frac{g}{6} q^3$.
The first diagram for $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is a single vertex with one external leg.
This is $q(t) \times (-g \int dt' q(t')^3)$.
We need to evaluate $\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$.
Using Wick's theorem:
$\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t') G_0(t',t') + G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t') G_0(t',t') + G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t') G_0(t',t')$ (This is still wrong. The pairings are between the four $q$'s).
The four fields are $q_A, q_B, q_C, q_D$. The pairings are (A,B)(C,D), (A,C)(B,D), (A,D)(B,C).
Here, $A=q(t)$, $B=q(t')$, $C=q(t')$, $D=q(t')$.
So, $\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t') + G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t') + G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t')$.
This is $3 G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t')$. This is still not right.

Let's consider the vertex as a single point in spacetime $t'$. The interaction is $-g q(t')^3$.
The contribution to $\langle q(t) \rangle$ at order $g$ is:
$-g \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$
$= -g \int dt' [ G_0(t,t') G_0(t',t') G_0(t',t') + \text{other contractions}]$
The structure of the diagram is one external line connected to a vertex. The vertex has three lines. One line is connected to the external point. The other two lines must be connected to each other, forming a loop.
So, the diagram is:
$t \xrightarrow{G_0} t' \xrightarrow{G_0} t' \xrightarrow{G_0} t'$
The vertex has weight $-g$.
The diagram is: $q(t)$ connected to $q(t')$ via a propagator, and $q(t')$ connected to itself via two propagators.
This means $q(t)$ is contracted with one $q$ from the $q^3$ vertex, and the remaining two $q$'s from the vertex are contracted with each other.
The contribution is: $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
Here, $G_0(t', t') = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{1}{p^2 + m^2}$. This is a divergent integral.

However, the question asks for the "leading term ... with respect to $g$". This implies we should consider the lowest order in $g$ that gives a non-zero result.
The $q^3$ term is odd under parity $q \to -q$. The free theory is invariant under parity.
The 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ must be odd under parity if the theory is to be self-consistent in terms of symmetry.
If $q \to -q$, then $\langle q(t) \rangle \to \langle -q(t) \rangle = -\langle q(t) \rangle$.
The potential $U(q) = \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$ is not symmetric under $q \to -q$ due to the $q^3$ term.
However, the *vacuum state* of the full theory must be invariant under the symmetries of the action. The action is not invariant under $q \to -q$.

Let's consider the structure of the problem. We are asked for $\langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) (- \int dt' \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3) \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.
The first order term is $-g \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$.
The only way to contract $q(t)$ is with one of the $q(t')$. Let's say $q(t)$ with the first $q(t')$.
Then we have $G_0(t, t')$. The remaining part is $\langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$.
This is $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$.
The term $\langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t', t')$ is an integral over momentum:
$G_0(t', t') = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{1}{p^2 + m^2}$. This is a constant with respect to time $t'$.
This integral is divergent. This indicates that the 1-point function is not well-defined at this order if we simply plug in the free propagator.

In a theory with a cubic interaction, the vacuum expectation value of the field itself is generally non-zero. This is often handled by a redefinition of the field or by recognizing that the perturbation series is around a free theory, not necessarily the true vacuum.

However, if we consider the problem as a direct application of perturbation theory on the free vacuum, the term $\langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$ is a loop.
The diagram is $t \xrightarrow{G_0} t'$. The vertex at $t'$ connects to $q(t')$, and the remaining two $q(t')$ fields are contracted: $q(t') \leftrightarrow q(t')$.
So, the contribution is $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
$G_0(t', t') = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{1}{p^2 + m^2}$. Let's call this constant $C$.
So, the contribution is $-g C \int dt' G_0(t, t')$.
$\int dt' G_0(t, t') = \int dt' \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{1}{p^2 + m^2} e^{-|t-t'|p}$ (using 1D momentum $p$).
If we use the Euclidean time propagator $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$, then:
$G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m}$. This is a finite result. Let's assume this convention.
Then the contribution is $-g \frac{1}{m} \int dt' G_0(t, t')$.
$\int dt' G_0(t, t') = \int dt' \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t-t'|} = \frac{1}{m} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t-t'|} dt'$.
Let $u = t-t'$. $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|u|} du = 2 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-mu} du = 2 [- \frac{1}{m} e^{-mu}]_0^\infty = \frac{2}{m}$.
So, $\int dt' G_0(t, t') = \frac{1}{m} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{2}{m^2}$.
The leading term is $-g \frac{1}{m} \frac{2}{m^2} = -\frac{2g}{m^3}$.
This result is a constant and does not depend on $t$. This seems odd for a 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$.

Let's reconsider the structure of the interaction. The problem states $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$.
The definition of $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is the vacuum expectation value.
In a theory with $q^3$ interaction, the vacuum itself is shifted. However, if we are calculating in perturbation theory around the free vacuum, then $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.
The first order correction is indeed from the diagram: external leg $q(t)$ connected to a $q^3$ vertex, with the remaining two legs of the vertex contracted.
This diagram is $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
Using $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$:
$G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m}$.
So, $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') \frac{1}{m} = - \frac{g}{m} \int dt' G_0(t, t')$.
As calculated before, $\int dt' G_0(t, t') = \frac{2}{m^2}$.
So the term is $- \frac{g}{m} \frac{2}{m^2} = -\frac{2g}{m^3}$.

This result is a constant and does not depend on $t$. This implies that the interpretation of the question might be about the "effective vacuum expectation value" or the leading *correction* to the 1-point function, which is constant.

Let's check if there's any issue with the time dependence. The integral $\int dt'$ is over all time.
The propagator $G_0(t, t')$ decays exponentially with $|t-t'|$.
The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} G_0(t, t') dt'$ converges.

The result $-\frac{2g}{m^3}$ is the leading *correction* to the 1-point function. If the question implies $\langle q(t) \rangle$ in the interacting theory, and we are perturbing around the free vacuum, then this is the lowest order non-zero contribution.

Let's confirm the free propagator convention. If the kinetic term is $\frac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2$, then $\langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 = \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{-i\omega(t-t')}}{\omega^2+m^2}$.
In Euclidean time, $\langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 = \int \frac{dk}{2\pi} \frac{e^{-|k||t-t'|}}{k^2+m^2}$.
If we use the convention from Example 1: $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$, then $G_0(t',t') = \frac{1}{m}$. This is finite.
So, $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = -g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') = -g \int dt' G_0(t, t') \frac{1}{m} = -\frac{g}{m} \int dt' G_0(t, t') = -\frac{g}{m} \frac{2}{m^2} = -\frac{2g}{m^3}$.

**(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function is the sum of all 1-particle irreducible (1PI) diagrams with two external legs.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 + \text{connected diagrams}$.
The full 2-point function is $G(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$.
The connected 2-point function is $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G(t_1, t_2) - G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
Alternatively, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all 1PI diagrams with two external legs.

We need to calculate up to $O(g^2)$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Order $O(g^0)$:**
The connected 2-point function is just the free propagator:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Order $O(g^1)$:**
The 1PI diagrams with two external legs at order $g^1$ involve one vertex of $-g q^3$.
The diagram is:
$t_1 \xrightarrow{G_0} t' \xrightarrow{G_0} t_2$
         |
         $q(t')$ (connected to itself)
This is a $q^3$ vertex with one leg connected to $t_1$, one to $t_2$, and the third leg contracted with itself.
The contribution is: $-g \int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t', t_2) G_0(t', t')$.
We use $G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m}$.
So, the contribution is: $-g \frac{1}{m} \int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t', t_2)$.
The integral $\int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t', t_2)$ is the convolution of two propagators, which results in a propagator between $t_1$ and $t_2$ with a higher mass.
$\int dt' \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t'|} \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t'-t_2|} = \frac{1}{m^2} \int dt' e^{-m(|t_1-t'| + |t'-t_2|)}$.
The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1-t'| + |t'-t_2|)} dt'$ represents the sum of distances from $t'$ to $t_1$ and $t_2$. This integral is maximized when $t'$ is between $t_1$ and $t_2$.
Let $t_1 < t_2$. If $t' < t_1$, $|t_1-t'| + |t'-t_2| = t_1-t' + t_2-t'$.
If $t_1 \le t' \le t_2$, $|t_1-t'| + |t'-t_2| = t'-t_1 + t_2-t' = t_2-t_1$.
If $t' > t_2$, $|t_1-t'| + |t'-t_2| = t'-t_1 + t'-t_2$.

The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1-t'| + |t'-t_2|)} dt'$ is known to be $\frac{2}{m^2} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$ if $t_1$ and $t_2$ are far apart, but here it's the convolution of $e^{-m|t|}$ with itself.
The Fourier transform of $e^{-m|t|}$ is $\frac{2m}{k^2+m^2}$.
The Fourier transform of $G_0(t_1, t_2)$ is $\frac{1}{k^2+m^2}$.
The Fourier transform of the convolution $\int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t', t_2)$ is $(\frac{1}{k^2+m^2})^2$.
The inverse Fourier transform of $(\frac{1}{k^2+m^2})^2$ is $\frac{1}{2m} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
So, $\int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t', t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

The $O(g)$ contribution to $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = -g \frac{1}{m} \left( \frac{1}{2m} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} \right) = -\frac{g}{2m^2} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Order $O(g^2)$:**
The 1PI diagrams with two external legs at order $g^2$ involve two $q^3$ vertices.
There are two types of diagrams:
1.  **Two vertices connected by two propagators:**
    $t_1 \xrightarrow{G_0} t' \xrightarrow{G_0} t'' \xrightarrow{G_0} t_2$
    The two vertices have their third legs contracted with each other.
    Diagram: $t_1 \xrightarrow{G_0} t' \xrightarrow{G_0} t'' \xrightarrow{G_0} t_2$ with a loop at $t'$ and $t''$.
    This is: $t_1 \xrightarrow{G_0} t' \xrightarrow{G_0} t'' \xrightarrow{G_0} t_2$, and the third leg of $t'$ is contracted with the third leg of $t''$.
