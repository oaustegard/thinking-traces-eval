**Reasoning through the solution:**

The Euclidean action for the given potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$ is:
$S[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.

The free theory ($g=0$) has action $S_0[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2\right)$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$. (Note: The example uses $1/(2m)$, which suggests a different normalization or definition of the kinetic term. Let's stick to the standard $\frac{1}{2}\dot{q}^2$ leading to $\frac{1}{m}$ for now, and re-evaluate if needed. If the kinetic term was $\frac{1}{2m}\dot{q}^2$, the propagator would be $\frac{1}{m}e^{-m^2|t_1-t_2|}$ or similar. Assuming the standard $\frac{1}{2}\dot q^2$ and mass $m$, the propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.)

The interaction term is $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$. This gives a Feynman vertex with weight $-g$.

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function is given by $\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S[q]}}$.
Using perturbation theory, $\langle q(t) \rangle = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) \rangle_0 \left( \frac{-S_1[q]}{1!} \right)_0 + \dots$, where $S_1[q] = \int dt (-\frac{g}{6} q^3)$.

The leading order term in $g$ comes from the first-order correction:
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \langle q(t) (-S_1[q]) \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) \left( \int dt' \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 \right) \rangle_0$.

$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.

We use Wick's theorem to evaluate $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.
$\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$
$= \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0$
$= 3 \langle q(t)q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0$.

The propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The vacuum expectation value of a single field is zero: $\langle q(t') \rangle_0 = 0$.
So, $\langle q(t')q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t'-t'|} = \frac{1}{m}$.

Substituting this back:
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \left( 3 G_0(t, t') \cdot \frac{1}{m} \right)$
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2m} \int dt' G_0(t, t') = \frac{g}{2m} \int dt' \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t-t'|}$.

The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt' e^{-m|t-t'|}$ is $\frac{2}{m}$.
So, $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{m} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{g}{m^3}$.

This is the leading term of the 1-point function with respect to $g$.
Alternatively, thinking in terms of Feynman diagrams: The 1-point function corresponds to diagrams with no external legs. The free theory has no such diagrams. The interaction term is $q^3$. The simplest diagram contributing to a 1-point function involves a single $q^3$ vertex. This vertex needs to be "closed" by connecting its three legs. The only way to do this with propagators is to connect them to each other. This results in a triangle diagram. However, a $q^3$ vertex has 3 legs, and a propagator connects 2 legs. To form a closed loop from a single vertex, we need to connect the legs of the vertex. This can be done by connecting each leg to itself via a propagator, or by forming a loop of propagators.

Let's re-examine the structure of the diagram for $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)}$. It's a single $q^3$ vertex at time $t'$. We need to contract the three $q$ fields at $t'$.
$\langle q(t) q(t'_1) q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0$ where $t'_1=t'_2=t'_3 = t'$.
The diagram is a single vertex at time $t'$ with three lines emanating from it. These lines must be contracted.
There are two ways to contract three lines:
1. Connect two lines and the third to itself (e.g., $1 \leftrightarrow 2$, $3 \leftrightarrow 3$). This is not how Wick contractions work for a single vertex.
2. The structure is $\langle q(t) \cdot q(t') \cdot q(t') \cdot q(t') \rangle_0$.
   We contract $q(t)$ with one of the $q(t')$ fields. Let's say $q(t)$ with $q(t'_1)$.
   This gives $G_0(t, t') \langle q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0$.
   The remaining $\langle q(t'_2) q(t'_3) \rangle_0$ is $G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m}$.
   So, $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = G_0(t, t') \cdot G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t-t'|} \cdot \frac{1}{m} = \frac{1}{m^2} e^{-m|t-t'|}$.

The factor of 3 comes from choosing which of the three $q(t')$ fields to contract with $q(t)$.

So, $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \left( 3 \cdot G_0(t, t') \cdot G_0(t', t') \right)$
$= \frac{g}{2} \int dt' \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t-t'|} \cdot \frac{1}{m}$
$= \frac{g}{2m^2} \int dt' e^{-m|t-t'|}$
$= \frac{g}{2m^2} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{g}{m^3}$.

This matches the previous result. The leading term of the 1-point function is $\frac{g}{m^3}$.
