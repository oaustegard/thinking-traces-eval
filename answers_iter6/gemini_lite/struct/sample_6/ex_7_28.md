Part (a): Relevant Examples and Differences

The most relevant example for this problem is **Example 1: Problem: Calculate the 1-particle irreducible 2-point function $\Sigma(t_1,t_2)$ for a quantum particle with potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} + \tfrac{g q^4}{24}$**.

Here's why:
*   **Perturbative Expansion:** Both Example 1 and the main problem involve calculating correlation functions (1-point and 2-point functions) using perturbation theory in a coupling constant ($g$).
*   **Field Theory Framework:** Both problems are set within a quantum field theory context, using concepts like actions, propagators, Feynman rules, and Dyson-Schwinger equations (implicitly for the 2-point function).
*   **Potential Form:** Example 1 deals with a potential involving powers of $q$. The main problem also has a potential involving powers of $q$, specifically $q^2$ and $q^3$.

**Differences between Example 1 and the Main Problem:**

1.  **Potential Term:** Example 1 has a $q^4$ term, while the main problem has a $q^3$ term. This difference in the interaction term will lead to different Feynman rules and diagrams.
2.  **Order of Calculation:** Example 1 calculates the 1-particle irreducible (1PI) 2-point function $\Sigma$. The main problem asks for the 1-point function $G_1$ and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c$. While related, $\Sigma$ is a component of $G_2^c$ via Dyson's equation.
3.  **Specific Quantities:** Example 1 focuses on $\Sigma$. The main problem asks for $G_1$ and $G_2^c$ up to a certain order.
4.  **Symmetry:** The $q^4$ potential in Example 1 has a $Z_2$ symmetry ($q \to -q$). The $q^3$ potential in the main problem does not have this symmetry.

**Example 2 and Example 3 are not directly relevant.** Example 2 deals with Morse theory and local coordinate transformations, which is a purely mathematical topic and not directly applicable to quantum field theory calculations. Example 3 concerns exponential generating functions for combinatorial structures (trees), which is also a different mathematical framework.

Part (b): Reasoning and Solution

The main problem asks for two quantities: the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$, up to a certain order in $g$.

The Euclidean action is given by $S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.
The free propagator (from the quadratic part $\frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2$) is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The Feynman rule for the interaction term $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$ is a vertex with weight $-g$.

**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is calculated by summing all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg. The free theory ($\text{g}=0$) has $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ because the potential is symmetric around $q=0$ and the action is quadratic.

To find the leading term in $g$, we need diagrams with one external leg and at least one $g$ vertex.
*   **Order $g^0$:** No diagrams with one external leg and zero $g$ vertices. The contribution is 0.
*   **Order $g^1$:** The simplest diagram is a single vertex of strength $-g$ connected to the external leg. This means we have the term $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$. To get $\langle q(t) \rangle$, we contract one of the $q$'s in $q^3$ with the external leg. This results in a diagram corresponding to the term $-\frac{g}{6} \langle q(t) q(t) q(t) \rangle_0$. Using Wick's theorem, this becomes $-\frac{g}{6} \cdot 3 \cdot G_0(t, t) \cdot G_0(t, t)$, where the factor of 3 comes from choosing which $q$ from $q^3$ contracts with the external $q(t)$. However, this is not how we calculate the 1-point function directly.

A more direct way using the generating functional $Z[J] = \int \mathcal{D}q e^{-S[q] + \int J(t)q(t)dt}$ is:
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{1}{Z[0]} \frac{\delta Z[J]}{\delta J(t)} \bigg|_{J=0}$.
$\frac{\delta S}{\delta q} = \dot{q} - \frac{m^2}{2} q + \frac{g}{6} 3q^2 = \dot{q} - \frac{m^2}{2} q + \frac{g}{2} q^2$.
The equation of motion for the expectation value is $\langle \frac{\delta S}{\delta q} \rangle = \langle J \rangle$.
At $J=0$: $\langle \dot{q} - \frac{m^2}{2} q + \frac{g}{2} q^2 \rangle = 0$.
$\frac{d}{dt} \langle q(t) \rangle - \frac{m^2}{2} \langle q(t) \rangle + \frac{g}{2} \langle q(t)^2 \rangle = 0$.

We need $\langle q(t) \rangle$ to order $g$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + g \langle q(t) \rangle_1 + O(g^2)$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

To calculate $\langle q(t) \rangle_1$, we consider diagrams with one external leg and one $g$ vertex.
The term $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$ contributes to the generating functional.
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int q(t) e^{-S[q]} \mathcal{D}q}{\int e^{-S[q]} \mathcal{D}q}$.
Expanding $e^{-S[q]} = e^{-S_0[q] + \int \frac{g}{6} q^3 dt} \approx e^{-S_0[q]} (1 + \int \frac{g}{6} q'^3 dt')$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle \approx \frac{\int q(t) e^{-S_0[q]} (1 + \int \frac{g}{6} q'^3 dt') \mathcal{D}q}{\int e^{-S_0[q]} (1 + \int \frac{g}{6} q'^3 dt') \mathcal{D}q}$.

$\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

Using Wick's theorem for $\langle q(t) q'(t_1) q'(t_2) q'(t_3) \rangle_0$:
This contracts into terms like $G_0(t, t_1)G_0(t_2, t_3) + G_0(t, t_2)G_0(t_1, t_3) + G_0(t, t_3)G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
Each of these terms is integrated over $dt_1, dt_2, dt_3$.
Consider the first term: $G_0(t, t_1) \int G_0(t_2, t_3) dt_2 dt_3$.
The integral $\int G_0(t_2, t_3) dt_2 dt_3$ is time-independent and depends on the propagator.
$\int G_0(t, t') dt' = \int \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t-t'|} dt'$. Let's assume we are working with regulated propagators or that such integrals are well-defined and lead to finite results. In Euclidean space, $\int G_0(t) dt$ is a constant. For $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$, $\int G_0(t_1, t_2) dt_2 = \frac{1}{m} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} dt_2$.     This result $\frac{g}{m^3}$ is consistent. The discrepancy with the diagrammatic calculation likely arises from how the $q^3$ vertex is handled. In the diagrammatic calculation, the vertex is often associated with a factor of $-g$. The action term is $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$.

    Let's use the convention that each $q^3$ vertex contributes $-g$.
    The diagram for $\langle q(t) \rangle$ at order $g$ is a vertex at $t'$ connected to $t$, with two lines forming a loop at $t'$.
    Value: $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') \times (\text{combinatorics})$.
    The vertex comes from the $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$ term.
    The diagram is formed by taking the term $-g \int q(t')^3 dt'$ and contracting one $q(t')$ with the external $q(t)$, and contracting the other two $q(t')$ with each other.
    So, $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') \times (\text{symmetry factor})$.
    The symmetry factor for a vertex with two internal lines connecting to itself is 1 (the two internal lines are indistinguishable).
    $G_0(t', t') = 1/m$.
    So, $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') (1/m)^2 = -\frac{g}{m^2} \int dt' G_0(t, t') = -\frac{g}{m^2} \frac{2}{m^2} = -\frac{2g}{m^4}$.

    The issue is likely in the mapping from the action term to the diagram value.
    If the vertex weight is $-g$, then the term is $-g \int q^3$.
    If the action is $S = S_0 - \int V(q) dt$, where $V(q) = \frac{g}{6} q^3$.
    Then the vertex weight is $-g$.
    The diagram is $-g \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') = -g \frac{1}{m^2} \frac{2}{m^2} = -\frac{2g}{m^4}$.

    Let's trust the equation of motion result for now, as it's more robust.
    $\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{g}{m^3}$ to order $g$.

**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$.**

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
The interaction is $-\frac{g}{6} q^3$. The vertex weight is $-g$.

*   **Order $g^0$**: The tree-level 2-point function is the free propagator.

*   **Order $g^1$**: Diagrams with one vertex and two external legs.
    The vertex has 3 legs. We connect two legs to the external points $t_1$ and $t_2$. The third leg must be contracted with itself, forming a loop at the vertex time $t'$.

    Value: $-g \int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t_2, t') G_0(t', t')$.
    The factor $G_0(t', t')$ comes from contracting the third leg of the $q^3$ vertex with itself at $t'$.
    The integral is over the vertex time $t'$.
    $G_0(t', t') = 1/m$.
    So, $-g \int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t_2, t') (1/m) = -\frac{g}{m} \int dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t_2, t')$.

    Let's evaluate the integral: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt' G_0(t_1, t') G_0(t_2, t')$.
    Assume $t_1 < t_2$.
    $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt' \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t'|} \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_2-t'|}$.
    $= \frac{1}{m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(t'-t_1)} e^{-m(t_2-t')} dt'$ for $t_1 < t' < t_2$.
    $= \frac{1}{m^2} \int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{-mt' + mt_1 - mt_2 + mt'} dt' = \frac{1}{m^2} \int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{m(t_1-t_2)} dt'$.
    $= \frac{1}{m^2} e^{-m(t_2-t_1)} [t']_{t_1}^{t_2} = \frac{1}{m^2} e^{-m(t_2-t_1)} (t_2 - t_1)$.
    This is for $t_1 < t_2$. In general, it's $|t_1-t_2|$.
    So, the integral is $\frac{1}{m^2} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

    The contribution at order $g$ is $-\frac{g}{m} \cdot \frac{1}{m^2} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = -\frac{g}{m^3} |t_1-t_2| e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

    This term is not proportional to $\delta(t_1-t_2)$ as in Example 1. This reflects the different interaction.
