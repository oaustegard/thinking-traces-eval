**Main Problem Setup:**
The Euclidean action for the potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$ is:
$S[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.

The Feynman rules are:
*   Propagator: $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$ (assuming mass $m$ is the coefficient of $q^2/2$ in the action, and we are in 1D Euclidean time). If we consider the coefficient of $\dot q^2/2$ to be $1/2$, then the propagator is $\frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$. Let's assume the standard kinetic term $\frac{1}{2}\dot q^2$.
*   Vertex: Each $q^3$ vertex carries a weight of $-g$.

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function is given by:
$G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S[q]}}$.

To calculate this perturbatively in $g$, we expand $e^{-S[q]}$ around the free theory ($g=0$).
$e^{-S[q]} = e^{-\int (\frac{1}{2}\dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2)} e^{\int \frac{g}{6} q^3} = e^{-S_0[q]} \left(1 + \int \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 dt' + O(g^2)\right)$.

Then,
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S_0[q]} \left(1 + \int \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 dt' + \dots\right)}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]} \left(1 + \int \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 dt' + \dots\right)}$.

The denominator is $Z = \int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]} (1 + \dots) = Z_0 (1 + \langle \int \frac{g}{6} q^3 \rangle_0 + \dots)$, where $Z_0$ is the partition function of the free theory and $\langle \dots \rangle_0$ denotes expectation in the free theory.
The numerator is $\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S_0[q]} (1 + \dots) = \int \mathcal{D}q \, (q(t) + q(t)\int \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 dt' + \dots) e^{-S_0[q]}$.
$= Z_0 \left(\langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) \int \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 dt' \rangle_0 + \dots \right)$.

In the free theory (Gaussian integral), $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ because the action $S_0$ is quadratic and symmetric around $q=0$.
So, $\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{Z_0 (\langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) \int \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 dt' \rangle_0 + \dots)}{Z_0 (1 + \langle \int \frac{g}{6} q^3 \rangle_0 + \dots)}$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{0 + \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 + \dots}{1 + 0 + \dots}$.

The leading term in $g$ comes from $\frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.
Using Wick's theorem for the free theory:
$\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) [q(t') q(t') q(t')] \rangle_0$
$= \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$
$= 3 \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 \langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$.

Here, $\langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t, t') = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t-t'|}$.
And $\langle q(t') q(t') \rangle_0 = G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t'-t'|} = \frac{1}{m}$.

So, $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t, t') \cdot \frac{1}{m} = \frac{3}{m^2} e^{-m|t-t'|}$.

The leading term of $G_1(t)$ is:
$G_1(t) \approx \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \left(3 \cdot \frac{1}{m^2} e^{-m|t-t'|}\right) = \frac{g}{2m^2} \int dt' e^{-m|t-t'|}$.

The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t-t'|} dt'$ is $\frac{2}{m}$.
So, $G_1(t) \approx \frac{g}{2m^2} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{g}{m^3}$.

This is the leading term, order $g^1$.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$ (up to $O(g^2)$).**

The connected 2-point function is given by the amputated diagrams.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$.
In terms of the full propagator $G(t_1, t_2)$, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G(t_1, t_2) - G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
Using Dyson's equation for the full propagator: $G = (G_0^{-1} - \Sigma)^{-1}$.
$G_0^{-1}$ is the operator corresponding to the quadratic part of the action. For $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$, $G_0^{-1}(t_1, t_2) = (-\partial_{t_1}^2 + m^2) \delta(t_1 - t_2)$.
So, $G(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) + \dots$
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) + \dots$
The connected 2-point function is obtained by summing all 1PI diagrams with two external legs, and then connecting them with free propagators. In essence, $G_2^c$ is the sum of all connected diagrams with two external legs.
