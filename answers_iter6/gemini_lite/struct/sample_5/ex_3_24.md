**Main Problem Solution:**

The problem has two parts:
1.  Compute the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for $S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})$.
2.  Compute the number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only.

**Part 1: 1-Loop Contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

The partition function is $Z = \int dx \, e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$. The free partition function is $Z_0 = \int dx \, e^{-S_0(x)/\hbar}$, where $S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$.
The term $\log(Z/Z_0)$ can be computed using the expansion:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \log \left\langle e^{-(S(x)-S_0(x))/\hbar} \right\rangle_0$, where $\langle \cdot \rangle_0$ denotes the average with respect to the free theory.
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \log \left\langle e^{-(-g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6}))/\hbar} \right\rangle_0 = \log \left\langle e^{\frac{g}{\hbar}(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})} \right\rangle_0$.

Expanding the exponential and taking the average:
$e^{\frac{g}{\hbar}(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})} = 1 + \frac{g}{\hbar}(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6}) + \frac{1}{2!} \left(\frac{g}{\hbar}\right)^2 (x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})^2 + \dots$

The 1-loop contribution corresponds to the term linear in $g$.
$\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = \left\langle \frac{g}{\hbar}(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6}) \right\rangle_0$.

The free theory is governed by $S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$. The partition function for the free theory is $Z_0 = \int dx \, e^{-x^2/(2\hbar)}$. This is a Gaussian integral.
The averages in the free theory are:
$\langle x^n \rangle_0 = \frac{\int x^n e^{-x^2/(2\hbar)} dx}{\int e^{-x^2/(2\hbar)} dx}$.
For $n=1$, $\langle x \rangle_0 = 0$ due to symmetry.
For $n=2$, $\langle x^2 \rangle_0 = \hbar$. This is the variance of the Gaussian.
For $n=3$, $\langle x^3 \rangle_0 = 0$ due to symmetry.

So, the 1-loop contribution is:
$\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{\hbar} \left( \langle x^2 \rangle_0 + \frac{1}{6} \langle x^3 \rangle_0 \right)$
$\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{\hbar} (\hbar + \frac{1}{6} \cdot 0) = g$.

This result can also be seen from the perspective of the effective action. The 1-loop contribution to the effective action $\Gamma^{(1)}(\phi)$ is given by $\frac{1}{2} \text{Tr} \log(S_0''(\phi))$, where $S_0''(x)$ is the second derivative of the free action with respect to $x$.
$S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$, so $S_0'(x) = x$ and $S_0''(x) = 1$.
If we consider $x$ as a constant field $\phi$, then $\Gamma^{(1)}(\phi) = \frac{1}{2} \log(1)$. This is not directly applicable here as the problem is in 1D and $x$ is a field.

Let's use the interpretation of $g$ as a coupling constant and $\hbar=1$. The action is $S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g x^2 - g \frac{x^3}{6}$.
The free action is $S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$. The interaction term is $V(x) = - g x^2 - g \frac{x^3}{6}$.
The 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is given by $\frac{1}{2} \langle V(x) V(x) \rangle_0 - \langle V(x) \rangle_0$.
However, the problem asks for $\log(Z/Z_0)$, which corresponds to the 1-loop contribution to the effective action.
The 1-loop contribution to the effective action is given by $\frac{1}{2} \text{Tr} \log(G_0 K)$, where $G_0$ is the free propagator and $K$ is the kernel of the interaction.
Alternatively, using the generating functional approach for 1-loop correction to the effective action:
$\Gamma^{(1)} = \frac{1}{2} \text{Tr} \log(S_0''(x))$.
Here, $S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$, so $S_0''(x) = 1$.
The "Tr" here should be interpreted as an integral over the field configuration, which in this 1D case is just multiplication by the "length" of the system, or if we consider $x$ as a constant field, it is simply the second derivative of the action with respect to $x$.
The action is $S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})$.
The free part is $S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$. The "interaction" part is $-g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})$.
The 1-loop contribution to the effective action is given by $\frac{1}{2}\log(\det(G_0^{-1} - \Sigma^{(1)})) \approx \frac{1}{2}\log(\det(G_0^{-1})) + \frac{1}{2}\text{Tr}(G_0 \Sigma^{(1)})$. This is not the right path.

Let's reconsider the expansion:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \log \left\langle e^{-(-g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6}))} \right\rangle_0$ (setting $\hbar=1$).
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \log \left\langle 1 + g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6}) + \frac{1}{2} g^2 (x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})^2 + \dots \right\rangle_0$.
The 1-loop contribution is the term linear in $g$:
$\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = g \left\langle x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6} \right\rangle_0$.
For the free theory $S_0(x) = \frac{x^2}{2}$, the average is taken with respect to $e^{-x^2/2}$.
$\langle x^2 \rangle_0 = \frac{\int x^2 e^{-x^2/2} dx}{\int e^{-x^2/2} dx} = 1$.
$\langle x^3 \rangle_0 = \frac{\int x^3 e^{-x^2/2} dx}{\int e^{-x^2/2} dx} = 0$.
So, $\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = g (1 + \frac{1}{6} \cdot 0) = g$.
