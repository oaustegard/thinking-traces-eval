## Solution to the Main Problem

**Problem Statement:** Let $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$ (Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator).
(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) := \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.
(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ (i.e., up to and including order $g^2$).

**Setup:**
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left( \tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The interaction term is $-S_{\text{int}} = \tfrac{g}{6} q^3$. This corresponds to a 3-valent vertex with weight $-g$ (after absorbing $1/3!$ and $\hbar$).

**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ is the vacuum expectation value of the field at time $t$. Using the Feynman expansion, this is given by the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg.

*   **Order $g^0$ (Free theory):** In the free theory ($g=0$), the action is quadratic, and the expectation value of $q(t)$ is zero due to the symmetry of the potential around $q=0$. $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

*   **Order $g^1$:** The diagrams contributing to $\langle q(t) \rangle$ at order $g$ involve one cubic vertex. A cubic vertex has 3 half-edges. For a 1-point function, one half-edge is external (connected to $q(t)$), and the other two must contract with each other. This forms a "tadpole" diagram.
    The diagram is: $q(t)$ connected to a vertex, and the other two legs of the vertex contract via free propagators.
    $$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \int dt_v \left( -g \right) \cdot \frac{1}{3!} \cdot \langle q(t) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$$
    The factor $1/3!$ comes from the action. The vertex has 3 legs, one connected to $q(t)$, and two contracting. The number of ways to contract two legs out of three is $\binom{3}{2}=3$. The symmetry factor of the cubic vertex in the action is $3!$. So the weight is $-g/3!$.
    Using Wick's theorem:
    $\langle q(t) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 + \text{permutations}$
    The only distinct term after contraction is:
    $$ \langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 = G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    The symmetry factor for this diagram (tadpole) is 2 (the two internal propagators connected to the vertex have a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry). The vertex weight is $-g$.
    $$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \int dt_v \left( \frac{-g}{2} \right) G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$$
    where the factor $-g/2$ comes from $(-g/3!) \times 3 \times 2 = -g$. No, the vertex weight is $-g$. The number of ways to contract 2 legs from 3 is $\binom{3}{2}=3$. The symmetry factor of the graph is 2. So the contribution is $(-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    Let's re-evaluate the vertex weight and symmetry factor carefully. The action is $\frac{g}{6}q^3$. The Feynman rule for a vertex is $-g$ times the symmetry factor of the diagram.
    The diagram is: $q(t)$ connected to a vertex. The other two legs of the vertex contract.
    $$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \int dt_v (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{\text{Sym}} \cdot \langle q(t) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0^{\text{connected part}} $$
    The connected part is $\langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$.
    The vertex has 3 legs. One leg is connected to the external point $t$. The other two legs contract.
    The number of ways to choose 2 legs to contract out of the 3 available legs at the vertex is $\binom{3}{2} = 3$. The symmetry factor for this tadpole diagram (where the loop is attached to a single vertex) is 2.
    So, the contribution is:
    $$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \int dt_v (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    We know $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
    $$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) \frac{1}{2m} = -\frac{g}{4m} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|} $$
    The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v e^{-m|t - t_v|} = \frac{2}{m}$.
    $$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{4m} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = -\frac{g}{4m^2} $$

    This is a constant shift, independent of $t$. This is expected for a 1-point function at this order in a theory with a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry (if it existed). However, the cubic potential breaks $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry.

    The leading term of $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$ is the $O(g)$ contribution.

**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$.**

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c = G_2(t_1, t_2) - G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
We need to calculate the connected diagrams contributing to $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$. These are diagrams with two external legs.

*   **Order $g^0$:** The connected 2-point function is simply the free propagator.
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} $$

*   **Order $g^1$:** Diagrams with one cubic vertex and two external legs.
    This is the "tadpole" diagram attached to two external legs.
    The diagram is: $q(t_1)$ connected to a vertex, $q(t_2)$ connected to the same vertex, and the third leg of the vertex contracts with itself.
    $$ \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = \int dt_v (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    Here, $-g$ is the vertex weight, $1/2$ is the symmetry factor of the tadpole. The $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ is the contracted loop.
    $$ \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{2} G_0(t_v, t_v) \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) $$
    We know $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$ is the convolution of two propagators, which is related to the propagator of a particle with mass $2m$.
    $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_v|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v - t_2|} = \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right)^2 \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_v - t_2|)} $$
    The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_v - t_2|)} = \frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
    So, $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right)^2 \frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} = \frac{1}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
    This is not correct. The convolution of two $G_0$ propagators is:
    $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \int dt_v \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_v|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v-t_2|} = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int dt_v e^{-m(|t_1-t_v| + |t_v-t_2|)}$.
    For $t_1 < t_2$: $\int_{-\infty}^{t_1} dt_v e^{-m(t_1-t_v + t_2-t_v)} + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} dt_v e^{-m(t_v-t_1 + t_2-t_v)} + \int_{t_2}^{\infty} dt_v e^{-m(t_v-t_1 + t_v-t_2)}$.
    This integral evaluates to $\frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
    So, $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \frac{1}{4m^2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{1}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$. Still seems wrong.

    Let's use the integral property: $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2)$. (This is a standard result for the convolution of $e^{-m|t|}$.)
    So, $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{2} \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{8m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
    The connected part is $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{8m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.

    This term contributes to the mass renormalization. This is part of the self-energy $\Sigma^{(1)}$.

*   **Order $g^2$:** Diagrams with two cubic vertices and two external legs.
    There are two types of 1PI diagrams contributing to the self-energy $\Sigma^{(2)}$:
    1.  **"Sunset" diagram:** Two vertices connected by three internal propagators. This is 1PI.
        The diagram is: $q(t_1)$ to vertex 1, $q(t_2)$ to vertex 2. Three propagators connect vertex 1 and vertex 2.
        $$ \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = (-g)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} \int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) [G_0(t_v, t_{v'})]^3 G_0(t_{v'}, t_2) $$
        Here $-g$ is the vertex weight. $1/6$ is the symmetry factor for the sunset diagram (from the 3! ways to pair up the internal lines if the vertices were identical, but here vertices are distinct by external legs, so it's $1/3!$).
        $$ \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{6} \int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_2) $$
        This integral corresponds to the self-energy calculated in Trace 1 for a quartic oscillator, but with a cubic vertex.
        The structure is: $G_0 \star (G_0)^3 \star G_0$.
        This integral is known to be $\frac{1}{2m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$ for the convolution of three propagators.
        The integral part: $\int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'})^3 G_0(t_{v'}, t_2)$.
        This is the Fourier transform of $p \mapsto \frac{1}{p^2+m^2} (\frac{1}{p^2+m^2})^3 \frac{1}{p^2+m^2} = \frac{1}{(p^2+m^2)^5}$.
        The corresponding time-domain function is proportional to $|t_1-t_2|^2 e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$. This is a bit complicated.

        Let's use the notation from Trace 1: $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        The integral is $\int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_2)$.
        This is the convolution of 5 propagators. The convolution of $n$ propagators $G_0$ gives a function proportional to $|t_1-t_2|^{n-1} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$. So for 5 propagators, it's $|t_1-t_2|^4 e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_2)$ is $\frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
        The integral $\int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_2)$ is $\frac{1}{2m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$ ? No.
        The convolution of $n$ propagators $G_0$ is $\int dt_1 \dots dt_{n-1} G_0(t_0, t_1) \dots G_0(t_{n-1}, t_n) = \frac{1}{(2m)^{n-1} m^{n-1}} G_0(t_0, t_n)$ ? No.
        The convolution of $n$ propagators is $\frac{1}{(2m)^n} \int dk e^{ik(t_1-t_n)} (\frac{1}{k^2+m^2})^n$.
        For $n=2$: $\frac{1}{(2m)^2} \int dk e^{ik(t_1-t_2)} (\frac{1}{k^2+m^2})^2 = \frac{1}{4m^2} \frac{1}{2m^2} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{1}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        So $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.

        The convolution of 3 propagators is $\int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
        The convolution of 4 propagators is $\int dt_v dt_{v'} dt_{v''} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_{v''}) G_0(t_{v''}, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m^4} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
        The convolution of 5 propagators is $\int dt_v dt_{v'} dt_{v''} dt_{v'''} G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_{v''}) G_0(t_{v''}, t_{v'''}) G_0(t_{v'''}, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m^5} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.

        So, $\int dt_v dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_v) [G_0(t_v, t_{v'})]^3 G_0(t_{v'}, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m^5} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
        $$ \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{2m^5} G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{12m^5} G_0(t_1, t_2) $$
        This contribution is to the full propagator $G_2$. The connected part $G_2^c$ is obtained by summing 1PI diagrams. So this sunset diagram is indeed a contribution to $G_2^c$.

    2.  **"Double tadpole" diagram:** This diagram consists of two separate tadpole diagrams, each attached to one of the external legs. This diagram is *reducible* because cutting the connection between the two tadpoles (i.e., cutting the line connecting the external legs to the vertices) disconnects the diagram. Reducible diagrams are not 1PI. They are already accounted for by the Dyson equation. Specifically, $G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$. The diagrammatic expansion of this leads to diagrams that are sums of 1PI components.

    The only 1PI diagram for $\Sigma^{(2)}$ is the sunset diagram.

    So, the contribution to $G_2^c$ from order $g^2$ is $\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2)$.

    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \frac{g^2}{12m^5} G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{12m^5} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{g^2}{24m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} $$

Let's re-check the $\Sigma^{(1)}$ calculation.
$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
$\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
$G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2) \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = -\frac{g}{8m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
This is the self-energy contribution at $g^1$.

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected diagrams with two amputated external legs. This is precisely the sum of 1PI diagrams $\Sigma$.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) + \Sigma^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) + \dots$
where $\Sigma^{(k)}$ are the $k$-loop contributions to the self-energy.

So, the connected 2-point function up to $g^2$ is:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) + \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) + \dots$

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{8m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{16m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{12m^5} G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{24m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

Total $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) + \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2)$
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - \frac{g}{16m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{24m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3)$.

Let's re-check the $\Sigma^{(1)}$ calculation again.
The diagram is a tadpole attached to two external legs.
$$ \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = \int dt_v (-g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
This is correct.
$G_0(t_v, t_v) = 1/(2m)$.
$\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
So, $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = -g \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m^2} G_0(t_1, t_2) \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = -\frac{g}{8m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
This is the contribution to the full propagator $G_2$.
The connected 2-point function $G_2^c$ is $G_2 - G_0$.
So $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{8m^3} G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
$$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{8m^3} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = -\frac{g}{16m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} $$
This seems correct.

Now for $\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}$ contribution to $G_2^c$.
The self-energy $\Sigma$ is the sum of 1PI diagrams.
$G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$
The contribution from $\Sigma^{(1)}$ to $G_2^c$ is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$.
The contribution from $\Sigma^{(2)}$ to $G_2^c$ is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0$.

Let's re-read the definition. $G_2 = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$.
$G_2^c = G_2 - G_0 = G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$
So $G_2^c$ is the sum of all connected diagrams with two *amputated* external legs. This is precisely the sum of the 1PI diagrams $\Sigma$.
So the previous calculation of $\Sigma^{(1)}$ and $\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}$ as direct contributions to $G_2^c$ was correct.
