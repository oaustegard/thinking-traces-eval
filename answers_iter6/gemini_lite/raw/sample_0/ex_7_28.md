## Solution to the Main Problem

**Problem Statement:** Calculate the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ and the connected 2-point function $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ for the Euclidean action $S[q] = \int dt \bigl(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}\bigr)$.

**Setup:**
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \bigl(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\bigr)$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\tfrac{g}{6} \int dt \, q(t)^3$.
The Feynman rule for the cubic vertex is $-g$ (from $-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar$ where $\hbar=1$, the $1/3!$ in the action cancels the $3!$ legs from vertex expansion, leaving a factor of $-g$).

**Part (i): 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ modulo $g$.**

The 1-point function is $\langle q(t) \rangle = \int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]}$.
Using perturbation theory, we expand $e^{-S[q]} = e^{-S_0[q] - S_{\text{int}}[q]} \approx e^{-S_0[q]} (1 - S_{\text{int}}[q])$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle \approx \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) (-S_{\text{int}}[q]) \rangle_0$.
The free 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ because the free theory is symmetric under $q \to -q$.

The leading order correction comes from the term $\langle q(t) (-S_{\text{int}}[q]) \rangle_0$:
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \langle q(t) \cdot \tfrac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 $$
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \tfrac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 $$
We use Wick's theorem. $\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$.
The contractions are:
1.  $q(t)$ contracts with one $q(t_v)$: $G_0(t, t_v) \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$.
2.  The remaining two $q(t_v)$ contract with each other: $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.

So, $\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = G_0(t, t_v) \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 + \text{permutations}$.
There are 3 ways to contract $q(t)$ with one of the three $q(t_v)$ terms. Each gives the same result due to symmetry.
$$ \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = 3 \cdot G_0(t, t_v) \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 $$
The expectation value of $q(t_v)^2$ in the free theory is $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
$$ \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 = G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} $$
So,
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \tfrac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, 3 \cdot G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \tfrac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
Substitute $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$:
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \tfrac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right) $$
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \tfrac{g}{2} \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right)^2 \int dt_v \, e^{-m|t - t_v|} $$
The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t - t_v|} dt_v$ is a standard integral. Let $u = t - t_v$, so $du = -dt_v$.
$$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|u|} du = 2 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-mu} du = 2 \left[-\frac{1}{m} e^{-mu}\right]_0^{\infty} = 2 \left(0 - (-\frac{1}{m})\right) = \frac{2}{m} $$
So,
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \tfrac{g}{2} \left(\frac{1}{4m^2}\right) \left(\frac{2}{m}\right) = \tfrac{g}{2} \frac{1}{2m^3} = \frac{g}{4m^3} $$
This result is constant in time, as expected for a 1-point function in time-translationally invariant theories when considering $q(t)$ itself.

The 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ to leading order in $g$ is the constant $\frac{g}{4m^3}$.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is related to the full propagator $G(t_1, t_2)$ by $G_2^c = G - G_0$.
Using Dyson's equation: $G = (G_0^{-1} - \Sigma)^{-1}$, so $G_2^c = (G_0^{-1} - \Sigma)^{-1} - G_0$.
$G_2^c = G_0 (I - G_0 \Sigma)^{-1} - G_0 = G_0 [(I - G_0 \Sigma)^{-1} - I]$.
Using $(I-A)^{-1} = I + A + A^2 + \dots$:
$G_2^c = G_0 [ (I + G_0 \Sigma + (G_0 \Sigma)^2 + \dots) - I ] = G_0 \Sigma + G_0 \Sigma G_0 \Sigma + \dots$
Thus, $G_2^c$ is obtained by summing all connected diagrams for $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ that are not just the free propagator $G_0$. Diagrammatically, these are diagrams with two external legs that cannot be disconnected by cutting a single internal line.

The contributions to $G_2^c$ up to order $g^2$ are:
1.  **Order $g^0$:** This is the free propagator $G_0(t_1, t_2)$. It is not part of $G_2^c$, as $G_2^c = G - G_0$.
2.  **Order $g^1$:** The 1PI diagram contributing to $\Sigma$ is the "tadpole". $\Sigma^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{2} G_0(t_v, t_v) \delta(t_1-t_v) \delta(t_2-t_v)$ integrated over $t_v$. This gives $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_1-t_2)$.
    The contribution to $G_2^c$ from $\Sigma^{(1)}$ is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$.
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) $$
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \left(-\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_a - t_b)\right) G_0(t_b, t_2) $$
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = -\frac{g}{4m} \int dt_a \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) $$
    This integral is the convolution of $G_0$ with itself.
    $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) dt_a = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_a|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_a - t_2|} dt_a $$
    $$ = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1 - t_a| + |t_a - t_2|)} dt_a $$
    The term $|t_1 - t_a| + |t_a - t_2|$ is minimized when $t_a$ is between $t_1$ and $t_2$, in which case it equals $|t_1 - t_2|$. For $t_a$ outside this interval, it's larger. The integral $\int e^{-m|x-y|} e^{-m|y-z|} dy$ is proportional to $e^{-m|x-z|}$. Specifically, it evaluates to $\frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
    $$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) dt_a = \frac{1}{4m^2} \left(\frac{1}{m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}\right) = \frac{1}{4m^3} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} $$
    So,
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = -\frac{g}{4m} \left(\frac{1}{4m^3} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}\right) = -\frac{g}{16m^4} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} $$
    This is the leading contribution to the connected 2-point function.

3.  **Order $g^2$:** The 1PI diagrams contributing to $\Sigma$ at order $g^2$ are:
    *   **Sunset diagram:** Two $q^3$ vertices connected by three $G_0$ propagators.
    *   **Double tadpole diagram:** This is reducible and does not contribute to $\Sigma$. It is already captured by $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$ terms in $G_2$.

    Let's calculate the sunset diagram for $\Sigma^{(2)}$.
    Each vertex is $q^3$. The diagram has two vertices, say at $t_a$ and $t_b$. Each vertex has 3 legs. Two legs are connected to the other vertex, and one leg is internal to the loop. But this is for the full 2-point function. For $\Sigma$, we have two external legs and the internal structure.
    The structure for $\Sigma$ from two $q^3$ vertices is: two external legs (one on each vertex), and the remaining 4 legs from the two vertices must form loops.
    The only 1PI diagram with two $q^3$ vertices and two external legs is the "sunset" (or "double triangle") graph.
    The diagram looks like: $t_1$ -- $G_0$ -- vertex 1 -- $G_0$ -- vertex 2 -- $G_0$ -- $t_2$.
    This is not correct. For $\Sigma$, the external legs are attached to the vertices.
    The diagram for $\Sigma^{(2)}$ is:
    External leg from $t_1$ connects to vertex 1.
    External leg from $t_2$ connects to vertex 2.
    Vertex 1 has 3 legs. One is external, so 2 legs connect to vertex 2.
    Vertex 2 has 3 legs. One is external, so 2 legs connect to vertex 1.
    This means 2 propagators connect vertex 1 and vertex 2. This is the "sunset".

    The vertex factor for $q^3$ is $-g$.
    The symmetry factor for the sunset diagram with two $q^3$ vertices is $2!$ (for swapping the two vertices).
    $$ \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = (-g)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2!} \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_a) G_0(t_b, t_2) $$
    This is not quite right. The structure of the sunset diagram for $\Sigma$ from $q^3$ vertices is that each vertex has 3 legs. Two legs must connect to the other vertex, and one leg must connect to a loop formed by other propagators.

    Let's re-evaluate the structure of $\Sigma^{(2)}$ from $q^3$ vertices.
    The diagram for $\Sigma$ has two external legs.
    At order $g^2$, we have two vertices, $V_1$ and $V_2$.
    Vertex $V_1$ has legs $l_{1a}, l_{1b}, l_{1c}$. Vertex $V_2$ has legs $l_{2a}, l_{2b}, l_{2c}$.
    The external legs are one from $V_1$ and one from $V_2$.
    The remaining 4 legs must connect the two vertices and form loops.
    The 1PI structure implies that cutting one internal line does not disconnect the diagram.
    The two $q^3$ vertices are connected by two propagators. One propagator connects $l_{1a}$ to $l_{2a}$, and another connects $l_{1b}$ to $l_{2b}$. The remaining legs $l_{1c}$ and $l_{2c}$ must also connect.
    If $l_{1c}$ connects to $l_{2c}$, we have a "double bridge" diagram. This is reducible.

    Let's follow the structure from Trace 1 for the quartic case. For $q^4$, the diagram was a loop between two external legs. For $q^3$, the vertex has 3 legs.
    For $\Sigma^{(1)}$, we had one $q^3$ vertex. Two legs contract to form a loop $G_0(t_v, t_v)$, one leg is external. This is wrong.
    The vertex rule is $-g$ for each $q^3$ vertex.
    For the 1-point function $\langle q \rangle$:
    $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, 3 G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$. This was correct.

    For $\Sigma^{(1)}$ (which is related to the mass renormalization):
    The diagram is a vertex with two external legs. The third leg must form a loop.
    $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2)$ involves one vertex. The vertex has 3 legs. Two legs are external (one to $t_1$, one to $t_2$). The third leg must form a loop *by itself*. This is not possible for a single line.
    In the context of $\Sigma$, the external legs are amputated. So $\Sigma$ is the sum of all 1PI diagrams with two amputated external legs.
    For a cubic interaction, this means a single vertex has 3 legs. Two are external, the third must loop back to itself. This is a "tadpole" diagram on the vertex.
    So $\Sigma^{(1)}$ comes from one vertex:
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, \delta(t_1-t_v) \delta(t_2-t_v) \cdot (\text{loop internal to vertex}) $$
    The loop internal to the vertex is a self-contraction. A $q^3$ vertex has 3 legs. If two are external, the third leg must contract with itself. This is not a standard diagram.

    Let's use the relation $\Sigma = G_0^{-1} - G^{-1}$.
    $G^{-1} = G_0^{-1} - \Sigma$.
    $G^{-1}(t_1, t_2) = (2m \partial_t^2 + m^2) \delta(t_1-t_2) - \Sigma(t_1, t_2)$.
    The leading order $\Sigma^{(1)}$ is a local correction to the mass.
    $\Sigma^{(1)}$ is the "tadpole" contribution. For a $q^3$ theory, the tadpole diagram for $\Sigma$ is a single vertex with two external lines, and the third line forms a loop.
    The structure is $t_1 - V - t_2$, where $V$ is a vertex. The vertex has 3 legs. Two are external. The third leg must form a loop. This is only possible if the third leg contracts with itself to form a $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ contribution.
    So, $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \delta(t_1-t_2) G_0(t_v,t_v)$ integrated over $t_v$.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, \delta(t_1-t_v) \delta(t_2-t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) G_0(t_1, t_1) \delta(t_1-t_2) $$
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \frac{1}{2m} \delta(t_1-t_2) = -\frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_1-t_2) $$
    This term contributes to mass renormalization. $m_{\text{eff}}^2 = m^2 + g/m$.

    Now, let's calculate $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$.
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) $$
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \left(-\frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_a - t_b)\right) G_0(t_b, t_2) $$
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = -\frac{g}{2m} \int dt_a \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) $$
    We already calculated $\int G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) dt_a = \frac{1}{4m^3} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
    $$ G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = -\frac{g}{2m} \left(\frac{1}{4m^3} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}\right) = -\frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} $$
    This is the contribution from the tadpole self-energy.

    **Order $g^2$ contribution to $G_2^c$:**
    This comes from $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0$ and also from $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$.
    The latter is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star (G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0) = G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_2^c(g^1)$. This accounts for diagrams like two tadpoles connected by $G_0$.

    Let's calculate $\Sigma^{(2)}$. The 1PI diagrams with two external legs and two $q^3$ vertices.
    The structure is two vertices connected by propagators.
    Vertex 1 (at $t_a$) and Vertex 2 (at $t_b$). Each has 3 legs.
    Two legs are external (one to $t_1$, one to $t_2$). This must be distributed.
    Case 1: One external leg from $V_1$, one external from $V_2$. Remaining 2 legs from $V_1$ and 2 from $V_2$. These 4 legs must connect $V_1$ and $V_2$ and form loops.
    The 1PI structure means cutting one line leaves it connected.
    The diagram is: $t_1 - V_1 - G_0 - V_2 - t_2$.
    $V_1$ has 3 legs. One is external to $t_1$. The other two must connect to $V_2$.
    $V_2$ has 3 legs. One is external to $t_2$. The other two must connect to $V_1$.
    This implies two propagators connect $V_1$ and $V_2$.
    This is the sunset diagram for $q^3$ vertices.
    $$ \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = (-g)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2!} \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) \cdot G_0(t_a, t_b) $$
    This is for $q^4$. For $q^3$:
    The vertex is $q^3$. Legs are $l_1, l_2, l_3$.
    External leg for $t_1$ attaches to $V_a$. External leg for $t_2$ attaches to $V_b$.
    Diagram: $t_1 \leftrightarrow V_a \leftrightarrow G_0 \leftrightarrow V_b \leftrightarrow t_2$.
    $V_a$ has 3 legs. One external. Two internal.
    $V_b$ has 3 legs. One external. Two internal.
    The internal legs connect $V_a$ and $V_b$.
    The two internal legs from $V_a$ connect to $V_b$. The two internal legs from $V_b$ connect to $V_a$.
    This means 2 propagators connect $V_a$ and $V_b$.
    The diagram is: $t_1 - V_a - G_0 - V_b - t_2$ and $t_1 - V_a - G_0 - V_b - t_2$ again.
    This is the "double bridge" diagram. Symmetry factor is $2!$ for swapping vertices.
    $$ \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{double bridge}}(t_1, t_2) = (-g)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2!} \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) \cdot G_0(t_a, t_b) $$
    This is incorrect. The diagram for $\Sigma$ has two external legs.
    The diagram for $\Sigma^{(2)}$: $t_1 \leftrightarrow V_1 \leftrightarrow G_0 \leftrightarrow V_2 \leftrightarrow t_2$.
    $V_1$ has 3 legs. One is external to $t_1$. The other two must connect to $V_2$.
    $V_2$ has 3 legs. One is external to $t_2$. The other two must connect to $V_1$.
    This means 2 propagators connect $V_1$ and $V_2$. The remaining 2 legs from $V_1$ and $V_2$ must also connect.
    The simplest 1PI diagram with two $q^3$ vertices and two external legs is the one where:
    $t_1$ connects to $V_1$. $t_2$ connects to $V_2$.
    $V_1$ connects to $V_2$ via $G_0$.
    $V_1$ connects to $V_2$ via $G_0$.
    The third leg of $V_1$ forms a loop. The third leg of $V_2$ forms a loop.
    This is not 1PI structure for $\Sigma$.

    Let's go back to the structure of $G_2^c$ directly.
    $G_2^c = G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$

    **Order $g^0$ contribution to $G_2^c$:**
    This is $G_0(t_1, t_2)$. However, $G_2^c$ is the *connected* part, so $G_2^c = G - G_0$. Thus, the $g^0$ contribution to $G_2^c$ is zero.

    **Order $g^1$ contribution to $G_2^c$:**
    This is from $\Sigma^{(1)}$.
    $\Sigma^{(1)} = -\frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_1-t_2)$.
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$.
    As calculated before: $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(g^1)} = -\frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

    **Order $g^2$ contribution to $G_2^c$:**
    This comes from two sources:
    a) $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0$, where $\Sigma^{(2)}$ is the sum of 1PI diagrams with two vertices.
    b) $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$.

    Let's calculate b) first, as it's simpler.
    $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 = G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star (G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0)$.
    Let $X = G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0 = G_2^c(g^1)$.
    Then this term is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star X$.
    $$ (G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star X)(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) X(t_b, t_2) $$
