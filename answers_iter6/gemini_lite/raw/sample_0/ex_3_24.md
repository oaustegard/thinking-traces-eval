## Solution to the Main Problem

Let the action be $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - gx^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$. We are working in zero dimensions, so the "propagator" is $1/S''(x)$ evaluated at the saddle point. The partition function is $Z = \int e^{-S(x)/\hbar} dx$. The free action is $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$, so $Z_0 = \int e^{-S_0(x)/\hbar} dx$. We are interested in the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$, which corresponds to the sum of all connected vacuum Feynman diagrams with 1 loop, where vertices and propagators are determined by the interaction terms in $S$.

The interaction terms in $S$ are $-gx^2$ and $-gx^3/6$.
The $-gx^2$ term can be seen as a 2-valent vertex with weight $-g$, or as a shift in the quadratic term.
The $-gx^3/6$ term is a 3-valent vertex with weight $-g$.

Let's rewrite the action by completing the square for the quadratic terms:
$S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(x^2 - 2gx^2) - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3 = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)x^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
For this to be a standard perturbation theory setup, we usually assume the quadratic term is in the "free" part, providing the propagator. If $1-2g \neq 0$, we can rewrite it as:
$S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)(x^2 - \tfrac{g}{3(1-2g)}x^3)$.
Let $x = y + \delta$, where $\delta$ is a constant.
$S(y+\delta) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)(y+\delta)^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}(y+\delta)^3$
$= \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)(y^2 + 2y\delta + \delta^2) - \tfrac{g}{6}(y^3 + 3y^2\delta + 3y\delta^2 + \delta^3)$
$= \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)y^2 + (1-2g)\delta y - \tfrac{g}{6}y^3 - \tfrac{g\delta}{2}y^2 - \tfrac{g\delta^2}{2}y - \tfrac{g\delta^3}{6} + \dots$

To absorb the $x^2$ term into the quadratic part, we can shift the field: $x = \tilde{x} + c$.
$S(\tilde{x}+c) = \tfrac{1}{2}(\tilde{x}+c)^2 - g(\tilde{x}+c)^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}(\tilde{x}+c)^3$
$= \tfrac{1}{2}\tilde{x}^2 + c\tilde{x} + \tfrac{c^2}{2} - g(\tilde{x}^2 + 2c\tilde{x} + c^2) - \tfrac{g}{6}(\tilde{x}^3 + 3\tilde{x}^2c + 3\tilde{x}c^2 + c^3)$
$= \tfrac{1}{2}\tilde{x}^2(1-2g) - \tilde{x}^2(g) + \tilde{x}(c - 2gc - \tfrac{gc^2}{2}) + \dots$
$= \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)\tilde{x}^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}\tilde{x}^3 + \dots$
We want the $x^2$ term to be $\tfrac{1}{2}\tilde{x}^2$. So, we need to choose $c$ such that the coefficient of $\tilde{x}^2$ in $S(\tilde{x}+c)$ is $1/2$.
Let's expand $S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)x^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
If $1-2g \ne 0$, we can define a new field $\phi = \sqrt{1-2g} x$. Then $x = \phi/\sqrt{1-2g}$.
$S(\phi) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g) \left(\tfrac{\phi}{\sqrt{1-2g}}\right)^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} \left(\tfrac{\phi}{\sqrt{1-2g}}\right)^3$
$S(\phi) = \tfrac{1}{2}\phi^2 - \tfrac{g}{6(1-2g)^{3/2}}\phi^3$.
This is now in the form of Trace 2, with a modified coupling constant for the cubic term: $g' = \tfrac{g}{(1-2g)^{3/2}}$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is the sum of all connected vacuum diagrams with one loop. The interaction terms are from $-gx^2$ and $-gx^3/6$.
In the context of perturbation theory around $S_0 = x^2/2$, the terms $-gx^2$ and $-gx^3/6$ provide vertices.
The $-gx^2$ term corresponds to a 2-valent vertex with weight $-g$.
The $-gx^3/6$ term corresponds to a 3-valent vertex with weight $-g$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is given by $\tfrac{\hbar}{2} \text{Tr} \log(G_0 \cdot K)$, where $K$ is the operator from the interaction terms. In zero dimensions, this is $\tfrac{1}{2}\log(\det G_0 \cdot K_{\text{int}})$, where $K_{\text{int}}$ is the matrix of interaction terms. Or, more directly, it's the sum of all connected 1-loop vacuum diagrams.

The free theory part is $S_0(x) = x^2/2$, which has $Z_0 = \int e^{-x^2/(2\hbar)} dx = \sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$. The propagator for the free theory is $G_0 = 1$.

The interaction terms are $-gx^2$ and $-gx^3/6$.
The $-gx^2$ term can be interpreted as a 2-valent vertex. A 2-valent vertex with two propagators attached forms a loop.
The $-gx^3/6$ term is a 3-valent vertex. A 3-valent vertex with three propagators attached forms a loop.

Let's consider the diagrams contributing to the 1-loop correction to $\log Z$. These are connected vacuum diagrams with a single loop.

1.  **Diagram from $-gx^2$ term:** This term represents a 2-valent vertex. A 1-loop diagram involving only this vertex means connecting two legs of this vertex with a propagator. Since it's a vacuum diagram, the "propagator" is just $1$ (from the free theory).
    *   Diagram: A single vertex with two legs, each connected by a propagator. This forms a loop.
    *   Contribution: The vertex has weight $-g$. There are $\binom{2}{2}=1$ way to pick legs for the loop. The symmetry factor for this diagram (a single loop with one vertex) is $1$. So, $(-g) \cdot 1 = -g$.

2.  **Diagram from $-gx^3/6$ term:** This term represents a 3-valent vertex. A 1-loop diagram involving only this vertex means connecting three legs of this vertex with propagators.
    *   Diagram: A single vertex with three legs, each connected by a propagator. This forms a loop.
    *   Contribution: The vertex has weight $-g$. There are $\binom{3}{3}=1$ way to pick legs. The symmetry factor for this diagram (a single loop with one vertex) is $1$. So, $(-g) \cdot 1 = -g$.

3.  **Diagrams involving both terms:**
    *   **2-valent vertex and 3-valent vertex:**
        *   Connecting one leg of the 2-valent vertex and two legs of the 3-valent vertex to form a loop. The remaining leg of the 3-valent vertex needs to be connected.
        *   Consider a loop formed by one 2-valent vertex and one 3-valent vertex.
            *   The 2-valent vertex has 2 legs, the 3-valent vertex has 3 legs. Total 5 legs.
            *   To form a vacuum diagram, these legs must be connected by propagators.
            *   If we have $V_2$ 2-valent vertices and $V_3$ 3-valent vertices, and $L$ loops, the number of propagators $E$ is given by $2V_2 + 3V_3 = 2E$. For $L=1$, $E = V_2 + V_3 - 1$.
            *   So, $2V_2 + 3V_3 = 2(V_2 + V_3 - 1) \implies 2V_2 + 3V_3 = 2V_2 + 2V_3 - 2 \implies V_3 = -2$. This is impossible.
            *   This implies that a single loop cannot be formed by just one 2-valent and one 3-valent vertex. What is missing? The action $S(x)$ is a polynomial in $x$, not a sum of independent vertex terms. The $-gx^2$ term modifies the *quadratic* part, effectively changing the propagator or providing a tadpole contribution.

Let's re-evaluate the action and its interpretation in perturbation theory.
$S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}x^2 - gx^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3 = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)x^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.
Let's assume $1-2g \ne 0$. We can rescale the field: $\phi = \sqrt{1-2g} x$, so $x = \phi/\sqrt{1-2g}$.
$S(\phi) = \tfrac{1}{2}\phi^2 - \tfrac{g}{6(1-2g)^{3/2}}\phi^3$.
