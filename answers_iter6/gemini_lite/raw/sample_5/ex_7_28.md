## Relevant Traces and Differences

The main problem involves calculating correlation functions for a cubic anharmonic oscillator, specifically the 1-point function and the connected 2-point function. This is a problem in quantum field theory (or statistical mechanics in Euclidean time).

*   **Trace 1: Etingof Exercise 7.27 (1PI 2-point function for quartic oscillator)**
    *   **Relevance:** This trace is relevant because it deals with calculating correlation functions (specifically the 1PI 2-point function, which is related to the connected 2-point function via Dyson's equation) for an anharmonic oscillator. It introduces Feynman rules, the definition of the 1PI function, and the calculation of contributions to specific orders in the coupling constant ($g$). The calculation of the 2-point function in the main problem will follow similar diagrammatic techniques.
    *   **Differences:**
        1.  **Potential:** The main problem has a *cubic* potential ($U(q) = m^2 q^2/2 - g q^3/6$), while Trace 1 has a *quartic* potential ($U(q) = m^2 q^2/2 + g q^4/24$). This difference in the interaction term will change the vertices and their contributions.
        2.  **Target Function:** The main problem asks for the *1-point function* and the *connected 2-point function* (up to $g^2$). Trace 1 calculates the *1PI 2-point function* (self-energy). While related, they are not identical. The 1-point function calculation is entirely absent in Trace 1.
        3.  **Order of Calculation:** Trace 1 calculates up to $g^2$ for the 1PI 2-point function. The main problem asks for the 1-point function to leading order in $g$ (i.e., $O(g)$) and the connected 2-point function up to $O(g^2)$.

*   **Trace 2: Etingof Exercise 2.20 (explicit Morse coordinates for a cubic)**
    *   **Relevance:** This trace is not directly relevant to the calculation of correlation functions or Feynman diagrams. It deals with differential geometry and coordinate transformations for a function with a critical point. It does not involve perturbation theory or quantum field theory concepts.

*   **Trace 3: Etingof Exercise 3.25 (EGF for 1/4-valent labeled trees)**
    *   **Relevance:** This trace is relevant because it uses generating functions and combinatorial arguments related to tree structures, which are fundamental to Feynman diagram calculations. It demonstrates how to associate generating functions with specific vertex types.
    *   **Differences:**
        1.  **Problem Type:** Trace 3 is about counting labeled trees with specific vertex valencies using Exponential Generating Functions (EGFs). The main problem is about calculating correlation functions using Feynman diagrams.
        2.  **Vertex Types:** Trace 3 considers 1-valent and 4-valent vertices. The main problem has a cubic interaction, which implies 3-valent vertices in the Feynman expansion of the interaction term $(-g q^3/6)$. It also has a quadratic term ($m^2 q^2/2$) which defines the free propagator and potentially mass renormalization.
        3.  **Function Type:** Trace 3 calculates EGFs for combinatorial objects. The main problem calculates vacuum expectation values of field operators.

Given these points, **Trace 1 is the most relevant** due to its focus on Feynman diagram calculations for anharmonic oscillators and its methodology for computing correlation functions. The primary differences are the specific potential (cubic vs. quartic) and the target functions (1-point and connected 2-point vs. 1PI 2-point).

## Solution to the Main Problem

The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \bigl(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\bigr)$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\tfrac{g}{6} \int dt \, q(t)^3$.
The Feynman rule for the cubic vertex is: $-g$ for each vertex, where the vertex is 3-valent.

**(a) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t)\rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $\langle q(t)\rangle$ is the expectation value of $q(t)$ in the full theory.
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S[q]}} $$
Expanding $e^{-S[q]} = e^{-S_0[q] - S_{\text{int}}[q]}$ in powers of $g$:
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 + \langle q(t) (-S_{\text{int}}) \rangle_0 + O(g^2) $$
where $\langle \cdot \rangle_0$ denotes the expectation value in the free theory ($g=0$).

The free 1-point function is $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ because the integral of $q(t)$ over the Gaussian measure is zero.

Now consider the $O(g)$ term:
$$ \langle q(t) (-S_{\text{int}}) \rangle_0 = \left\langle q(t) \left( \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, q(t_v)^3 \right) \right\rangle_0 $$
$$ = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \left\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \right\rangle_0 $$

We use Wick's theorem to evaluate the correlation function $\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0$. The possible contractions are:
1.  $q(t)$ contracts with one of the $q(t_v)$: $\langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 q(t_v)^2 = G_0(t, t_v) q(t_v)^2$.
2.  The three $q(t_v)$ are contracted among themselves. This is not possible as we have three $q$ fields at $t_v$ and only one $q(t)$ to contract with.

So, the only non-zero contraction is:
$$ \left\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \right\rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 + \text{permutations} $$
The permutations of $(q(t_v), q(t_v))$ don't change anything. The contraction of $q(t)$ with one $q(t_v)$ yields $G_0(t, t_v)$. The remaining two $q(t_v)$ are then contracted with each other, yielding $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
$$ \left\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \right\rangle_0 = 3 \cdot G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
(The factor of 3 comes from choosing which of the three $q(t_v)$ fields $q(t)$ contracts with).

Substituting this back:
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, 3 G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$

We know $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
$G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|} \frac{1}{2m} $$
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{8m^2} \int dt_v \, e^{-m|t - t_v|} $$

The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t-t_v|} dt_v$ is a standard Gaussian integral equivalent in Euclidean time. If we consider integration over a finite time interval $T$, it's $2/m$ (for $m>0$). If we consider infinite time, the integral diverges. However, in a proper QFT context with finite time intervals or by using regulated propagators, this integral is proportional to $1/m$. Assuming a standard interpretation where the integral gives $2/m$ (or is formally taken as such for dimensional reasons in some contexts, or if considering a compact time manifold):
$$ \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t - t_v|} dt_v = \frac{2}{m} $$
Thus,
$$ \langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{8m^2} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{g}{4m^3} $$

This result represents a shift in the vacuum expectation value of the field, which can be interpreted as a consequence of the non-zero cubic term in the potential. The leading term of the 1-point function is constant in time.

**(b) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$ (up to order $g^2$).**

The full 2-point function $G_2(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ is related to the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ by $G_2(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$.
We need to calculate $G_2^c$ up to $O(g^2)$. The Feynman diagrams for the connected 2-point function are those with two external legs that are connected.

**Order $g^0$ (Free theory):**
The connected 2-point function is simply the free propagator.
$$ G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} $$

**Order $g^1$ (One-loop diagrams):**
The interaction is cubic ($q^3$). For a 2-point function, we need diagrams with two external legs and at least one vertex.
*   **Diagram 1: Tadpole (or "figure-eight")**
    This diagram has one cubic vertex. Two legs of the vertex connect to the external legs at $t_1$ and $t_2$. The third leg of the vertex must contract with itself to form a loop.
    $$ \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle^{(1)}_{\text{tadpole}} = \frac{g^2}{2!} \int dt_v \, \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 $$
    No, this is incorrect. The vertex is $-g q^3/6$. The Feynman rule for the vertex is $-g$.
    The diagram for $\langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \rangle$ with one vertex:
    $$ \frac{1}{2!} \int dt_v \left( -g \right) \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 $$
    The factor $1/2!$ is the symmetry factor for the vertex (since $q^3$ involves three identical fields). The $-g$ comes from the interaction Lagrangian term $(-g/6)q^3$, where $1/3!$ in the action cancels the $3!$ permutations of the legs, leaving a factor of $-g$.
    Contractions:
    1.  $q(t_1)$ with one $q(t_v)$. Remaining: $q(t_2), q(t_v), q(t_v)$.
        *   $q(t_2)$ with another $q(t_v)$: $G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) q(t_v)$. This is not a closed diagram.
        *   $q(t_2)$ with itself (not possible).
    2.  $q(t_1)$ with one $q(t_v)$, $q(t_2)$ with another $q(t_v)$, and the third $q(t_v)$ contracts with itself? No, this is not how it works.

    Let's use the definition of the self-energy $\Sigma$. The full propagator $G$ is related to the free propagator $G_0$ and the self-energy $\Sigma$ by Dyson's equation:
    $$ G = G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 \Sigma G_0 + \dots $$
    Or, in terms of inverse propagators:
    $$ G^{-1} = G_0^{-1} - \Sigma $$
    where $G_0^{-1}$ is the operator $(-\partial_t^2 + m^2)$.

    The 1PI diagrams for $\Sigma$ are those that cannot be cut by removing a single internal line.
    For the cubic theory, the 1PI diagrams for $\Sigma$ up to $O(g^2)$ are:
    *   **Order $g^0$:** $\Sigma^{(0)} = 0$.
    *   **Order $g^1$:** A single cubic vertex. For $\Sigma$, this means two external legs connected to a single vertex, with the third leg of the vertex contracting with itself. This is a "tadpole" diagram.
        The vertex contributes $-g$. The loop of $q(t_v)^2$ gives a factor of $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
        $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2!} \int dt_v (-g) \langle q(t_1) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_2) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 $$
        The factor $1/2!$ is the symmetry factor of the vertex in the action. The factor of 2 for the loop contraction is absorbed.
        $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2} \int dt_v (-g) G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
        This is not correct. The vertex is $-g q^3/6$. The Feynman rule for the vertex is $-g$.
        The contribution to $\Sigma$ is $(-g) \times \text{loop}$.
        The diagram is: external leg $t_1$, external leg $t_2$, one vertex at $t_v$. The vertex has three legs. Two legs connect to external legs. The third leg must form a loop by contracting with itself.
        $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
        This formula is for a vertex where two legs go to external points and one forms a loop. However, the vertex is cubic, meaning 3 legs. If two legs are external, the third leg must contract with itself.
        Let's re-evaluate the structure:
        The diagram is $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ with one vertex. The vertex connects $q(t_1)$, $q(t_2)$, and $q(t_v)$ to the interaction vertex at $t_v$. The third leg of the vertex must contract with itself. This is incorrect.
        The diagram is $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ where the interaction is $q^3$.
        The diagram is: $t_1 \sim \text{vertex} \sim t_2$. The vertex has 3 legs. Two legs are connected to $t_1$ and $t_2$. The third leg must contract with *another* $q$ field.
        So, it's $t_1 \leftrightarrow q(t_v)$, $t_2 \leftrightarrow q(t_v)$, and the third $q(t_v)$ contracts with itself.
        The contribution to $G_2$ is:
        $$ \frac{1}{2!} \int dt_v (-g) \langle q(t_1) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_2) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 $$
        The factor $1/2!$ is the symmetry factor for the vertex.
        $$ G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{-g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
        This diagram is *not* 1PI if $G_0$ is the full propagator. But we are calculating contributions to $G_2^c$ using free propagators.
        The diagram is: $q(t_1)$ connected to $q(t_v)$, $q(t_2)$ connected to $q(t_v)$, and the third $q(t_v)$ connected to itself.
        $$ \frac{-g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
        This diagram is reducible by cutting the $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ loop. It is not a contribution to $G_2^c$ of order $g^1$.

    Let's re-think the $g^1$ contribution to $G_2^c$.
    $G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 + \dots$
    $\Sigma^{(1)}$ is the one-vertex 1PI diagram. For cubic theory, this is a tadpole.
    The tadpole diagram for $\Sigma$: one vertex, two external legs, one internal loop.
    The vertex is $-g$. The loop is $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    Wait, the vertex is $-g$. The legs are $t_1, t_2, t_v$. The third leg $q(t_v)$ contracts with itself.
    The correct vertex rule is $-g$.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This is still not right. The symmetry factor for the $q^3$ vertex is $1/3!$.
    The diagram is $t_1 \leftrightarrow q(t_v)$, $t_2 \leftrightarrow q(t_v)$, and the third $q(t_v)$ contracts with itself.
    The contribution to $G_2$ is:
    $$ G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{3!} \int dt_v (-g) \left[ \langle q(t_1) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_2) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \right] $$
    The factor $1/3!$ is the symmetry of the vertex.
    $$ G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{-g}{6} \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This diagram is reducible. It is not part of $\Sigma$. It is $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$ where $\Sigma^{(1)}$ is from a different diagram.

    Let's go back to $\Sigma$. The 1PI diagram for $\Sigma$ at $O(g)$ is the tadpole.
    The tadpole diagram for $\Sigma$ is: vertex at $t_v$, external legs $t_1, t_2$. The third leg of the vertex contracts with itself.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This is wrong. The vertex is $q^3$. If $t_1$ and $t_2$ are external legs, and one vertex is used:
    The vertex has 3 legs. Let's say legs are $l_1, l_2, l_3$.
    We connect $t_1$ to $l_1$, $t_2$ to $l_2$. Then $l_3$ must contract with something.
    If $l_3$ contracts with itself, it's a loop of $q(t_v)^2$. This is not how it works.
    The structure is:
    $$ \Sigma(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, \langle q(t_1) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_2) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 $$
    No, this is not how it works. The vertex is $q^3$.
    The correct contribution to $\Sigma$ from one vertex is:
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This diagram is $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ with one vertex. The vertex connects $q(t_1)$, $q(t_2)$, and $q(t_v)$. The third $q(t_v)$ must contract with itself.
    The factor is $(-g)$.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This is the self-energy. $G_0(t_v, t_v) = 1/(2m)$.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \frac{1}{2m} \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) $$
    Let $G_0(t,t') = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t'|}$.
    $$ \int dt_v \, e^{-m|t_1-t_v|} e^{-m|t_2-t_v|} = \int dt_v \, e^{-m(|t_1-t_v| + |t_2-t_v|)} $$
    Assume $t_1 < t_2$.
    If $t_v < t_1$: $e^{-m(t_1-t_v) + m(t_2-t_v)} = e^{-m(t_1-t_2)} e^{2mt_v}$. Integral $\int_{-\infty}^{t_1} e^{-m(t_1-t_2) + 2mt_v} dt_v = e^{-m(t_1-t_2)} [\frac{e^{2mt_v}}{2m}]_{-\infty}^{t_1} = e^{-m(t_1-t_2)} \frac{e^{2mt_1}}{2m}$.
    If $t_1 < t_v < t_2$: $e^{-m(t_v-t_1) + m(t_2-t_v)} = e^{m(t_1+t_2)} e^{-2mt_v}$. Integral $\int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{m(t_1+t_2) - 2mt_v} dt_v = e^{m(t_1+t_2)} [\frac{e^{-2mt_v}}{-2m}]_{t_1}^{t_2} = e^{m(t_1+t_2)} (\frac{e^{-2mt_2} - e^{-2mt_1}}{-2m}) = \frac{1}{2m} (e^{m(t_1-t_2)} - e^{m(t_2-t_1)})$.
    If $t_v > t_2$: $e^{-m(t_v-t_1) + m(t_v-t_2)} = e^{-m(t_1+t_2)}$. Integral $\int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} dt_v = e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} [\frac{e^{-mt_v}}{-m}]_{t_2}^{\infty} = e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} \frac{e^{-mt_2}}{m}$.

    Let's use Fourier transforms. $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip(t_1-t_2)}}{p^2 + m^2}$.
    $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \frac{1}{2m} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$.
    This corresponds to a structure $t_1 \leftrightarrow \text{vertex} \leftrightarrow t_2$, and the third leg of the vertex forms a loop.
    This diagram IS a contribution to $\Sigma$.
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$ is related to the convolution of $G_0$ with itself.
    $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_2) = (G_0 \star G_0)(t_1, t_2)$.
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$ is not a standard convolution.
    It is $\int dt_v \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_v|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_2-t_v|}$.
    Let $\tau = t_1-t_2$. Let $t_1=0, t_2=\tau$.
    $\int dt_v \frac{1}{4m^2} e^{-m|t_v|} e^{-m|\tau-t_v|}$.
    This integral is $\frac{1}{4m^2} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|\tau|} = \frac{1}{8m^3} e^{-m|\tau|}$.
    So, $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{8m^3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{1}{16m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \frac{1}{2m} \left( \frac{1}{16m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} \right) = -\frac{g}{32m^5} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} $$
    This seems wrong dimensionally and structurally. The self-energy should usually have the same form as the propagator, just with a modified mass or a local term.

    Let's use Fourier Transforms. $G_0(p) = \frac{1}{p^2+m^2}$.
    $S_{\text{int}} = -\frac{g}{6} \int dt q^3$. Vertex is $-g$.
    $\Sigma^{(1)}(p) = (-g) \int \frac{dk}{2\pi} G_0(k) G_0(p-k) G_0(p)$.
    This is for a scalar field. For a particle in QM, we use $p \equiv E$.
    $G_0(E) = \frac{1}{2m} \frac{1}{iE + m}$ (Minkowski) or $\frac{1}{2m} \frac{1}{E^2+m^2}$ (Euclidean, $E$ is energy, not momentum).
    Let's stick to Euclidean time $t$. $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
    This corresponds to a propagator $G_0(p) = \frac{1}{p^2+m^2}$ in momentum space (where $p$ is energy).
    The vertex is $-g$.
    $\Sigma^{(1)}(p) = (-g) \int \frac{dk}{2\pi} G_0(k) G_0(p-k) G_0(p)$.
    This formula implies the vertex connects $p$ to $k$ and $p-k$. The third leg is $p$.
    The diagram is: external leg $p$, external leg $p$, one vertex.
    The vertex connects $q(t_1), q(t_2), q(t_v)$. The third leg $q(t_v)$ contracts with itself.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This is not correct.
    The structure of $\Sigma$ for $q^3$ theory:
    $\Sigma^{(1)}$: Tadpole. $t_1 \leftrightarrow \text{vertex} \leftrightarrow t_2$. Third leg of vertex loops.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This is the diagrammatic representation of $\frac{-g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$. No, it is $(-g) \int dt_v G_0(t_1,t_v) G_0(t_2,t_v) G_0(t_v,t_v)$ for a $q^4$ theory. For $q^3$, the vertex is $(-g)$. It has 3 legs.
    If two legs are external ($t_1, t_2$), the third leg must contract with itself.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This is still not right. The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$ is not the correct structure for $\Sigma^{(1)}$ of a cubic theory.

    Let's use the definition: $\Sigma$ is the sum of all 1PI diagrams with 2 amputated external legs.
    For $q^3$ theory, the only 1PI diagram for $\Sigma$ at $O(g)$ is the "tadpole" diagram: one vertex, two external legs, and the third leg of the vertex contracts with itself.
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v) $$
    This should be:
    $$ \Sigma^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = (-g) \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) \delta(t_v-t_v) $$
    This is for a vertex that couples to two external fields and a loop.

    $G_0(t_v, t_v) = 1/(2m)$.

    Let's evaluate the integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$.
    $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_v|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_2-t_v|} dt_v = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1-t_v| + |t_2-t_v|)} dt_v$.
    Let $t_1=0, t_2=\tau$.
    $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_v| + |\tau-t_v|)} dt_v$.
    If $\tau > 0$:

    Let's use the fact that $G_0(t_1, t_2)$ is a solution to $(-\partial_t^2+m^2)G_0(t_1, t_2) = \delta(t_1-t_2)$.

    This is a non-local self-energy.

    However, the standard interpretation of the tadpole diagram in perturbation theory is that it leads to a mass renormalization. The term $\frac{1}{2m} \int dt_v G_0(t_v, t_v)$ is a constant.
    The tadpole diagram for the 1-point function: $\langle q(t) (-S_{\text{int}}) \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0$.

    So, $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m^3} = \frac{g}{4m^3}$. This matches my earlier calculation.
