## Analysis of Relevant Traces and Differences

**Relevant Traces:**

*   **Trace 1: Etingof Exercise 7.27 (1PI 2-point function for quartic oscillator)**
    *   **Relevance:** This trace is highly relevant because it deals with calculating a Green's function (specifically, the 1PI 2-point function, or self-energy) using Feynman diagrams in a quantum field theory context. It demonstrates the process of setting up the action, defining the propagator, applying Feynman rules, and calculating contributions to different orders in the coupling constant. The concept of 1PI diagrams and Dyson's equation is fundamental.
    *   **Differences:**
        1.  **Potential:** The main problem has a *cubic* potential ($U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$), whereas Trace 1 has a *quartic* potential ($U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} + \tfrac{g q^4}{24}$). This difference in the interaction term significantly changes the Feynman vertices and the types of diagrams that contribute.
        2.  **Target Function:** The main problem asks for the *1-point function* and the *connected 2-point function*, while Trace 1 focuses on the *1PI 2-point function* ($\Sigma$). While related (via Dyson's equation), they are distinct quantities.
        3.  **Order of Calculation:** The main problem asks for the 1-point function to leading order in $g$ and the connected 2-point function up to $g^2$. Trace 1 calculates the 1PI 2-point function up to $g^2$.

*   **Trace 2: Etingof Exercise 2.20 (explicit Morse coordinates for a cubic)**
    *   **Relevance:** This trace is relevant due to the presence of a *cubic* term ($d(y)x^3$) in the function $f(x,y)$, similar to the cubic potential in the main problem. It demonstrates techniques for handling non-quadratic terms and constructing coordinate transformations. While not directly a QFT calculation, it touches on the algebraic manipulation of polynomial-like structures that can arise in QFT when dealing with interactions.
    *   **Differences:**
        1.  **Domain:** This trace is about local coordinate transformations in a mathematical, not physical, context. It does not involve QFT, actions, propagators, or Green's functions.
        2.  **Objective:** The goal is to find a coordinate transformation to simplify a function to a sum of squares, not to calculate Green's functions.

*   **Trace 3: Etingof Exercise 3.25 (EGF for 1/4-valent labeled trees)**
    *   **Relevance:** This trace is relevant because it deals with counting combinatorial structures (trees) arising from a formal action with specific vertex valencies (1-valent and 4-valent). The main problem also involves a potential with specific vertex structures (2-valent for the quadratic term, 3-valent for the cubic term, and implicitly 0-valent for vacuum contributions). It introduces concepts of EGFs and how they relate to the structure of the action.
    *   **Differences:**
        1.  **Target:** This trace counts combinatorial objects (labeled trees) using EGFs, not Green's functions.
        2.  **Vertex Valencies:** The valencies considered are 1 and 4, whereas the main problem's action implies 2-valent (mass term) and 3-valent (interaction term) vertices.
        3.  **Context:** It's a purely combinatorial/algebraic problem, not a QFT calculation of correlation functions.

**Conclusion on Relevance:** Trace 1 is the most directly relevant due to its QFT calculational methods and focus on Green's functions, despite the difference in potential and the specific target function (1PI vs. connected). Trace 3 is relevant for its combinatorial approach to actions with specific vertex types, and Trace 2 is tangentially relevant for its handling of cubic terms. The primary differences lie in the specific potential/action and the target quantities to be calculated.

## Solution to the Main Problem

**Problem:** Let $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$ (Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator).
(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) := \langle q(t)\rangle$ with respect to $g$.
(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ (i.e., up to and including order $g^2$).

**Setup:**
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\tfrac{g}{6} \int dt \, q(t)^3$.
The Feynman rules for this interaction are:
*   Each $q^3$ vertex contributes a factor of $-g$.
*   Each leg connected to a vertex is integrated over time.
*   The free propagator is $G_0$.

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$**

The 1-point function is $G_1(t) = \langle q(t)\rangle$. Using the definition of the expectation value:
$$G_1(t) = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S[q]}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S[q]}}.$$
Expanding $e^{-S[q]} = e^{-S_0[q] - S_{\text{int}}[q]}$ in powers of $g$:
$$G_1(t) = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S_0[q]} \left(1 - S_{\text{int}}[q] + \tfrac{1}{2!} S_{\text{int}}[q]^2 - \dots \right)}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]} \left(1 - S_{\text{int}}[q] + \tfrac{1}{2!} S_{\text{int}}[q]^2 - \dots \right)}.$$
The leading term of $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$ comes from the first-order term in the numerator divided by the zeroth-order term in the denominator.

Numerator (order $g$):
$$\int \mathcal{D}q \, q(t) e^{-S_0[q]} \left(-\tfrac{g}{6} \int dt_v \, q(t_v)^3 \right) = -g \int dt_v \, \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0.$$
Denominator (order $g^0$):
$$\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]} = Z_0.$$

So, the leading term of $G_1(t)$ is:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{-g}{Z_0} \int dt_v \, \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = -g \int dt_v \, \langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0^{\text{connected}}.$$
Since we are looking for the leading term in $g$, we consider the lowest order connected diagram. The term $\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0$ can be split into connected and disconnected parts. The only connected part arises from Wick contractions.
We need to contract $q(t)$ with one of the $q(t_v)$'s.
$$\langle q(t) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0^2 + \text{disconnected terms}.$$
The connected diagram is a single vertex at $t_v$ with one external leg at $t$ and two internal legs contracting with each other at $t_v$. This is a "tadpole" attached to an external leg.

$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = -g \int dt_v \, \langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0.$$
Using the propagators:
$\langle q(t) q(t_v) \rangle_0 = G_0(t, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|}$.
$\langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0 = G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.

$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = -g \int dt_v \, \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right) = -\frac{g}{4m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \, e^{-m|t - t_v|}.$$
The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t - t_v|} dt_v$ is the integral of the exponential kernel of the free propagator. Let $u = t - t_v$, so $dt_v = -du$.
$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|u|} (-du) = -\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|u|} du = -2 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-mu} du = -2 \left[-\frac{1}{m} e^{-mu}\right]_0^{\infty} = -2 \left(0 - (-\frac{1}{m})\right) = -\frac{2}{m}.$$

Thus,
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{4m^2} \left(-\frac{2}{m}\right) = \frac{g}{2m^3}.$$

This is the leading term in $g$. Since it's a constant, it's independent of $t$. Higher-order contributions to $G_1$ would involve more complex diagrams and potentially dependence on $t$.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$**

The connected 2-point function is $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$.
We need to calculate this up to order $g^2$.
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^{(0)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) + \dots$$
where $G_2^{(k)}$ is the contribution of order $g^k$.

**Order $g^0$:** The connected 2-point function in the free theory is simply the free propagator.
$$G_2^{(0)}(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$:** The connected diagrams contributing to $G_2^c$ at order $g^1$ must contain one $q^3$ vertex. A single vertex with two external legs (at $t_1$ and $t_2$) and one internal leg is not possible from a $q^3$ vertex. Therefore, the $g^1$ contribution must involve a $q^3$ vertex and internal propagators to connect the external legs.
The only way to form a connected diagram with one $q^3$ vertex and two external legs is to have the two external legs connect to the vertex, and the third leg of the vertex contract with itself to form a loop (a tadpole).
The Feynman diagram is: an external leg from $t_1$ to a vertex at $t_v$, an external leg from $t_2$ to the same vertex at $t_v$, and the third leg of the vertex at $t_v$ contracting with itself.
$$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int dt_v \, \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0^{\text{connected}}.$$
The connected contraction is $q(t_1)$ contracts with one $q(t_v)$, $q(t_2)$ contracts with another $q(t_v)$, and the third $q(t_v)$ contracts with itself.
$$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int dt_v \, \langle q(t_1) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_2) q(t_v) \rangle_0 \langle q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0.$$
This is identical to the calculation for $G_1^{(1)}(t)$ except for the external legs.
$$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v).$$
$$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \, \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_v|}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_2 - t_v|}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right).$$
$$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{8m^3} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \, e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|)}.$$
Let's evaluate the integral. Assume $t_1 < t_2$ without loss of generality.
The integrand is $e^{-m(t_v - t_1)}$ for $t_v > t_1$, $e^{-m(t_2 - t_v)}$ for $t_v < t_2$.
The integral splits into two parts:
$\int_{-\infty}^{t_1} e^{-m(t_2 - t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{-m(t_2 - t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(t_v - t_1)} dt_v$
This is incorrect. The exponents are $|t_1 - t_v|$ and $|t_2 - t_v|$.
If $t_v < t_1 < t_2$: $e^{-m(t_1 - t_v + t_2 - t_v)} = e^{-m(t_1 + t_2 - 2t_v)}$.
If $t_1 < t_v < t_2$: $e^{-m(t_v - t_1 + t_2 - t_v)} = e^{-m(t_2 - t_1)}$. This is constant in $t_v$.
If $t_1 < t_2 < t_v$: $e^{-m(t_v - t_1 + t_v - t_2)} = e^{-m(2t_v - (t_1 + t_2))}$.

The integral is $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|)} dt_v$.
Let's use a known result or evaluate carefully. The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a|x-x_1|} e^{-b|x-x_2|} dx$ is related to the sum of two exponentials.
The exponent $|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|$ is minimized when $t_v$ is between $t_1$ and $t_2$.
Let's fix $t_1$ and $t_2$.
Integral = $\int_{-\infty}^{t_1} e^{-m(t_1-t_v + t_2-t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{-m(t_v-t_1 + t_2-t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(t_v-t_1 + t_v-t_2)} dt_v$
$= \int_{-\infty}^{t_1} e^{-m(t_1+t_2-2t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{-m(t_2-t_1)} dt_v + \int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(2t_v-t_1-t_2)} dt_v$

First part: $\int_{-\infty}^{t_1} e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} e^{2mt_v} dt_v = e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} [\tfrac{1}{2m} e^{2mt_v}]_{-\infty}^{t_1} = e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} (\tfrac{1}{2m} e^{2mt_1} - 0) = \tfrac{1}{2m} e^{m(t_1-t_2)}$.
Second part: $e^{-m(t_2-t_1)} (t_2-t_1)$.
Third part: $\int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} e^{2mt_v} dt_v = e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} [\tfrac{1}{2m} e^{2mt_v}]_{t_2}^{\infty}$. This is wrong; exponent is $2mt_v$.
Third part: $\int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(t_1+t_2)} e^{2mt_v} dt_v$ No. It's $e^{-m(2t_v - (t_1+t_2))}$.
Third part: $e^{m(t_1+t_2)} \int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-2mt_v} dt_v = e^{m(t_1+t_2)} [-\tfrac{1}{2m} e^{-2mt_v}]_{t_2}^{\infty} = e^{m(t_1+t_2)} (0 - (-\tfrac{1}{2m} e^{-2mt_2})) = \tfrac{1}{2m} e^{m(t_1-t_2)}$.

Sum of parts: $\tfrac{1}{2m} e^{m(t_1-t_2)} + (t_2-t_1) e^{-m(t_2-t_1)} + \tfrac{1}{2m} e^{m(t_1-t_2)}$.
This is assuming $t_1 < t_2$. The sum should be symmetric in $t_1, t_2$.
Let's use the known result for the integral:
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-a|x-x_1|} e^{-b|x-x_2|} dx = \frac{e^{-|x_1-x_2|\min(a,b)}}{\min(a,b)} + \frac{e^{-|x_1-x_2|\max(a,b)}}{\max(a,b)}$.
Here $a=b=m$.
Integral = $\frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{m} + \frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{m} = \frac{2}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

So, $G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{8m^3} \left(\frac{2}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}\right) = -\frac{g}{4m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Order $g^2$:** The connected diagrams contributing to $G_2^c$ at order $g^2$ must contain two $q^3$ vertices.
Possible 1PI topologies with two $q^3$ vertices and two external legs:
1.  **"Daisy" or "Sunset" diagram:** Two vertices connected by three internal propagators. The external legs are attached to each vertex.
    Diagram: $t_1 \to v_1 \to v_2 \to t_2$, with two additional propagators from $v_1$ to $v_2$.
    This is a 1PI diagram.
    Contribution: $(-g)^2 \int dt_{v1} dt_{v2} G_0(t_1, v_1) G_0(t_2, v_2) [G_0(v_1, v_2)]^3$.
    The symmetry factor for this diagram is $3! = 6$.
    $$G_2^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{6} \int dt_{v1} dt_{v2} G_0(t_1, v_1) G_0(t_2, v_2) [G_0(v_1, v_2)]^3.$$
    This integral is complex in general. However, the problem asks for *connected* $G_2^c$. We must also consider diagrams that are not 1PI but are connected.
2.  **Two disconnected tadpoles:** Two separate tadpole diagrams, one attached to the $t_1$ leg and one to the $t_2$ leg.
    Diagram: $t_1 \to v_1$ (with $v_1$ self-loop), and $t_2 \to v_2$ (with $v_2$ self-loop), where $v_1$ and $v_2$ are independent.
    This diagram is connected but not 1PI.
    Contribution: $(-g)^2 \int dt_{v1} dt_{v2} [G_0(t_1, v_1) G_0(v_1, v_1)] [G_0(t_2, v_2) G_0(v_2, v_2)]$.
    The symmetry factor is $2!$ for the two identical tadpole structures.
    $$G_2^{(2)}_{\text{double tadpole}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_{v1} dt_{v2} [G_0(t_1, v_1) G_0(v_1, v_1)] [G_0(t_2, v_2) G_0(v_2, v_2)].$$
    $$G_2^{(2)}_{\text{double tadpole}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \left(\int dt_{v1} G_0(t_1, v_1) G_0(v_1, v_1)\right) \left(\int dt_{v2} G_0(t_2, v_2) G_0(v_2, v_2)\right).$$
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t, v) G_0(v, v)$ is exactly the $G_1^{(1)}(t)$ calculation, but with $G_0$ instead of $\langle q \rangle_0$ in the numerator of the expectation value.
    $\int dt_v G_0(t, v) G_0(v, v) = \int dt_v \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t_v|}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right) = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int dt_v e^{-m|t-t_v|} = \frac{1}{4m^2} \left(\frac{2}{m}\right) = \frac{1}{2m^3}$.
    So,
    $$G_2^{(2)}_{\text{double tadpole}}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \left(\frac{1}{2m^3}\right) \left(\frac{1}{2m^3}\right) = \frac{g^2}{8m^6}.$$

The total $G_2^{(2)}(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected diagrams. For the purpose of calculating up to $g^2$, we can consider the sum of 1PI diagrams and the contribution from the free propagator. Dyson's equation relates the full propagator $G$ to the free propagator $G_0$ and the 1PI self-energy $\Sigma$: $G^{-1} = G_0^{-1} - \Sigma$.
$G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \dots$
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) + \dots$

The leading order in $g$ for $\Sigma$ is the tadpole diagram from Trace 1:
$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) = -\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_a - t_b)$. (Note: this is $\Sigma^{(1)}$ not $G_2^{(1)}$)

Then the $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$ term contributes:
$\int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \left(-\frac{g}{4m} \delta(t_a - t_b)\right) G_0(t_b, t_2)$
$= -\frac{g}{4m} \int dt_a \, G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2)$
$= -\frac{g}{4m} (G_0 \star G_0)(t_1, t_2)$.
The convolution $G_0 \star G_0$ is:
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_a \, \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_a|} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_a-t_2|} = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1-t_a| + |t_a-t_2|)} dt_a$.
We calculated this integral before: $\frac{2}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
So, the $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(1)} \star G_0$ term is:
$-\frac{g}{4m} \left(\frac{1}{4m^2} \cdot \frac{2}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}\right) = -\frac{g}{8m^3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

This contribution is of order $g$. It should be added to $G_2^{(0)}$.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) - \frac{g}{8m^3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^2)$.

Let's re-evaluate the direct diagrammatic approach for $G_2^c$:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) + \dots$

$G_2^{(0)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$
$= -\frac{g}{8m^3} \int dt_v \, e^{-m(|t_1-t_v| + |t_2-t_v|)}$
$= -\frac{g}{8m^3} \left(\frac{2}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}\right) = -\frac{g}{4m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

Now, consider order $g^2$. Connected diagrams:
1.  **Sunset diagram:** $\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}$ from Trace 1 (if it were a 2-point function). The 1PI 2-point function calculation in Trace 1 had a sunset diagram, but for a quartic oscillator. For a cubic oscillator, the 1PI 2-point function at order $g^2$ would have diagrams involving two $q^3$ vertices. The only topology for the 1PI 2-point function with two $q^3$ vertices is the one calculated as $G_2^{(2)}_{\text{double tadpole}}$ above, but this is NOT 1PI.
    The 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma^{(2)}$ for the cubic oscillator is actually zero. This is a known result for scalar $\phi^3$ theory; the only 1PI diagrams at 2-loop are for $\phi^4$ or higher.
    Let's verify this for the cubic case. A 1PI 2-point diagram must be connected if any single internal line is cut.
    - Two vertices $v_1, v_2$. External legs $t_1, t_2$.
    - Possible connections: $t_1 \to v_1$, $t_2 \to v_2$.
    - From $v_1$: 2 legs remaining. From $v_2$: 2 legs remaining. Total 4 legs.
    - Connect $v_1$ and $v_2$:
        - One propagator $v_1 \leftrightarrow v_2$. Then $t_1 \to v_1 \leftrightarrow v_2 \to t_2$. This is not 1PI - cutting $v_1 \leftrightarrow v_2$ disconnects.
        - Two propagators $v_1 \leftrightarrow v_2$. Then $t_1 \to v_1 \leftrightarrow v_2 \to t_2$. Not 1PI.
        - Three propagators $v_1 \leftrightarrow v_2$. Not possible with 2 legs remaining at each vertex.
    - No connection between $v_1$ and $v_2$: then each vertex has 2 legs. These must connect to $t_1, t_2$ or form loops.
        - $t_1 \to v_1$ and $t_2 \to v_1$. This requires $v_1$ to have degree 2. The remaining leg of $v_1$ must form a loop. This is the tadpole. This is $G_2^{(1)}$.
        - $t_1 \to v_1$ and $t_2 \to v_2$. The remaining legs must form loops. This is the double tadpole.

    So, the only connected diagrams of order $g^2$ are the double tadpole and potentially other non-1PI diagrams.
    The sum of all connected diagrams up to $g^2$ is $G_2^c = G_0 + G_2^{(1)} + G_2^{(2)}$.
    We have $G_2^{(0)}$ and $G_2^{(1)}$.
    The $G_2^{(2)}$ contribution comes from diagrams with two $q^3$ vertices. The only such diagram that is connected is the double tadpole.
    $$G_2^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_{v1} dt_{v2} [G_0(t_1, v_1) G_0(v_1, v_1)] [G_0(t_2, v_2) G_0(v_2, v_2)] = \frac{g^2}{8m^6}.$$

Therefore,
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} - \frac{g}{4m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{8m^6} + O(g^3)$.

Let's re-check the $G_1$ calculation and its relation to the $G_2^{(1)}$ calculation.
$G_1^{(1)}(t) = -g \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
$G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
These are indeed different.

The problem asks for $G_2^c$ modulo $g^3$, meaning up to $g^2$.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^{(0)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{(2)}_{\text{connected}}(t_1, t_2)$.

Integral: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \, e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|)}$.

This is not symmetric for $t_1, t_2$. The formula used before was $\frac{2}{m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$. Where did that come from?

$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|)} dt_v$.
Let $f(t_v) = |t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|$.

$f(t_v) = (t_v-t_1) - (t_2-t_v) = 2t_v - t_1 - t_2$ is wrong.
$f(t_v) = (t_v-t_1) + (t_2-t_v) = t_2-t_1$ for $t_1 \le t_v \le t_2$.

Let's use the standard result for $\int dx e^{-a|x-x_1| - b|x-x_2|}$:
If $a=b=m$, then $\int dx e^{-m|x-x_1|-m|x-x_2|} = \frac{2}{m} e^{-m|x_1-x_2|}$. This assumes $x$ is the integration variable, and $x_1, x_2$ are fixed.

The calculation for $G_2^{(2)}$ (double tadpole) is:
