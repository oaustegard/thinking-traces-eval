The problem asks us to calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ up to order $g^2$ for a quantum particle with Euclidean action:
$$S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right).$$

**Feynman Rules:**
1.  **Free propagator:** $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
2.  **Vertex:** Each $q^3$ interaction vertex (at time $t_v$) contributes a factor of $g$. The $1/3!$ in the action cancels the $3!$ permutations of legs at the vertex.

**Relevant Traces:**
Trace 1 (Etingof Exercise 7.27) is relevant for its general approach to calculating Feynman diagrams, including understanding propagators, vertex factors, and symmetry factors. However, the specific interaction ($q^3$ vs $q^4$) and the quantities requested ($G_1$ and $G_2^c$ vs 1PI $\Sigma$) mean the diagrams and calculations will differ significantly. Trace 2 and Trace 3 are not relevant.

---

**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg at time $t$. We need the leading term in $g$, which is order $g^1$.

**Order $g^0$:**
$\langle q(t) \rangle^{(0)} = 0$, as the free action is symmetric under $q \to -q$.

**Order $g^1$:**
We need one interaction vertex. A $q^3$ vertex has three legs. One leg connects to the external time $t$. The remaining two legs must contract with each other to form a closed loop (a "tadpole" diagram).
*   **Diagram:** A single vertex at time $t_v$ with one external leg to $t$ and two internal legs forming a loop at $t_v$.
*   **Vertex factor:** $g$.
*   **Propagators:** $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ for the loop. The external leg connects directly to the vertex, effectively $\delta(t-t_v)$.
*   **Symmetry factor:** The two internal legs forming the loop are indistinguishable, so $S=2$.
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = g \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int dt_v \, \delta(t - t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{g}{2} G_0(t,t).$$
Using $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m}.$$
This is the leading term for $G_1(t)$.

---

**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs at $t_1$ and $t_2$.

**Order $g^0$:**
This is the free propagator itself.
$$G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

**Order $g^1$:**
One interaction vertex. The $q^3$ vertex has three legs. Two legs connect to the external times $t_1$ and $t_2$. The remaining leg must form a closed loop at the vertex.
*   **Diagram:** A single vertex at $t_v$ with external legs to $t_1, t_2$ and one internal leg forming a loop at $t_v$.
*   **Vertex factor:** $g$.
*   **Propagators:** $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ for the loop. External legs effectively $\delta(t_1-t_v)\delta(t_2-t_v)$.
*   **Symmetry factor:** The two external legs are distinguishable, and the loop is distinct. No internal symmetry, so $S=1$.
$$G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = g \int dt_v \, \delta(t_1 - t_v) \, \delta(t_2 - t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v) = g \, G_0(t_1, t_1) \, \delta(t_1 - t_2).$$
Using $G_0(t_1,t_1) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$$G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{2m} \, \delta(t_1 - t_2).$$

**Order $g^2$:**
Two interaction vertices ($V_1, V_2$), each contributing $g$. Total $g^2$. Each vertex is 3-valent. Total 6 half-edges. Two are external (to $t_1, t_2$), and four are internal. We identify three distinct connected topologies:

1.  **"Eye" diagram:** Two vertices $V_1, V_2$ are connected by two parallel propagators.
    *   **Diagram:** $t_1$ connects to $V_1$, $V_1$ connects to $V_2$ by two propagators, $V_2$ connects to $t_2$.
    *   **Symmetry factor:** The two propagators between $V_1$ and $V_2$ are indistinguishable. So $S=2$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        $$G_2^{c,(2.1)}(t_1, t_2) = g^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, G_0(t_1, t_v) \, [G_0(t_v, t_{v'})]^2 \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_2).$$
        This integral is equivalent to the inverse Fourier transform of $G_0(\omega)^4 = \frac{1}{(\omega^2 + m^2)^4}$.
        The inverse Fourier transform is $\frac{1}{96m^7} (15 + 15m|t| + 6m^2|t|^2 + m^3|t|^3) e^{-m|t|}$.
        $$G_2^{c,(2.1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{192m^7} (15 + 15m|t_1-t_2| + 6m^2|t_1-t_2|^2 + m^3|t_1-t_2|^3) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

2.  **"Double tadpole" diagram:** Two vertices $V_1, V_2$ are connected by a single propagator, and each vertex has a self-loop.
    *   **Diagram:** $t_1$ connects to $V_1$. $V_1$ has a self-loop and connects to $V_2$. $V_2$ has a self-loop and connects to $t_2$.
    *   **Symmetry factor:** The two internal legs forming the loop at $V_1$ are indistinguishable ($2!$). Same for $V_2$ ($2!$). So $S=2! \cdot 2! = 4$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        $$G_2^{c,(2.2)}(t_1, t_2) = g^2 \cdot \frac{1}{4} \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, G_0(t_1, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_{v'}) \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_2).$$
        Using $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
        $$G_2^{c,(2.2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{4} \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right)^2 \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, G_0(t_1, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} (G_0 \star G_0 \star G_0)(t_1, t_2).$$
        The convolution $(G_0 \star G_0 \star G_0)(t_1, t_2)$ is the inverse Fourier transform of $G_0(\omega)^3 = \frac{1}{(\omega^2 + m^2)^3}$.
        The inverse Fourier transform is $\frac{1}{16m^5} (3 + 3m|t| + m^2|t|^2) e^{-m|t|}$.
        $$G_2^{c,(2.2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{256m^7} (3 + 3m|t_1-t_2| + m^2|t_1-t_2|^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

3.  **"Loop on one vertex" diagram:** Both external legs $t_1, t_2$ attach to one vertex $V_1$, which then connects to $V_2$. $V_2$ has a self-loop.
    *   **Diagram:** $t_1$ and $t_2$ connect to $V_1$. $V_1$ connects to $V_2$. $V_2$ has a self-loop.
    *   **Symmetry factor:** The two internal legs forming the loop at $V_2$ are indistinguishable ($2!$). So $S=2$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        $$G_2^{c,(2.3)}(t_1, t_2) = g^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int dt_v dt_{v'} \, \delta(t_1 - t_v) \, \delta(t_2 - t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_{v'}) \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_{v'}).$$
        $$G_2^{c,(2.3)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \, \delta(t_1 - t_2) \int dt_{v'} \, G_0(t_1, t_{v'}) \, G_0(t_{v'}, t_{v'}).$$
        The integral $\int dt_{v'} G_0(t_1, t_{v'}) G_0(t_{v'}, t_{v'})$
        $= \int dt_{v'} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_{v'}|} \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{1}{4m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|t_1 - t_{v'}|} dt_{v'} = \frac{1}{4m^2} \cdot \frac{2}{m} = \frac{1}{2m^3}$.
        $$G_2^{c,(2.3)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \, \delta(t_1 - t_2) \cdot \frac{1}{2m^3} = \frac{g^2}{4m^3} \, \delta(t_1 - t_2).$$

**Total $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $O(g^2)$:**
Summing all contributions:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^{c,(0)} + G_2^{c,(1)} + G_2^{c,(2.1)} + G_2^{c,(2.2)} + G_2^{c,(2.3)} + O(g^3).$$
Combine the $\delta$-function terms:
$$G_2^{c,(1)} + G_2^{c,(2.3)} = \frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_1-t_2) + \frac{g^2}{4m^3} \delta(t_1-t_2) = \left(\frac{g}{2m} + \frac{g^2}{4m^3}\right) \delta(t_1-t_2).$$
Combine the non-local $g^2$ terms:
$$G_2^{c,(2.1)} + G_2^{c,(2.2)} = \frac{g^2}{192m^7} (15 + 15m|t| + 6m^2|t|^2 + m^3|t|^3) e^{-m|t|} + \frac{g^2}{256m^7} (3 + 3m|t| + m^2|t|^2) e^{-m|t|}.$$
Let $t = |t_1-t_2|$ for brevity.
$$= \frac{g^2}{256m^7} \left[ \frac{256}{192}(15 + 15mt + 6m^2t^2 + m^3t^3) + (3 + 3mt + m^2t^2) \right] e^{-mt}.$$
Since $\frac{256}{192} = \frac{4}{3}$:
$$= \frac{g^2}{256m^7} \left[ \frac{4}{3}(15) + \frac{4}{3}(15)mt + \frac{4}{3}(6)m^2t^2 + \frac{4}{3}m^3t^3 + 3 + 3mt + m^2t^2 \right] e^{-mt}.$$
$$= \frac{g^2}{256m^7} \left[ (20+3) + (20+3)mt + (8+1)m^2t^2 + \frac{4}{3}m^3t^3 \right] e^{-mt}.$$
$$= \frac{g^2}{256m^7} \left[ 23 + 23m|t_1-t_2| + 9m^2|t_1-t_2|^2 + \frac{4}{3}m^3|t_1-t_2|^3 \right] e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

The complete connected 2-point function up to $O(g^2)$ is:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \left(\frac{g}{2m} + \frac{g^2}{4m^3}\right) \delta(t_1-t_2) + \frac{g^2}{256m^7} \left( 23 + 23m|t_1-t_2| + 9m^2|t_1-t_2|^2 + \frac{4}{3}m^3|t_1-t_2|^3 \right) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).$$

The final answer is $\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(i) \quad \langle q(t) \rangle &= \frac{g}{4m} + O(g^2). \\
(ii) \quad \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c &= \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \left(\frac{g}{2m} + \frac{g^2}{4m^3}\right) \delta(t_1-t_2) \\
&\quad + \frac{g^2}{256m^7} \left( 23 + 23m|t_1-t_2| + 9m^2|t_1-t_2|^2 + \frac{4}{3}m^3|t_1-t_2|^3 \right) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3).
\end{aligned}
}$
