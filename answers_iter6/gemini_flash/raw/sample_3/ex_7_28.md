The problem asks for the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to order $g^2$ for a quantum particle with potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$.

The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left( \tfrac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.
The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = -\int dt \tfrac{g}{6} q^3$.
The Feynman rules are derived from the expansion $e^{-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar}$. Each cubic vertex carries a factor of $g$ (the $1/3!$ in the action cancels the $3!$ permutations of legs for a basic vertex).

The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

### (b) Reasoning and Calculation

#### (i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$

The 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg at time $t$. The leading term in $g$ means we consider diagrams with a single interaction vertex, i.e., $O(g^1)$.

For a cubic interaction, a single vertex has three legs. One leg is external (connected to $t$). The remaining two legs must contract with each other to form a loop. This is a "tadpole" diagram.

*   **Diagram:** A single vertex at time $t_v$, with one leg connected to $t$ and the other two forming a self-loop.
*   **Vertex Factor:** $g$.
*   **Symmetry Factor:** The two internal legs forming the loop are indistinguishable, so there's a symmetry factor of $1/2$.
*   **Propagators:** $G_0(t, t_v)$ for the external leg, and $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ for the internal loop.

The contribution is:
$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v)$.

We know $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v - t_v|} = \frac{1}{2m}$.
The integral $\int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v)$ evaluates to:
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v \, \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|} = \frac{1}{2m} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} du \, e^{-m|u|} = \frac{1}{2m} \left( \int_{-\infty}^{0} e^{mu} du + \int_{0}^{\infty} e^{-mu} du \right) = \frac{1}{2m} \left( \frac{1}{m} + \frac{1}{m} \right) = \frac{1}{m^2}$.

Substituting these values:
$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{m^2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.

This is a constant, as expected for a translationally invariant system in equilibrium.

#### (ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs at $t_1, t_2$.

**Order $g^0$ (free theory):**
This is the free propagator:
$G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

**Order $g^1$:**
A single cubic vertex has three legs. Two are external (to $t_1, t_2$). The third leg must form a self-loop. This is a "tadpole insertion" on the propagator.

*   **Diagram:** A vertex at $t_v$, with legs going to $t_1$ and $t_2$, and a self-loop.
*   **Vertex Factor:** $g$.
*   **Symmetry Factor:** The self-loop has a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry, so $1/2$.
*   **Propagators:** $G_0(t_1, t_v)$, $G_0(t_v, t_2)$, and $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.

The contribution is:
$G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_2) \, G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
Again, $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
The integral $\int dt_v \, G_0(t_1, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_2)$ is a standard convolution. In frequency space, this corresponds to $\tilde{G}_0(\omega)^2 = (\omega^2+m^2)^{-2}$. Its inverse Fourier transform is $\frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

So, $G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} = \frac{g}{16m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Order $g^2$:**
For order $g^2$, we need diagrams with two cubic vertices. Each vertex contributes $g$.
There are two types of connected diagrams:

1.  **Bubble Diagram (1PI self-energy insertion):**
    This is a "bubble" or "double-line" diagram connecting the two external legs. It is 1PI.
    *   **Diagram:** Two vertices at $t_a, t_b$. Each connects to an external leg ($t_1$ or $t_2$) and the other vertex via two internal propagators.
    *   **Vertex Factors:** $g^2$.
    *   **Symmetry Factor:** The two propagators connecting $t_a$ and $t_b$ are indistinguishable, so $1/2$.
    *   **Amputated form (1PI self-energy $\Sigma^{(2)}$):** The 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b)$ is the sum of all 1PI graphs with two amputated external legs.
        For two vertices, this is simply two propagators connecting $t_a$ and $t_b$.
        $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b) = \frac{g^2}{2} [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2 = \frac{g^2}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_a - t_b|} \right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_a - t_b|}$.
    *   **Contribution to $G_2^c$:** This 1PI insertion is dressed by free propagators:
        $G_2^{c,(2,\text{bubble})}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, \Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b) \, G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        $G_2^{c,(2,\text{bubble})}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, e^{-2m|t_a - t_b|} \, G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        This is a convolution $G_0 \star (\text{exp-decay}) \star G_0$. In frequency space, this corresponds to $\tilde{G}_0(\omega) \left(\frac{2(2m)}{\omega^2+(2m)^2}\right) \tilde{G}_0(\omega) = \frac{4m}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+4m^2)}$.
        The inverse Fourier transform of $\frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+4m^2)}$ is $\frac{1}{36m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right]$.
        Therefore, $G_2^{c,(2,\text{bubble})}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{36m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} - e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right]$.
        Checking the general formula for $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+k^2)} \right]$:
        $\frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2(\omega^2+k^2)} = \frac{1}{(k^2-m^2)^2} \frac{1}{\omega^2+k^2} + \frac{1}{k^2-m^2} \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2} - \frac{1}{(k^2-m^2)^2} \frac{1}{\omega^2+m^2}$.
        For $k=2m$: $\frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{1}{\omega^2+4m^2} + \frac{1}{3m^2} \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2} - \frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{1}{\omega^2+m^2}$.
        Inverse Fourier transform gives:
        $\frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{1}{4m} e^{-2m|t|} + \frac{1}{3m^2} \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t|) e^{-m|t|} - \frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t|}$
        $= \frac{1}{36m^5} e^{-2m|t|} + \frac{1}{12m^5} (1+m|t|) e^{-m|t|} - \frac{1}{18m^5} e^{-m|t|}$
        $= \frac{1}{36m^5} \left[ e^{-2m|t|} + 3(1+m|t|) e^{-m|t|} - 2 e^{-m|t|} \right]$
        $= \frac{1}{36m^5} \left[ (1+3m|t|) e^{-m|t|} + e^{-2m|t|} \right]$.
        So, $G_2^{c,(2,\text{bubble})}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{36m^5} \left[ (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right]$
        $= \frac{g^2}{72m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right]$.
        (There was a factor of $1/2$ difference in my thought process, corrected now)

2.  **Double Tadpole Diagram (two $O(g^1)$ insertions):**
    This diagram consists of two separate tadpole insertions along the propagation path.
    *   **Diagram:** $t_1 \to t_a \text{ (with loop)} \to t_b \text{ (with loop)} \to t_2$.
    *   **Contribution:** Each tadpole insertion at $t_a$ (or $t_b$) contributes $\frac{g}{2} G_0(t_a, t_a) = \frac{g}{4m}$. Let $M_1 = \frac{g}{4m}$.
        $G_2^{c,(2,\text{double tadpole})}(t_1, t_2) = M_1^2 \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, G_0(t_a, t_b) \, G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        The integral $\int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, G_0(t_a, t_b) \, G_0(t_b, t_2)$ is the inverse Fourier transform of $\tilde{G}_0(\omega)^3 = (\omega^2+m^2)^{-3}$.
        This evaluates to $\frac{1}{16m^5} (3+3m|t_1-t_2|+m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
    *   **Total:**
        $G_2^{c,(2,\text{double tadpole})}(t_1, t_2) = \left(\frac{g}{4m}\right)^2 \frac{1}{16m^5} (3+3m|t_1-t_2|+m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \frac{1}{16m^5} (3+3m|t_1-t_2|+m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{256m^7} (3+3m|t_1-t_2|+m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Total $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $O(g^2)$:**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(2,\text{bubble})}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(2,\text{double tadpole})}(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3)$.

The final expression is the sum of these four terms.

### Final Answer

The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$:**
The leading term is of order $g^1$, from the single-vertex tadpole diagram:
$$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2).$$

**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$:**
The connected 2-point function is the sum of diagrams:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3),$$
where $G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of the bubble and double tadpole diagrams.
Explicitly:
*   **Order $g^0$ (free propagator):**
    $$G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$
*   **Order $g^1$ (single tadpole insertion):**
    $$G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{16m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$
*   **Order $g^2$ (bubble diagram):**
    $$G_2^{c,(2,\text{bubble})}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{72m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right].$$
*   **Order $g^2$ (double tadpole diagram):**
    $$G_2^{c,(2,\text{double tadpole})}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{256m^7} (3+3m|t_1-t_2|+m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

Combining these terms:
$$\boxed{\begin{aligned} G_1(t) &= \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2). \\ G_2^c(t_1, t_2) &= \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} \\ &+ \frac{g}{16m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} \\ &+ \frac{g^2}{72m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right] \\ &+ \frac{g^2}{256m^7} (3+3m|t_1-t_2|+m^2(t_1-t_2)^2) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3). \end{aligned}}$$
