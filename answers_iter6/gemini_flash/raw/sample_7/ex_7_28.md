The problem asks for the leading term of the 1-point function and the connected 2-point function up to order $g^2$ for a quantum particle with Euclidean action $S[q] = \int dt \left(\frac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.

**Part (b): Solution to the main problem**

**Feynman Rules for the cubic oscillator:**
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left(\frac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.
1.  **Free Propagator:** $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2)\rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
    Note: $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$. In frequency space, $G_0(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2}$.
2.  **Vertex:** The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = \int dt \left(- \frac{g}{6} q^3\right)$. In the path integral expansion $e^{-S_{\text{int}}}$, each 3-valent vertex at time $t_v$ contributes a factor of $+g$. (The $1/3!$ cancels the $3!$ permutations of legs.)
3.  **Symmetry Factors:** For each diagram, divide by the number of permutations of internal lines and vertices that leave the diagram unchanged.

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg at time $t$.
*   **Order $g^0$:** There are no diagrams with an external leg and no vertices. So $G_1^{(0)}(t) = 0$.
*   **Order $g^1$ (leading term):** We need one vertex. The $q^3$ vertex has three legs. One leg is connected to the external line at $t$. The remaining two legs must connect to each other to form a closed loop (a "tadpole").
    *   **Diagram:** A 3-valent vertex at time $t_v$, with one leg connected to $t$ and the other two forming a loop at $t_v$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        *   Vertex factor: $+g$.
        *   Propagator for the loop: $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
        *   External leg connection: $\delta(t - t_v)$.
        *   Symmetry factor: The two internal legs forming the loop are indistinguishable, so $1/2! = 1/2$.
        *   Integration over vertex time $t_v$.
    $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \int dt_v \, (+g) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \delta(t - t_v) \cdot G_0(t_v, t_v)$$
    $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \, G_0(t, t).$$
    Substitute $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
    $$G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m}.$$
    This is constant in time, as expected for this simple tadpole diagram. This is the leading (lowest non-zero order) term.

**(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ (i.e., up to and including order $g^2$).**

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs at $t_1$ and $t_2$. It can be expressed via the Dyson series: $G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 + \ldots$, where $\Sigma$ is the 1PI 2-point function (self-energy).

*   **Order $g^0$:** This is the free propagator, which is a connected diagram with two external legs.
    $$G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}.$$

*   **Order $g^1$:** We need one vertex. A 3-valent vertex has three legs. If two are connected to external lines at $t_1, t_2$, there is one remaining internal leg. This single leg cannot form a closed loop. Therefore, there are no connected 2-point diagrams at order $g^1$.
    $$G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = 0.$$

*   **Order $g^2$:** We need two vertices. Each vertex is 3-valent, so there are a total of $2 \times 3 = 6$ half-edges. Two are external (to $t_1, t_2$), leaving 4 half-edges for internal lines.
    The only 1PI diagram at $O(g^2)$ is the "bubble" diagram. This diagram consists of two vertices ($V_A, V_B$) connected by *two* internal propagators. The external legs are attached to $V_A$ and $V_B$.
    First, let's find the 1PI self-energy $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B)$. It's the amputated bubble diagram:
    *   **Diagram for $\Sigma^{(2)}$:** $V_A \rightrightarrows V_B$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        *   Two vertex factors: $(+g) \cdot (+g) = g^2$.
        *   Two propagators between $t_A$ and $t_B$: $G_0(t_A, t_B)^2$.
        *   Symmetry factor: The two internal propagators between $V_A$ and $V_B$ are indistinguishable, so $1/2! = 1/2$.
    $$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B) = \frac{g^2}{2} G_0(t_A, t_B)^2.$$
    To evaluate this, we use Fourier transforms. In frequency space, $G_0(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2}$.
    $$\Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) = \frac{g^2}{2} \int \frac{d\nu}{2\pi} G_0(\nu)^2 = \frac{g^2}{2} \int \frac{d\nu}{2\pi} \frac{1}{(\nu^2 + m^2)^2}.$$
    The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{d\nu}{(\nu^2 + m^2)^2} = \frac{\pi}{2m^3}$.
    So, $\int \frac{d\nu}{2\pi} \frac{1}{(\nu^2 + m^2)^2} = \frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot \frac{\pi}{2m^3} = \frac{1}{4m^3}$.
    Thus, $\Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) = \frac{g^2}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{4m^3} = \frac{g^2}{8m^3}$.
    Since $\Sigma^{(2)}(\omega)$ is a constant, its inverse Fourier transform is a delta function in time:
    $$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B) = \frac{g^2}{8m^3} \delta(t_A - t_B).$$
    Now, we insert this into the Dyson series term $G_0 \star \Sigma^{(2)} \star G_0$:
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_A dt_B \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, \Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B) \, G_0(t_B, t_2)$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_A dt_B \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \left(\frac{g^2}{8m^3} \delta(t_A - t_B)\right) G_0(t_B, t_2)$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{8m^3} \int dt_A \, G_0(t_1, t_A) G_0(t_A, t_2).$$
    The integral $\int dt_A \, G_0(t_1, t_A) G_0(t_A, t_2)$ is the convolution $(G_0 \star G_0)(t_1, t_2)$.
    In frequency space, this corresponds to $G_0(\omega)^2 = \frac{1}{(\omega^2 + m^2)^2}$. The inverse Fourier transform is:
    $$(G_0 \star G_0)(t_1, t_2) = \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{i\omega(t_1-t_2)}}{(\omega^2 + m^2)^2} = \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$
    Substituting this back:
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{8m^3} \cdot \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$$
    $$G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{32m^6} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}.$$

Combining all terms up to $O(g^2)$:
$$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3).$$

The final answer is:
**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function:**
$$\boxed{\;G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} + O(g^2)\;}$$

**(ii) Connected 2-point function modulo $g^3$:**
$$\boxed{\;G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{32m^6} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3)\;}$$
