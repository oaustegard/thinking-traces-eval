The problem asks for two things:
1.  The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for a given action $S(x)$.
2.  The number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with only 1-valent and 3-valent vertices.

### Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$

**Relevant Trace:** Trace 2 (Etingof Exercise 3.26) is relevant because it discusses the 1-loop contribution to effective action and partition functions in zero-dimensional QFT.

**Differences from Trace 2:**
*   The action $S(x)$ is different. Here, $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{6})$. In Trace 2, $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$.
*   The problem specifically asks for $\log(Z/Z_0)$, where $Z_0$ is the free partition function, not the effective action $S_1(x_c)$.

**Reasoning:**
The partition function in zero dimensions is $Z = \int e^{-S(x)/\hbar} dx$.
The free partition function $Z_0$ corresponds to $g=0$, so $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$.
Thus, $Z_0 = \int e^{-x^2/(2\hbar)} dx = \sqrt{2\pi\hbar/1} = \sqrt{2\pi\hbar}$.

The given action is $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6} = \tfrac{1-2g}{2} x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$.
For a general action $S(x)$, the 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is obtained by evaluating the Gaussian integral around the classical saddle point $x_c$.
The saddle point $x_c$ satisfies $S'(x_c) = 0$.
$S'(x) = (1-2g)x - \tfrac{g x^2}{2}$. Setting $S'(x)=0$ gives $x((1-2g) - \tfrac{g x}{2}) = 0$.
Assuming $g$ is small, the relevant saddle point is $x_c = 0$.
At $x_c=0$:
$S(0) = 0$.
The second derivative is $S''(x) = (1-2g) - gx$. So, $S''(0) = 1-2g$.

The 1-loop approximation for $Z$ (valid for small $\hbar$) is $Z \approx e^{-S(x_c)/\hbar} \sqrt{2\pi\hbar/S''(x_c)}$.
Substituting $x_c=0$:
$Z \approx e^0 \sqrt{2\pi\hbar/(1-2g)} = \sqrt{2\pi\hbar/(1-2g)}$.

Therefore, $\log Z = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)$.
And $\log Z_0 = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1) = \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar)$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \log Z - \log Z_0 = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)\right) - \tfrac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar)$.
$$ \boxed{\log(Z/Z_0) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g).} $$

### Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only

**Relevant Trace:** Trace 1 (Etingof Exercise 3.25) discusses counting labeled trees with specific valences, which involves combinatorial methods for labeled graphs. Trace 2 briefly touches on 1-loop diagrams, but for unlabeled graphs in the context of effective action.

**Differences from Trace 1:**
*   Trace 1 counts trees (0-loop graphs) with 1-valent and 4-valent vertices. This problem counts 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices.
*   The method for finding $a_n$ will rely on basic graph theory and combinatorics, adapted for 1-loop graphs.

**Reasoning:**
Let $n_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $n_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
The total number of vertices is $n = n_1 + n_3$.
For a connected graph with $V$ vertices and $E$ edges, the number of loops is $L = E - V + 1$.
We are looking for 1-loop graphs, so $L=1$, which implies $E = V = n$.
The sum of degrees in any graph is $2E$.
Here, $\sum_{v} \deg(v) = n_1 \cdot 1 + n_3 \cdot 3$.
So, $n_1 + 3n_3 = 2E = 2n$.
Substitute $n = n_1 + n_3$:
$n_1 + 3n_3 = 2(n_1 + n_3)$
$n_1 + 3n_3 = 2n_1 + 2n_3$
$n_3 = n_1$.
This means that for such a graph to exist, the number of 1-valent vertices must equal the number of 3-valent vertices. Let $n_1 = n_3 = k$.
Then the total number of vertices is $n = n_1 + n_3 = k+k = 2k$.
Thus, $n$ must be an even number. If $n$ is odd, $a_n=0$.

The structure of such a graph is a cycle formed by the $k$ 3-valent vertices, with each 3-valent vertex having one 1-valent vertex (a "leaf") attached to it.

Let $a_n$ be the number of such labeled graphs on $n$ vertices.
We need to consider three cases for $k$ (which is $n/2$):

**Case 1: $k=1$ (i.e., $n=2$)**
We have one 3-valent vertex and one 1-valent vertex.
There are $\binom{2}{1}=2$ ways to choose which labeled vertex (from $\{1,2\}$) is 3-valent. The other is then 1-valent.
Let vertex $i$ be 3-valent and vertex $j$ be 1-valent.
The 3-valent vertex $i$ must have a self-loop (contributing 2 to its degree) and an edge to the 1-valent vertex $j$ (contributing 1 to its degree).
The edges are $\{(i,i), (i,j)\}$. This uniquely defines the graph structure for fixed $i,j$.
So, $a_2 = 2$.

**Case 2: $k=2$ (i.e., $n=4$)**
We have two 3-valent vertices and two 1-valent vertices.
There are $\binom{4}{2}=6$ ways to choose which two labeled vertices (from $\{1,2,3,4\}$) are 3-valent. Let these be $i,j$. The remaining two, $p,q$, are 1-valent.
The two 3-valent vertices $i,j$ must be connected by a double edge (contributing 2 to each of their degrees).
Each of $i,j$ needs one more edge to reach degree 3. These must connect to the 1-valent vertices $p,q$.
There are $2! = 2$ ways to pair the 3-valent vertices with the 1-valent vertices (i.e., $i$ connects to $p$ and $j$ to $q$, OR $i$ connects to $q$ and $j$ to $p$).
So, $a_4 = \binom{4}{2} \times 2 = 6 \times 2 = 12$.

**Case 3: $k \ge 3$ (i.e., $n=2k$ with $k \ge 3$)**
We have $k$ 3-valent vertices and $k$ 1-valent vertices.
There are $\binom{2k}{k}$ ways to choose the $k$ labeled vertices that are 3-valent. Let this set be $V_3$. The remaining $k$ vertices form the set $V_1$ of 1-valent vertices.
The $k$ vertices in $V_3$ must form a simple cycle (each contributing 2 to its degree). The number of ways to form a labeled cycle on $k$ specific vertices is $\frac{(k-1)!}{2}$.
Each of the $k$ vertices in $V_3$ needs one more edge to reach degree 3. These edges must connect to the $k$ vertices in $V_1$.
Since all vertices in $V_1$ are distinct and must be connected to distinct vertices in $V_3$ (to maintain 1-valence), there are $k!$ ways to pair the vertices in $V_3$ with the vertices in $V_1$.
So, $a_{2k} = \binom{2k}{k} \times \frac{(k-1)!}{2} \times k!$.
$a_{2k} = \frac{(2k)!}{k!k!} \times \frac{(k-1)!}{2} \times k! = \frac{(2k)! \cdot (k-1)! \cdot k!}{k!k! \cdot 2} = \frac{(2k)!}{2k}$.

**Summary of $a_n$:**
*   If $n$ is odd, $a_n = 0$.
*   If $n$ is even, let $n=2k$:
    *   For $k=1$ ($n=2$), $a_2 = 2$.
    *   For $k=2$ ($n=4$), $a_4 = 12$.
    *   For $k \ge 3$ ($n \ge 6$), $a_{2k} = \frac{(2k)!}{2k}$.

This can be expressed compactly by defining a modified cycle factor:
Let $C_k^{\text{labeled}}$ be the number of ways to form a 1-loop structure on $k$ labeled vertices such that each vertex has degree 2 within the structure.
$C_1^{\text{labeled}} = 1$ (a single vertex with a self-loop).
$C_2^{\text{labeled}} = 1$ (two vertices with a double edge).
$C_k^{\text{labeled}} = \frac{(k-1)!}{2}$ for $k \ge 3$.

Then the number of labeled graphs is $a_{2k} = \binom{2k}{k} \times C_k^{\text{labeled}} \times k!$.

The final answer for the number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only:
*   If $n$ is odd, $a_n = 0$.
*   If $n$ is even, let $n=2k$:
    *   $a_2 = 2$
    *   $a_4 = 12$
    *   $a_{2k} = \frac{(2k)!}{2k}$ for $k \ge 3$.
