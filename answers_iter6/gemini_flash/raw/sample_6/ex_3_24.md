**Part (a): 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

The problem asks for the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for the action $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{6})$.

**Relevant Trace:** Trace 2 (Etingof Exercise 3.26) deals with the 1-loop effective action and the 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$.

**Differences from Trace 2:**
1.  Trace 2 considers the effective action $S_{\text{eff}}(x_c)$, while the current problem asks for $\log(Z/Z_0)$, where $Z$ is the partition function.
2.  The interaction term in Trace 2 is only a cubic term ($-\tfrac{g x^3}{6}$), while here we have both a quadratic and a cubic interaction term ($ -g x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$).

**Reasoning:**
In zero-dimensional quantum field theory, the partition function is $Z = \int dx e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$.
The 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is given by the leading-order saddle-point approximation to the integral.
For an action $S(x)$, the exact result for $\log Z$ is $\tfrac12 \log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac12 \log S''(x_0) - S(x_0)/\hbar$, where $x_0$ is the classical solution (minimum of $S(x)$) satisfying $S'(x_0)=0$.
The free partition function $Z_0$ corresponds to $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$, so $S_0'(x)=x$, $S_0''(x)=1$. Thus $x_0=0$ for the free theory, and $\log Z_0 = \tfrac12 \log(2\pi\hbar)$.

For the given action $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$, we can rewrite it as:
$S(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}(1-2g)x^2 - \tfrac{g}{6}x^3$.

First, find the classical solution $x_0$ by setting $S'(x)=0$:
$S'(x) = (1-2g)x - \tfrac{g}{2}x^2 = x\left(1-2g - \tfrac{g}{2}x\right)$.
The solutions are $x_0 = 0$ or $x_0 = \tfrac{2(1-2g)}{g}$.
To determine which is a minimum, we evaluate $S''(x)$:
$S''(x) = (1-2g) - gx$.
At $x_0=0$: $S''(0) = 1-2g$. For $x_0=0$ to be a minimum, we must have $1-2g > 0$.
At $x_0=\tfrac{2(1-2g)}{g}$: $S''\left(\tfrac{2(1-2g)}{g}\right) = (1-2g) - g\left(\tfrac{2(1-2g)}{g}\right) = (1-2g) - 2(1-2g) = -(1-2g)$. If $1-2g>0$, this is a maximum.
So, assuming $1-2g>0$, the relevant classical solution is $x_0=0$.

At $x_0=0$, $S(0)=0$.
Therefore, $\log Z = \tfrac12 \log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac12 \log S''(0) - S(0)/\hbar = \tfrac12 \log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac12 \log(1-2g)$.
Then, $\log(Z/Z_0) = \log Z - \log Z_0 = \left(\tfrac12 \log(2\pi\hbar) - \tfrac12 \log(1-2g)\right) - \tfrac12 \log(2\pi\hbar)$.
$$ \boxed{\log(Z/Z_0) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)} $$

**Part (b): Number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only**

The problem asks for the number $a_n$ of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with only 1-valent and 3-valent vertices. It also asks to "use this" (the result from part (a)) and to check for small $n$.

**Relevant Traces:**
*   Trace 1 (Etingof Exercise 3.25) provides context for exponential generating functions (EGFs) for labeled trees and mentions the dissymmetry theorem.
*   Trace 2 (Etingof Exercise 3.26) discusses 1-loop diagrams, specifically the "necklace" type, which are relevant here.

**Differences from Traces:**
*   This is a direct combinatorial enumeration problem for labeled graphs, not a calculation of an EGF or effective action from a specific action.
*   The vertex types (1-valent and 3-valent) are specified for the graphs, which might differ from the vertex types implied by the action in part (a).

**Reasoning:**
Let $V_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $V_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
Total number of vertices $n = V_1 + V_3$.
For a connected graph with $V$ vertices and $E$ edges to be 1-loop, we must have $E = V$ (Euler characteristic $V-E+1=1$).
The sum of degrees is $1 \cdot V_1 + 3 \cdot V_3 = 2E$.
Substituting $E=V=n$:
$V_1 + 3V_3 = 2(V_1 + V_3)$
$V_1 + 3V_3 = 2V_1 + 2V_3$
$V_3 = V_1$.
This implies that the number of 1-valent vertices must equal the number of 3-valent vertices. So $n = V_1 + V_3 = 2V_1 = 2V_3$. Thus, $n$ must be an even number. Let $n=2k$, so $V_1=k$ and $V_3=k$.

The structure of such a graph must be a cycle of $k$ 3-valent vertices, where each 3-valent vertex has one edge connecting to a 1-valent vertex (a leaf). The remaining two edges of each 3-valent vertex form the cycle. These are the "necklace" graphs from Trace 2, where the external legs are explicitly drawn as 1-valent vertices.

We want to find $a_n$, the number of such labeled graphs. $a_n=0$ if $n$ is odd.
For $n=2k$:
1.  **Choose $k$ vertices to be 3-valent:** From $n=2k$ labeled vertices, we choose $k$ of them to be 3-valent. The remaining $k$ vertices will be 1-valent. There are $\binom{2k}{k}$ ways.
2.  **Connect 1-valent vertices to 3-valent vertices:** Each of the $k$ 1-valent vertices must connect to exactly one of the $k$ 3-valent vertices. There are $k!$ ways to form these pairings. (Each 3-valent vertex now has two remaining half-edges).
3.  **Form a cycle with the remaining half-edges:** We have $k$ labeled 3-valent vertices, each with two available "half-edges" (or "ports"). These $2k$ half-edges must be connected to form a single cycle.
    *   For $k=1$ ($n=2$): One 3-valent vertex ($v_1$), one 1-valent vertex ($v_2$). $v_2$ connects to $v_1$. The two remaining half-edges of $v_1$ must form a self-loop. There is 1 way to do this.
    *   For $k \ge 2$: We need to form a cycle on $k$ labeled vertices, where each vertex has two specific half-edges. The number of ways to form an undirected cycle on $k$ labeled vertices (where edges are chosen from these specific half-edges) is $2^{k-2}(k-1)!$. This factor accounts for the choices of which half-edge connects to which neighbor in the cycle.

Combining these factors, the number of labeled graphs $a_{2k}$ is:
$$a_{2k} = \binom{2k}{k} \cdot k! \cdot C_k$$
where $C_k = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } k=1 \\ 2^{k-2}(k-1)! & \text{if } k \ge 2 \end{cases}$.

Let's calculate for small $n$:
*   **For $n=2$ ($k=1$):**
    $a_2 = \binom{2}{1} \cdot 1! \cdot 1 = 2 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 = 2$.
    *Check:* Let vertices be $\{1,2\}$. If 1 is 3-valent, 2 is 1-valent: 2 connects to 1. 1's remaining half-edges form a self-loop. (1 graph). If 2 is 3-valent, 1 is 1-valent: 1 connects to 2. 2's remaining half-edges form a self-loop. (1 graph). Total $2$ graphs.

*   **For $n=4$ ($k=2$):**
    $a_4 = \binom{4}{2} \cdot 2! \cdot 2^{2-2}(2-1)! = 6 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 = 12$.
    *Check:* Choose two 3-valent vertices (e.g., $\{1,2\}$). The others ($\{3,4\}$) are 1-valent. Pair the 1-valent with 3-valent (e.g., 3-1, 4-2 or 3-2, 4-1; 2 ways). The remaining two half-edges from 1 and two from 2 form a double edge between 1 and 2 (1 way). $\binom{4}{2} \cdot 2! \cdot 1 = 6 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 = 12$.

*   **For $n=6$ ($k=3$):**
    $a_6 = \binom{6}{3} \cdot 3! \cdot 2^{3-2}(3-1)! = 20 \cdot 6 \cdot 2^1 \cdot 2! = 120 \cdot 2 \cdot 2 = 480$.

The result from part (a) is $-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(2g)^k}{2k}$. This is the generating function for *unlabeled* 1-loop graphs where a cycle of $k$ vertices has weight $(2g)^k$ and symmetry factor $2k$. It does not directly provide $a_n$ for *labeled* graphs. However, the connection is that both refer to 1-loop structures. The question "use this" likely refers to the type of structure (cycles) implied by the log form, and the fact that $n$ must be even.

$$ \boxed{a_n = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \\ \binom{n}{n/2} \cdot (n/2)! \cdot C_{n/2} & \text{if } n \text{ is even, where } C_k = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } k=1 \\ 2^{k-2}(k-1)! & \text{if } k \ge 2 \end{cases} \end{cases} } $$
For small $n$:
$a_2 = 2$
$a_4 = 12$
$a_6 = 480$
