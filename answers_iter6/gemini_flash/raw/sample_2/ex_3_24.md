The problem asks for two distinct calculations:
1.  The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for a given action $S(x)$.
2.  The number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices.

Let's address each part.

---

### Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$

**Relevant Trace:**
Trace 2: Etingof Exercise 3.26 (1-loop effective action) is highly relevant. It discusses 1-loop contributions in zero-dimensional QFT and the interpretation of Feynman diagrams for such calculations.

**Differences from Trace 2:**
1.  **Action:** Trace 2 used $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$. The main problem uses $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{6})$. The new action includes an additional $x^2$ interaction term.
2.  **Quantity:** Trace 2 computed $S_1(x_c)$ (1-loop effective action). The main problem asks for $\log(Z/Z_0)$ (1-loop partition function).

**Reasoning:**

The partition function is $Z = \int e^{-S(x)/\hbar} dx$. The free partition function $Z_0$ is for $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$. We are interested in the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$.

The action is $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$.
We can write $S(x) = S_0(x) + S_{\text{int}}(x)$, where $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$ and $S_{\text{int}}(x) = -g x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is given by the sum of all connected 1-loop vacuum Feynman diagrams, with each diagram weighted by $1/|\text{Aut}(\Gamma)|$. The Feynman rules are derived from $e^{-S/\hbar} = e^{-S_0/\hbar} e^{-S_{\text{int}}/\hbar}$.
Since $S_{\text{int}}(x) = -g x^2 - \tfrac{g x^3}{6}$, the interaction term in the exponent is $+g x^2/\hbar + g x^3/(6\hbar)$.
This gives the following Feynman rules:
*   **Propagator:** From the $S_0(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2}$ term, the bare propagator is $1$.
*   **Vertices:**
    *   A 2-valent vertex from the $g x^2$ term, with factor $g$.
    *   A 3-valent vertex from the $g x^3/6$ term, with factor $g$.

We need to find all connected vacuum diagrams with exactly one loop ($L=1$). Let $V_2$ be the number of 2-valent vertices and $V_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices. Let $V = V_2 + V_3$ be the total number of vertices and $E$ be the total number of internal edges.
For a connected graph, the loop number is $L = E - V + 1$.
For vacuum diagrams, all edges are internal, and the sum of degrees is $2E$. So $2V_2 + 3V_3 = 2E$.

Substituting $E = V + L - 1 = V_2 + V_3 + 1 - 1 = V_2 + V_3$ (since $L=1$):
$2V_2 + 3V_3 = 2(V_2 + V_3)$
$2V_2 + 3V_3 = 2V_2 + 2V_3$
$V_3 = 0$.

This critical result implies that any 1-loop vacuum diagram must consist *only* of 2-valent vertices. No 3-valent vertices can be part of a 1-loop vacuum diagram in this theory.
Such a diagram is a simple cycle of $k$ 2-valent vertices.
*   For a cycle of $k$ 2-valent vertices: $V_2 = k$, $V_3 = 0$.
*   The value of such a diagram is the product of $k$ vertex factors ($g^k$) and $k$ propagator factors ($1^k$), so $g^k$.
*   The automorphism factor for a labeled cycle of $k$ vertices is $2k$ (for $k \ge 1$).

Summing over all possible lengths $k$ for the cycle:
$\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{g^k}{2k}$.
This is a known series expansion: $\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{x^k}{k} = -\log(1-x)$.
So, $\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{g^k}{2k} = -\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{g^k}{k} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-g)$.

### Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only

**Relevant Trace:**
Trace 1: Etingof Exercise 3.25 (EGF for 1/4-valent labeled trees) is relevant for understanding combinatorial enumeration of labeled graphs and the use of graph properties. However, the specific type of graph (trees vs. 1-loop, valences) differs.

**Differences from Trace 1:**
1.  **Graph type:** The problem asks for 1-loop graphs, not trees.
2.  **Valences:** The problem specifies 1-valent and 3-valent vertices, not 1-valent and 4-valent.

**Reasoning:**

Let $V_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $V_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
The total number of vertices is $n = V_1 + V_3$.
For a connected graph, the loop number is $L = E - V + 1$. We are given $L=1$.
So, $E = V_1 + V_3$.

The sum of degrees in any graph is $2E$.
$1 \cdot V_1 + 3 \cdot V_3 = 2E$.
Substituting $E = V_1 + V_3$:
$V_1 + 3V_3 = 2(V_1 + V_3)$
$V_1 + 3V_3 = 2V_1 + 2V_3$
$V_3 = V_1$.

This means that in any such graph, the number of 1-valent vertices must equal the number of 3-valent vertices.
Let $k = V_1 = V_3$. Then the total number of vertices $n = k + k = 2k$.
Therefore, $n$ must be an even number. If $n$ is odd, the number of such graphs is 0.

Now we need to construct such a graph: it has $k$ 1-valent vertices and $k$ 3-valent vertices. It has exactly one loop.
The $k$ 1-valent vertices must be leaves, each attached to a 3-valent vertex.
The only way to form a 1-loop graph with this structure is to have the $k$ 3-valent vertices form a cycle, and each 3-valent vertex in the cycle has its third edge connected to a unique 1-valent vertex.

*   **Case $k=1$:** $n=2$. One 1-valent and one 3-valent vertex. This is a single edge, which is a tree ($L=0$), not a 1-loop graph. So $k \ge 2$.
*   **Case $k=2$:** $n=4$. Two 1-valent and two 3-valent vertices. The two 3-valent vertices must form a cycle, meaning they are connected by two edges. Each 3-valent vertex then connects to one 1-valent vertex.
    *   Number of ways to choose 2 vertices out of $n=4$ to be 3-valent: $\binom{4}{2}$. The other 2 are 1-valent.
    *   The two 3-valent vertices are connected by two edges. For labeled vertices, there's only 1 way to form this pair of double edges.
    *   The two 1-valent vertices must be attached to the two 3-valent vertices. There are $2!$ ways to assign these.
    *   Total for $n=4$: $\binom{4}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot 2! = 6 \cdot 2 = 12$.
*   **Case $k \ge 3$:** $n=2k$. We have $k$ 3-valent vertices and $k$ 1-valent vertices.
    *   Choose $k$ labels for the 3-valent vertices out of $n$: $\binom{n}{k}$. The remaining $k$ labels are for the 1-valent vertices.
    *   Form a labeled cycle with the $k$ chosen 3-valent vertices. There are $(k-1)!$ ways to do this (for labeled vertices, orientation matters for the cycle).
    *   Assign the $k$ 1-valent vertices to the $k$ 3-valent vertices (one to each). There are $k!$ ways to do this.
    *   Total for $n=2k, k \ge 3$: $\binom{n}{k} (k-1)! k!$.
    *   This formula also works for $k=2$: $\binom{4}{2} (2-1)! 2! = 6 \cdot 1 \cdot 2 = 12$.

Combining these, for $n=2k$ where $k \ge 2$, the number of such labeled graphs is:
$a_n = \binom{n}{k} (k-1)! k! = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} (k-1)! k! = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} (k-1)!$.
Since $n=2k$, we have $n-k = k$.
$a_n = \frac{n!}{k!} (k-1)! = \frac{n!}{k}$.

**Check for small numbers of vertices:**
*   **$n=2$:** $k=1$. Our analysis above showed $k \ge 2$ for 1-loop graphs. So $a_2 = 0$.
*   **$n=4$:** $k=2$. Using the formula $a_4 = \frac{4!}{2} = \frac{24}{2} = 12$. This matches our manual calculation.
*   **$n=6$:** $k=3$. Using the formula $a_6 = \frac{6!}{3} = \frac{720}{3} = 240$.
    *   Manual check: $\binom{6}{3}=20$ ways to choose 3-valent vertices. $(3-1)!=2$ ways to form a cycle with these. $3!=6$ ways to attach the 1-valent vertices. $20 \cdot 2 \cdot 6 = 240$. This matches.

### Final Answer

**Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**
For the action $S(x) = \tfrac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \tfrac{x^3}{6})$, the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is the sum of all connected 1-loop vacuum Feynman diagrams. Using the bare propagator (from $x^2/2$) and vertices corresponding to the interaction terms ($+g$ for the 2-valent vertex from $-gx^2$, and $+g$ for the 3-valent vertex from $-gx^3/6$), only diagrams consisting of cycles of 2-valent vertices contribute at 1-loop.
The contribution is:
$$\boxed{\log(Z/Z_0)^{(1)} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-g).}$$

**Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only**
Let $a_n$ be the number of such graphs on $n$ labeled vertices.
For a 1-loop graph with only 1-valent and 3-valent vertices, the number of 1-valent vertices must equal the number of 3-valent vertices. Let this number be $k$. Then the total number of vertices $n = 2k$.
Thus, $a_n = 0$ if $n$ is odd.
If $n$ is even (so $n=2k$ for some integer $k$), a 1-loop graph requires $k \ge 2$.
The structure of such graphs is a cycle of $k$ 3-valent vertices, with each 3-valent vertex having one 1-valent vertex attached to it.

The number of such labeled graphs is:
$$\boxed{a_n = \begin{cases} \frac{n!}{n/2} & \text{if } n \text{ is even and } n \ge 4 \\ 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd or } n < 4 \end{cases}}$$

**Check for small numbers of vertices:**
*   $n=2$: $k=1$. $a_2=0$ (a 2-vertex graph is a tree, not a 1-loop graph).
*   $n=4$: $k=2$. $a_4 = \frac{4!}{2} = \frac{24}{2} = 12$.
    (Example: Choose $\{1,2\}$ as 3-valent, $\{3,4\}$ as 1-valent. $1$ and $2$ are connected by two edges. $1$ is connected to $3$, $2$ to $4$. Or $1$ to $4$, $2$ to $3$. $\binom{4}{2} \times 2 = 12$ graphs.)
*   $n=6$: $k=3$. $a_6 = \frac{6!}{3} = \frac{720}{3} = 240$.
    (Example: Choose $\{1,2,3\}$ as 3-valent, $\{4,5,6\}$ as 1-valent. $(3-1)! = 2$ ways to form a cycle of $\{1,2,3\}$. $3! = 6$ ways to attach $\{4,5,6\}$. $\binom{6}{3} \times 2 \times 6 = 20 \times 12 = 240$ graphs.)
