**Solution to the Main Problem:**

The Euclidean action for the quantum particle is given by $S[q] = \int dt \left(\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.
The interaction term is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = \int dt \left(- \tfrac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.

**Feynman Rules:**
1.  **Free Propagator:** $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
2.  **Vertex:** A 3-valent vertex (from the $q^3$ term) carries a factor of $g$. The $1/3!$ in the action cancels the $3!$ ways to connect legs to the vertex.

---

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg. We expand in powers of $g$.

*   **Order $g^0$:** No vertices. A single external leg cannot form a connected diagram on its own. So, $G_1^{(0)}(t) = 0$.

*   **Order $g^1$:** One vertex. A 3-valent vertex has three half-edges. One half-edge connects to the external leg at time $t$. The remaining two half-edges must contract with each other to form a closed loop (a "tadpole").
    *   **Diagram:** A single 3-valent vertex with one external leg and one internal loop.
    *   **Vertex Factor:** $g$.
    *   **Propagator:** The internal loop contributes $G_0(t_v, t_v)$, where $t_v$ is the vertex time.
    *   **Symmetry Factor:** The two internal legs forming the loop are indistinguishable, giving a factor of $1/2$.
    *   **Contribution:**
        $G_1^{(1)}(t) = g \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int dt_v \, \delta(t - t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
        Evaluating the integral and substituting $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
        $G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m}$.

The leading term of the 1-point function is $G_1^{(1)}(t) = \frac{g}{4m}$.

---

**(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs. We calculate up to and including order $g^2$.

*   **Order $g^0$:** This is the free propagator.
    $G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

*   **Order $g^1$:** One vertex. A 3-valent vertex has three half-edges. If two are external (to $t_1$ and $t_2$), the third must be internal. However, a single internal half-edge cannot form a closed loop or connect to another vertex (which would make it a 2-vertex diagram). Thus, there are no connected 2-point diagrams at order $g^1$. $G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = 0$.

*   **Order $g^2$:** Two vertices.
    The connected 2-point function $G_2^c$ can be expressed using the 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma$ via Dyson's equation:
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \int dt_A dt_B \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, \Sigma(t_A, t_B) \, G_0(t_B, t_2) + O(g^4)$.
    (The term $G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0 \star \Sigma \star G_0$ would be $O(g^4)$ or higher if $\Sigma$ is $O(g^2)$).

    We need to find the $O(g^2)$ contribution to $\Sigma(t_A, t_B)$.
    *   **1PI 2-point function $\Sigma(t_A, t_B)$ at order $g^2$:**
        For a cubic interaction, there are no $O(g^0)$ or $O(g^1)$ 1PI diagrams for $\Sigma$ (as argued above, a single 3-valent vertex cannot form a 1PI diagram with two amputated external legs).
        The leading 1PI diagram is at order $g^2$:
        *   **Diagram:** Two 3-valent vertices ($V_A$ at $t_A$, $V_B$ at $t_B$). Each vertex has one amputated external leg and two internal legs connecting to the other vertex. This forms a "dumbbell" shape.
        *   **Vertex Factors:** $g \cdot g = g^2$.
        *   **Internal Propagators:** Two propagators connect $V_A$ and $V_B$, contributing $G_0(t_A, t_B)^2$.
        *   **Symmetry Factor:** The two propagators connecting $V_A$ and $V_B$ are indistinguishable, giving a factor of $1/2$.
        *   **Contribution:**
            $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B) = g^2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \, G_0(t_A, t_B)^2$.
            Substituting $G_0(t_A, t_B) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_A - t_B|}$:
            $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B) = \frac{g^2}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_A - t_B|} \right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|t_A - t_B|}$.

    *   **$O(g^2)$ contribution to $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$:**
        We substitute $\Sigma^{(2)}$ into Dyson's equation:
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_A dt_B \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, \Sigma^{(2)}(t_A, t_B) \, G_0(t_B, t_2)$.
        This is a convolution integral. It is most easily solved in momentum space.
        Let $\tau = t_1 - t_2$.
        The Fourier transform of $G_0(t)$ is $G_0(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2}$.
        The Fourier transform of $\Sigma^{(2)}(t)$ is $\Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) = \int d\tau \, e^{i\omega\tau} \frac{g^2}{8m^2} e^{-2m|\tau|} = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \frac{2(2m)}{\omega^2 + (2m)^2} = \frac{g^2}{2m(\omega^2 + 4m^2)}$.
        In momentum space, the $O(g^2)$ term is $G_0(\omega) \Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) G_0(\omega)$:
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2} \cdot \frac{g^2}{2m(\omega^2 + 4m^2)} \cdot \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2} = \frac{g^2}{2m} \frac{1}{(\omega^2 + m^2)^2 (\omega^2 + 4m^2)}$.

        To transform back to time domain, we use partial fraction decomposition:
        $\frac{1}{(x+m^2)^2 (x+4m^2)} = -\frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{1}{x+m^2} + \frac{1}{3m^2} \frac{1}{(x+m^2)^2} + \frac{1}{9m^4} \frac{1}{x+4m^2}$.
        Using known inverse Fourier transforms:
        $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{\omega^2+k^2} \right](t) = \frac{1}{2k} e^{-k|t|}$
        $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(\omega^2+k^2)^2} \right](t) = \frac{1}{4k^3} (1+k|t|) e^{-k|t|}$

        Applying these to $G_2^{c,(2)}(\omega)$ (with $t = t_1 - t_2$):
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t) = \frac{g^2}{2m} \left[ -\frac{1}{9m^4} \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t|}\right) + \frac{1}{3m^2} \left(\frac{1}{4m^3}(1+m|t|)e^{-m|t|}\right) + \frac{1}{9m^4} \left(\frac{1}{2(2m)} e^{-2m|t|}\right) \right]$
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t) = \frac{g^2}{2m} \left[ -\frac{1}{18m^5} e^{-m|t|} + \frac{1}{12m^5}(1+m|t|)e^{-m|t|} + \frac{1}{36m^5} e^{-2m|t|} \right]$
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t) = \frac{g^2}{36m^6} \left[ -2 e^{-m|t|} + 3(1+m|t|)e^{-m|t|} + e^{-2m|t|} \right]$
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t) = \frac{g^2}{36m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t|)e^{-m|t|} + e^{-2m|t|} \right]$.

    **Total Connected 2-point function:**
    Combining the terms up to $O(g^2)$:
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) + G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) + O(g^3)$.
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{36m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1 - t_2|)e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} \right] + O(g^3)$.

---

**Final Answer:**

**(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$ is:**
$$\boxed{\;G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} + O(g^2)\;}$$

**(ii) The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ is:**
$$\boxed{\;G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{36m^6} \left[ (1+3m|t_1 - t_2|)e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|} \right] + O(g^3)\;}$$
