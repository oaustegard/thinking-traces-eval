The problem asks for two quantities related to the Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator:
(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.
(ii) The connected 2-point function $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ up to and including order $g^2$.

The interaction potential is $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$.
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.

**Relevant Example:**
Example 1 (Quartic Anharmonic Oscillator) is highly relevant.
*   **Relevance:** It provides the framework for calculating correlation functions in Euclidean QFT using Feynman diagrams. It establishes the Euclidean action, free propagator, Feynman rules for vertices, and the systematic expansion in powers of the coupling constant. It demonstrates how to identify and compute contributions from different diagrams.
*   **Differences:**
    *   **Interaction Term:** Example 1 has a $q^4$ interaction term, leading to a different vertex Feynman rule and different diagram topologies compared to the $q^3$ interaction in the main problem.
    *   **Quantity Calculated:** Example 1 focuses on the 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma(t_1, t_2)$. The main problem asks for the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ and the *connected* 2-point function $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$. For a $q^3$ theory, $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is generally non-zero, which means tadpole diagrams play a crucial role.
    *   **Order of Calculation:** Example 1 calculates $\Sigma$ up to $O(g^2)$. The main problem asks for $\langle q(t) \rangle$ to $O(g)$ and $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ up to $O(g^2)$.

**Step 1: Establish Feynman Rules**
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = S_0[q] + S_I[q]$, where $S_0[q] = \int dt (\tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2)$ and $S_I[q] = \int dt (-\tfrac{g}{6} q^3)$.
*   **Free Propagator:** The Fourier transform of $S_0[q]$ gives $\tilde{G}_0^{-1}(p) = p^2 + m^2$. So, the free propagator in real time is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$. In momentum space, $\tilde{G}_0(p) = \frac{1}{p^2+m^2}$.
*   **Vertex Factor:** From the interaction action $S_I = \int dt (-\tfrac{g}{6}) q^3$, the Feynman rule for a $q^3$ vertex at time $t_v$ is $-(-\tfrac{g}{6}) = \frac{g}{6}$. This factor already includes the $1/3!$ for the three identical legs of the vertex.

---

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is the sum of all connected diagrams with one external leg.
*   **Order $O(g^0)$:** There are no diagrams with an external leg and no vertices. So, $\langle q(t) \rangle^{(0)} = 0$.
*   **Order $O(g^1)$:** We need one vertex. A $q^3$ vertex has three legs. To form a connected diagram with one external leg $q(t)$, one leg must connect to $q(t)$, and the remaining two legs must form a closed loop (a tadpole).
    *   **Diagram:** A single $q^3$ vertex at time $t_v$, with one leg connected to $q(t)$ and the other two legs forming a loop at $t_v$.
    *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
        *   Vertex factor: $\frac{g}{6}$.
        *   Propagators: $G_0(t, t_v)$ for the external leg, and $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ for the loop.
        *   Integration: $\int dt_v$ over the internal vertex time.
        *   Combinatorial factor: There are 3 ways to choose which leg of the $q^3$ vertex connects to the external field $q(t)$. The remaining two legs must form the loop (1 way). So, the factor is $3$.
    *   **Contribution:** $3 \times \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    *   **Evaluation:**
        *   $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \lim_{\epsilon \to 0} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|\epsilon|} = \frac{1}{2m}$. This is a UV divergent term, but in dimensional regularization or after renormalization, it's a constant.
        *   The expression becomes $\frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v)$.
        *   In momentum space, $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v)$ corresponds to evaluating the free propagator at zero momentum: $\tilde{G}_0(p=0) = \frac{1}{0^2+m^2} = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
        *   So, the leading term is $\frac{g}{4m} \times \frac{1}{m^2} = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.
    *   **Higher orders:** Any diagram with more vertices will be of order $g^2$ or higher. Thus, this is the leading term.

---

**(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with two external legs $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$.

*   **Order $O(g^0)$:**
    *   The only diagram is the free propagator connecting $t_1$ and $t_2$.
    *   **Contribution:** $G_0(t_1, t_2)$.

*   **Order $O(g^1)$:**
    *   We need one vertex. A $q^3$ vertex at $t_v$ connects to $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$. The third leg must form a loop back to $t_v$.
    *   **Diagram:** One $q^3$ vertex at $t_v$, with legs connected to $q(t_1)$, $q(t_2)$, and a loop at $t_v$.
    *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
        *   Vertex factor: $\frac{g}{6}$.
        *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_v)$, $G_0(t_2, t_v)$, and $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
        *   Integration: $\int dt_v$.
        *   Combinatorial factor: There are 3 ways to choose which leg of the $q^3$ vertex forms the loop. The remaining two legs connect to $t_1$ and $t_2$ (1 way to assign). So, the factor is $3$.
    *   **Contribution:** $3 \times \frac{g}{6} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    *   **Evaluation:** $\frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$.

*   **Order $O(g^2)$:**
    *   We need two $q^3$ vertices, say at $t_a$ and $t_b$. The overall diagram must be connected.
    *   **1. Sunset Diagram:** This is the lowest-order 1PI (one-particle irreducible) contribution to the self-energy.
        *   **Diagram:** $q(t_1) \to v_a$, $v_b \to q(t_2)$. Vertices $v_a$ and $v_b$ are connected by three internal propagators.
        *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
            *   Vertex factors: $(\frac{g}{6})^2$.
            *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_a)$, $G_0(t_b, t_2)$ for the external lines. $G_0(t_a, t_b)^3$ for the three internal lines connecting $v_a$ and $v_b$.
            *   Integration: $\int dt_a dt_b$.
            *   Combinatorial factor: There are 3 ways to connect $q(t_1)$ to $v_a$, and 3 ways to connect $q(t_2)$ to $v_b$. The remaining two legs from $v_a$ must connect to the remaining two legs from $v_b$. The last leg from $v_a$ to $v_b$. This is $3! = 6$ ways to pair the 3 legs of $v_a$ with the 3 legs of $v_b$. So, $3 \times 3 \times 6 = 54$? No, this is simpler using 1PI.
            *   The 1PI self-energy $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b)$ is $\frac{g^2}{6} G_0(t_a, t_b)^3$ (as in Example 1, but for $\phi^3$ vertex, the factor is $3! \times (g/6)^2 = g^2/6$). The full $G_2^c$ term is then obtained by "dressing" this $\Sigma$ with external propagators.
        *   **Contribution:** $\int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) \left( \frac{g^2}{6} G_0(t_a, t_b)^3 \right) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        *   **Evaluation:** $\frac{g^2}{6} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)^3 G_0(t_b, t_2)$.

    *   **2. Two Tadpole Insertions in Series:**
        *   **Diagram:** A propagator $G_0(t_1, t_2)$ with two consecutive insertions of the $O(g^1)$ tadpole self-energy (which is not 1PI, but is a valid connected diagram contribution). $q(t_1) \to v_a(\text{loop}) \to v_b(\text{loop}) \to q(t_2)$.
        *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
            *   Each tadpole-loop-insertion contributes $\frac{g}{2}G_0(t_v,t_v) = \frac{g}{4m}$.
            *   The diagram is $G_0(t_1,t_a) \times (\frac{g}{4m}) \times G_0(t_a,t_b) \times (\frac{g}{4m}) \times G_0(t_b,t_2)$, integrated over $t_a, t_b$.
        *   **Contribution:** $\left(\frac{g}{4m}\right)^2 \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        *   **Evaluation:** $\frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.

    *   **3. Figure-Eight Diagram (crossed tadpole):**
        *   **Diagram:** $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$ connect to the same vertex $v_a$. The third leg of $v_a$ connects to $v_b$. Two legs of $v_b$ form a loop, and the third leg connects to $v_a$.
        *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
            *   Vertex factors: $(\frac{g}{6})^2$.
            *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_a)$, $G_0(t_2, t_a)$, $G_0(t_a, t_b)$, $G_0(t_b, t_b)$.
            *   Integration: $\int dt_a dt_b$.
            *   Combinatorial factor: For $v_a$: 3 ways to choose the leg connecting to $v_b$. The other 2 legs connect to $t_1, t_2$ (1 way to assign). So, factor is $3$. For $v_b$: 3 ways to choose the leg connecting to $v_a$. The other 2 legs form a loop (1 way). So, factor is $3$. Total combinatorial factor: $3 \times 3 = 9$.
        *   **Contribution:** $9 \times \left(\frac{g}{6}\right)^2 \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_b)$.
        *   **Evaluation:** $\frac{9g^2}{36} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g^2}{8m} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)$.

**Summary of Connected 2-point Function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$:**

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2)$
$\quad + \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$
$\quad + \frac{g^2}{6} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)^3 G_0(t_b, t_2)$
$\quad + \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$
$\quad + \frac{g^2}{8m} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b)$
$\quad + O(g^3)$

The integrals can be performed explicitly using the form of $G_0(t_1, t_2)$, but the problem asks for the expression modulo $g^3$, which usually means listing the diagrammatic contributions.

The final answer is $\boxed{\text{(i) } \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2) \\ \text{(ii) } G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) + \frac{g^2}{6} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1,t_a) G_0(t_a,t_b)^3 G_0(t_b,t_2) + \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) + \frac{g^2}{8m} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1,t_a) G_0(t_2,t_a) G_0(t_a,t_b) + O(g^3)}$
