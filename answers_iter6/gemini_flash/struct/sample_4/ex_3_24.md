The problem has two parts:
1.  Compute the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for a given action $S(x) = x^2/2 - g(x^2 + x^3/6)$.
2.  Compute the number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only, allowing for double edges and self-loops.

---

**Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

**Relevant Example:**
*   **Example 2: Structured Reasoning: 1-Loop Effective Action** is directly relevant. It calculates the 1-loop effective action for a 0D theory and uses the formula $S_1(x_c) = \frac{1}{2}\log(S''(x_c))$.
*   **Differences:** Example 2 has an interaction term $-\frac{gx^3}{6}$, while the main problem has $-g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})$. The problem asks for $\log(Z/Z_0)$ rather than the effective action $S_1(x_c)$, but these are related for a 0D theory.

**Reasoning:**
For a 0D quantum field theory, the partition function is $Z = \int dx \, e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$. The 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ can be found by expanding the action around its classical minimum $x_c$.
The action is given by $S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g\left(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6}\right) = \frac{1-2g}{2}x^2 - \frac{g}{6}x^3$.
The classical equation of motion is $S'(x) = (1-2g)x - \frac{g}{2}x^2 = 0$.
Assuming $g$ is small and non-zero, the classical minimum is at $x_c=0$.
The second derivative of the action is $S''(x) = (1-2g) - gx$.
Evaluating at the classical minimum, $S''(x_c) = S''(0) = 1-2g$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is given by $\log Z_{1\text{-loop}} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(S''(x_c)/\hbar) + \text{const}$. (The constant is $\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar)$).
So, $\log Z_{1\text{-loop}} = -\frac{1}{2}\log\left(\frac{1-2g}{\hbar}\right) + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g) + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi)$.

The free partition function $Z_0$ corresponds to the action $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
For $S_0(x)$, $S_0'(x) = x = 0 \implies x_c=0$.
$S_0''(x) = 1$. So $S_0''(0) = 1$.
$\log Z_0 = -\frac{1}{2}\log\left(\frac{1}{\hbar}\right) + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\hbar) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1) + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi)$.

Now, compute $\log(Z/Z_0)_{1\text{-loop}}$:
$\log(Z/Z_0)_{1\text{-loop}} = \log Z_{1\text{-loop}} - \log Z_0 = \left(-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g) + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi)\right) - \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi)$.
$\log(Z/Z_0)_{1\text{-loop}} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)$.

Alternatively, using Feynman diagrams for vacuum energy (connected diagrams without external legs):
The interaction part of the action is $S_{\text{int}}(x) = -g x^2 - g x^3/6$.
The Feynman rules for vacuum diagrams are:
*   Propagator: $\langle x^2 \rangle_0 = \hbar$. (From the free action $x^2/(2\hbar)$).
*   Vertex for $-g x^2$: $g/\hbar$. (The coefficient of $x^2$ in $S_{\text{int}}/\hbar$).
*   Vertex for $-g x^3/6$: $g/\hbar$. (The coefficient of $x^3/6$ in $S_{\text{int}}/\hbar$).

1-loop vacuum diagrams are simple cycles.
*   A vertex from $x^3$ (a 3-valent vertex) cannot form a simple cycle by itself, nor can it be part of a simple cycle without leaving dangling legs or forming multi-loop diagrams.
*   Only the $x^2$ interaction term (a 2-valent vertex) can form simple cycles.
Consider a cycle of $k$ 2-valent vertices. Each vertex contributes $g/\hbar$, and each of the $k$ propagators contributes $\hbar$. The total contribution for a $k$-cycle is $(g/\hbar)^k \hbar^k = g^k$.
The symmetry factor for a $k$-cycle vacuum diagram is $1/(2k)$.
Summing over all $k$-cycles:
$\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2k} (g^k) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-g)$.
This is for an interaction $-gx^2$. In our case, the interaction is $-g x^2$. The coefficient of $x^2/2$ in $S_{\text{int}}$ is $-2g$.
So the effective "mass" contribution from interaction is $-2g$.
The 1-loop vacuum energy is $-\frac{1}{2}\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(2g)^k}{k} = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)$.
This matches the previous result.

**Answer for Part 1:**
The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is $-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)$.

**Part 2: Number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only**

**Relevant Example:**
*   **Example 1: Structured Reasoning: EGF for Restricted-Valence Labeled Trees** is relevant for combinatorial graph enumeration with labeled vertices and valence restrictions.
*   **Differences:** Example 1 is about trees (no loops) and specific valences (1-valent and 4-valent). The main problem is about 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices.

**Reasoning:**
Let $n$ be the total number of labeled vertices. Let $n_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $n_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
So $n = n_1 + n_3$.
For a graph with $E$ edges, the sum of degrees is $2E$.
$n_1 \cdot 1 + n_3 \cdot 3 = 2E$.
Since the graph is a 1-loop graph, it is connected and has $E=n$ edges.
So $n_1 + 3n_3 = 2n$.
Substitute $n_1 = n - n_3$:
$(n - n_3) + 3n_3 = 2n$
$n + 2n_3 = 2n$
$2n_3 = n$.
This implies $n$ must be an even number, so $n_3 = n/2$.
Then $n_1 = n - n_3 = n - n/2 = n/2$.
So, any such graph must have an equal number of 1-valent and 3-valent vertices, $n_1 = n_3 = n/2$. If $n$ is odd, the number of such graphs is 0.

A 1-loop graph consists of a single cycle with trees attached to its vertices. Since all non-cycle vertices must be 1-valent, these "trees" can only be single edges connecting a 3-valent vertex on the cycle to a 1-valent vertex (a leaf).
Therefore, all $n_3 = n/2$ vertices must be on the cycle, and each of them must be connected to one of the $n_1 = n/2$ 1-valent vertices.

Let $m = n/2$. We have $m$ 3-valent vertices and $m$ 1-valent vertices.
The construction proceeds as follows:
1.  **Choose the $m$ vertices that will be 3-valent:** There are $\binom{n}{m} = \binom{n}{n/2}$ ways to choose these labeled vertices. The remaining $m$ vertices will be 1-valent.
2.  **Form a cycle with the $m$ 3-valent vertices:**
    *   If $m=1$ (i.e., $n=2$): There is one 3-valent vertex. A cycle with one vertex is a self-loop. There is 1 way to form a self-loop on a labeled vertex.
    *   If $m=2$ (i.e., $n=4$): There are two 3-valent vertices. A cycle with two vertices is a double edge between them. There is 1 way to form a double edge between two labeled vertices.
    *   If $m \ge 3$ (i.e., $n \ge 6$): There are $m$ 3-valent vertices. A simple cycle connecting $m$ labeled vertices can be formed in $\frac{1}{2}(m-1)!$ ways. (The factor $1/2$ accounts for reversing the cycle direction, and $(m-1)!$ for fixing one vertex and permuting the others).
3.  **Connect the remaining legs:** Each of the $m$ 3-valent vertices on the cycle has one free leg. Each of the $m$ 1-valent vertices has one free leg. We must connect these $m$ free legs from the 3-valent vertices to the $m$ 1-valent vertices. There are $m!$ ways to pair them up.

Combining these steps for $n=2m$:
*   **Case $n=2$ ($m=1$):**
    $N_2 = \binom{2}{1} \times (\text{cycle factor for } m=1) \times 1! = 2 \times 1 \times 1 = 2$.
*   **Case $n=4$ ($m=2$):**
    $N_4 = \binom{4}{2} \times (\text{cycle factor for } m=2) \times 2! = 6 \times 1 \times 2 = 12$.
*   **Case $n \ge 6$ ($m \ge 3$ and $n$ is even):**
    $N_n = \binom{n}{n/2} \times \frac{1}{2}(\frac{n}{2}-1)! \times (\frac{n}{2})!$
    $N_n = \frac{n!}{(n/2)!(n/2)!} \times \frac{1}{2}(\frac{n}{2}-1)! \times (\frac{n}{2})!$
    $N_n = \frac{n!}{(n/2)!} \times \frac{1}{2}(\frac{n}{2}-1)! = \frac{n!}{2 \cdot (n/2)} = \frac{n!}{n} = (n-1)!$.

**Summary for $N_n$:**
*   If $n$ is odd, $N_n = 0$.
*   If $n=2$, $N_2 = 2$.
*   If $n=4$, $N_4 = 12$.
*   If $n \ge 6$ and $n$ is even, $N_n = (n-1)!$.

**Check with small numbers of vertices:**
*   **$n=2$:** We have two labeled vertices, say {1, 2}. One is 1-valent, one is 3-valent.
    1.  Vertex 1 is 1-valent, Vertex 2 is 3-valent. Edges: (1,2) (connects the 1-valent vertex to one leg of the 3-valent vertex). Vertex 2 has two legs remaining. These must form a self-loop (2,2). This is one graph.
    2.  Vertex 2 is 1-valent, Vertex 1 is 3-valent. Edges: (2,1) and (1,1). This is another graph.
    Total $N_2=2$. Matches the formula.
*   **$n=4$:** We have four labeled vertices, say {1, 2, 3, 4}. Two are 1-valent, two are 3-valent.
    Choose the two 3-valent vertices: $\binom{4}{2}=6$ ways. Let them be {1, 2}. So {3, 4} are 1-valent.
    The two 3-valent vertices {1, 2} must form the cycle. A cycle of two vertices means a double edge between them: (1,2) and (1,2). This uses two legs from vertex 1 and two legs from vertex 2.
    Vertex 1 has one leg left, Vertex 2 has one leg left. Vertices 3 and 4 are 1-valent.
    We must connect the remaining legs:
    1.  (1,3) and (2,4).
    2.  (1,4) and (2,3).
    These are 2 ways. So for each of the 6 choices of 3-valent vertices, there are 2 graphs. Total $N_4 = 6 \times 2 = 12$. Matches the formula.

The enumeration checks out.

The final answer is $\boxed{\text{Part 1: } -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g) \\ \text{Part 2: } N_n = \begin{cases} 2 & \text{if } n=2 \\ 12 & \text{if } n=4 \\ (n-1)! & \text{if } n \ge 6 \text{ and } n \text{ is even} \\ 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \end{cases}}$
