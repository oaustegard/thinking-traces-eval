The problem asks for the leading term of the 1-point function and the connected 2-point function up to order $g^2$ for a Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator.

**Relevant Example Analysis:**
Example 1 is highly relevant. It provides the framework for calculating Green's functions in Euclidean quantum field theory using Feynman diagrams. It establishes the Euclidean action, free propagator, and Feynman rules, and demonstrates how to calculate diagrammatic expansions.

**Differences from Example 1:**
1.  **Interaction Term:** Example 1 features a $q^4$ interaction, leading to 4-valent vertices with a Feynman rule of $-g$. Our problem has a $q^3$ interaction, leading to 3-valent vertices. This changes the Feynman rule for the vertex and the structure of diagrams.
2.  **Symmetry:** The $q^4$ interaction has a $\mathbb{Z}_2$ symmetry ($q \to -q$), implying that all odd $n$-point functions are zero (e.g., $\langle q(t) \rangle = 0$). The $q^3$ interaction breaks this symmetry, so the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ will generally be non-zero.
3.  **Quantity Calculated:** Example 1 calculates the 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma$. We need to calculate the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ and the *connected* 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$. While related, these are different quantities that require summing different sets of diagrams.

---

**Step-by-step Solution:**

The Euclidean action is given by $S[q] = \int dt \left(\frac12 \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.
The free part of the action is $S_0[q] = \int dt \left(\frac12 \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2\right)$.
The interaction part of the action is $S_I[q] = \int dt \left(-\frac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.

**Feynman Rules:**
*   **Free Propagator:** $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$. In momentum space, $G_0(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2}$.
*   **Vertex:** Each $q^3$ interaction vertex (3 legs) carries a factor of $-g$. (The $1/6$ from the action cancels with $3!$ from Wick's theorem when contracting the three fields at the vertex).
*   Integrate over internal vertex times.
*   Divide by symmetry factors (already implicitly handled by counting contractions and $1/N!$ for identical vertices).

---

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) := \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is the sum of all connected diagrams with one external leg at time $t$.

*   **Order $g^0$:**
    In the absence of interactions ($g=0$), the action $S_0[q]$ is quadratic and even in $q$. Thus, $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.

*   **Order $g^1$ (Leading Term):**
    The lowest order non-zero contribution comes from diagrams with one vertex. A single 3-valent vertex at $t_v$ has one leg connected to the external field $q(t)$, and the remaining two legs must form a closed loop (a "tadpole" diagram).
    The contribution is given by:
    $G_1^{(1)}(t) = \int dt_v \left(-\frac{g}{6}\right) \langle q(t) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$.
    By Wick's theorem, there are 3 ways to contract $q(t)$ with one of the $q(t_v)$ fields, and the remaining two $q(t_v)$ fields contract with each other.
    $G_1^{(1)}(t) = \int dt_v \left(-\frac{g}{6}\right) \times 3 \times G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    Using $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$ (the propagator at coincident points, $e^{-m|0|}/(2m)$).
    $G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) \frac{1}{2m} = -\frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v)$.
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v)$ represents $G_0(\omega=0)$ in momentum space, which is $\frac{1}{m^2}$.
    $G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{4m} \frac{1}{m^2} = -\frac{g}{4m^3}$.
    This is a constant, as expected for a translationally invariant system.

The leading term of the 1-point function with respect to $g$ is $G_1(t) = -\frac{g}{4m^3}$.

**(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$ (i.e., up to and including order $g^2$).**

The connected 2-point function is the sum of all connected diagrams with two external legs at $t_1$ and $t_2$.

*   **Order $g^0$:**
    The only diagram is the free propagator connecting $t_1$ and $t_2$.
    $G_2^{c(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2)$.

*   **Order $g^1$:**
    We need diagrams with one interaction vertex. The diagram consists of two external legs ($t_1, t_2$) connected to a 3-valent vertex at $t_v$, with the third leg forming a loop.
    The contribution is:
    $G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{1!} \int dt_v \left(-\frac{g}{6}\right) \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_v) q(t_v) q(t_v) \rangle_0$.
    By Wick's theorem, there are $3! = 6$ ways to contract the fields: $q(t_1)$ with one $q(t_v)$, $q(t_2)$ with another $q(t_v)$, and the remaining $q(t_v)$ with itself.
    $G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_v \left(-\frac{g}{6}\right) \times 6 \times G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    $G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -g \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) \frac{1}{2m}$.
    $G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{2m} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$.
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$ is the inverse Fourier transform of $G_0(\omega)^2 = \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2}$. This integral is known to be $\frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|)$.
    $G_2^{c(1)}(t_1, t_2) = -\frac{g}{2m} \frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) = -\frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+m|t_1-t_2|)$.

*   **Order $g^2$:**
    We need diagrams with two interaction vertices. Let the vertices be at $t_a$ and $t_b$. The overall prefactor for two vertices is $\frac{1}{2!} \left(-\frac{g}{6}\right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{72}$.
    We need to consider contractions for $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_a)^3 q(t_b)^3 \rangle_0$.

    There are two distinct types of connected diagrams at this order:

    1.  **Two "tadpole" insertions on the propagator:**
        This diagram involves the two external legs connecting to $t_a$ and $t_b$, which in turn are connected to each other and each form a loop.
        Diagram structure: $t_1 \leftrightarrow V(t_a) \leftrightarrow V(t_b) \leftrightarrow t_2$, where $V(t_a)$ has a loop and $V(t_b)$ has a loop.
        Contractions:
        *   $q(t_1)$ contracts with one $q(t_a)$ (3 ways).
        *   $q(t_2)$ contracts with one $q(t_b)$ (3 ways).
        *   One remaining $q(t_a)$ contracts with one remaining $q(t_b)$ (2 ways for $q(t_a)$, 2 ways for $q(t_b)$).
        *   The last $q(t_a)$ forms a loop with itself (1 way).
        *   The last $q(t_b)$ forms a loop with itself (1 way).
        Total number of contractions: $3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 2 = 36$.
        Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \times 36 \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) [G_0(t_a, t_a)]^2$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right)^2$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        This integral is the inverse Fourier transform of $G_0(\omega)^3 = \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^3}$. This is $\frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{16m^5} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|+3m^2 (t_1-t_2)^2)$.
        $G_2^{c(2),A}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \frac{e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}}{16m^5} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|+3m^2 (t_1-t_2)^2)$
        $= \frac{g^2}{128m^7} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|+3m^2 (t_1-t_2)^2)$.

    2.  **"Sunset" diagram (two propagators between vertices):**
        This is a 1PI diagram. The two external legs connect to $t_a$ and $t_b$, and $t_a$ and $t_b$ are connected by two internal propagators.
        Diagram structure: $t_1 \leftrightarrow V(t_a)$ and $t_2 \leftrightarrow V(t_b)$, with two lines connecting $V(t_a)$ and $V(t_b)$.
        Contractions:
        *   $q(t_1)$ contracts with one $q(t_a)$ (3 ways).
        *   $q(t_2)$ contracts with one $q(t_b)$ (3 ways).
        *   One remaining $q(t_a)$ contracts with one remaining $q(t_b)$ (2 ways for $q(t_a)$, 2 ways for $q(t_b)$). This forms the first internal propagator.
        *   The last $q(t_a)$ contracts with the last $q(t_b)$ (1 way). This forms the second internal propagator.
        Total number of contractions: $3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 2 = 36$.
        Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \times 36 \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_b, t_2) [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_b, t_2) [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2$.
        This integral involves a loop in momentum space. Let $T = |t_1-t_2|$.
        This term can be expressed in momentum space as $\int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} e^{-i\omega T} G_0(\omega)^2 I_{\text{loop}}(\omega)$, where $I_{\text{loop}}(\omega)$ is the Fourier transform of $[G_0(t_a,t_b)]^2$ after integrating $t_a, t_b$ over $e^{i\omega(t_a-t_b)}$.
        $I_{\text{loop}}(\omega) = \int \frac{d\omega'}{2\pi} G_0(\omega') G_0(\omega-\omega') = \int \frac{d\omega'}{2\pi} \frac{1}{(\omega'^2+m^2)((\omega-\omega')^2+m^2)}$.
        The inverse Fourier transform of $G_0(\omega)^2 I_{\text{loop}}(\omega)$ is complicated. It is common to leave such terms in momentum space or define the loop integral.
        $G_2^{c(2),B}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{2} \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} e^{-i\omega(t_1-t_2)} \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2} I_{\text{loop}}(\omega)$, where $I_{\text{loop}}(\omega) = \frac{1}{2m\sqrt{\omega^2+4m^2}} \operatorname{arccot}\left(\frac{2m}{\sqrt{\omega^2+4m^2}}\right)$.

**Summary of $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $O(g^2)$:**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) - \frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) + \frac{g^2}{128m^7} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|+3m^2 (t_1-t_2)^2) + \frac{g^2}{2} \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} e^{-i\omega(t_1-t_2)} \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2} I_{\text{loop}}(\omega) + O(g^3)$.

Where $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$ and $I_{\text{loop}}(\omega) = \int \frac{d\omega'}{2\pi} \frac{1}{(\omega'^2+m^2)((\omega-\omega')^2+m^2)}$.

The final answer is $\boxed{G_1(t) = -\frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2), \quad G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) - \frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) + \frac{g^2}{128m^7} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} (1+3m|t_1-t_2|+3m^2 (t_1-t_2)^2) + \frac{g^2}{2} \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} e^{-i\omega(t_1-t_2)} \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2} \left(\int \frac{d\omega'}{2\pi} \frac{1}{(\omega'^2+m^2)((\omega-\omega')^2+m^2)}\right) + O(g^3)}$
