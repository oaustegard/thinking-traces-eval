2.  **Example 1: # Structured Reasoning: EGF for Restricted-Valence Labeled Trees**

Example 1 is an enumeration problem for labeled trees with restricted valencies. **Part 1: Compute the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

The partition function is $Z = \int dx e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$, and $Z_0 = \int dx e^{-S_0(x)/\hbar}$ where $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
The action is $S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g(x^2 + \frac{x^3}{6})$.
We can rewrite $S(x)$ as $S(x) = \frac{(1-2g)x^2}{2} - \frac{g x^3}{6}$.

For a 0-dimensional field theory (a single variable integral), the 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is given by the formula:
$\log Z = -\frac{S(x_c)}{\hbar} + \frac{1}{2} \log \left(\frac{2\pi\hbar}{S''(x_c)}\right)$, where $x_c$ is a classical solution (a minimum of $S(x)$).
For the free theory, $S_0(x) = x^2/2$, the classical solution is $x_c=0$, and $S_0''(0)=1$.
So, $\log Z_0 = \frac{1}{2} \log(2\pi\hbar)$.

For the full action $S(x)$, we first find the classical solution $x_c$.
$S'(x) = x - 2gx - \frac{g x^2}{2} = x(1 - 2g - \frac{gx}{2})$.
Setting $S'(x)=0$ gives $x_c=0$ or $1 - 2g - \frac{gx}{2} = 0 \implies x_c = \frac{2(1-2g)}{g}$.
For computing the vacuum energy (which $\log(Z/Z_0)$ represents), we typically expand around $x_c=0$ if it's a stable minimum (i.e., $S''(0) > 0$).
$S''(x) = 1 - 2g - gx$.
At $x_c=0$, $S''(0) = 1 - 2g$.
For $g < 1/2$, $S''(0)>0$, so $x_c=0$ is a stable minimum.
Using $x_c=0$, we have $S(0)=0$.
Thus, $\log Z = \frac{1}{2} \log \left(\frac{2\pi\hbar}{1-2g}\right)$.

Now we can compute $\log(Z/Z_0)$:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \log Z - \log Z_0 = \frac{1}{2} \log \left(\frac{2\pi\hbar}{1-2g}\right) - \frac{1}{2} \log(2\pi\hbar)$
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \frac{1}{2} \log \left(\frac{1}{1-2g}\right) = -\frac{1}{2} \log(1-2g)$.

This result is the *exact* contribution from the quadratic part of the action. The cubic term $-\frac{gx^3}{6}$ does not contribute to this 1-loop vacuum quantity because it vanishes at $x_c=0$ and its derivative $gx_c$ also vanishes at $x_c=0$, so it does not affect $S''(0)$. This is consistent with Example 2, where the 1-loop effective action for a cubic interaction term $S_1(x_c)$ becomes zero for vacuum diagrams ($x_c=0$).

**Answer for Part 1:**
The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is $\boxed{-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)}$.

**Part 2: Number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only (be careful with double edges and self-loops).**

Let $G=(V,E)$ be a labeled $n$-vertex graph. Let $n_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $n_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices. The problem states that *only* 1-valent and 3-valent vertices are present, so $n = n_1 + n_3$.

For any graph, the sum of degrees is $2|E|$. So, $1 \cdot n_1 + 3 \cdot n_3 = 2|E|$.
For a connected graph with exactly one loop (a 1-loop graph), the number of edges is $|E| = |V| = n$.
Substituting $|E|=n$ and $n_3 = n - n_1$ into the degree sum equation:
$n_1 + 3(n - n_1) = 2n$
$n_1 + 3n - 3n_1 = 2n$
$3n - 2n_1 = 2n$
$n = 2n_1$.
This implies $n_1 = n/2$. Since $n_1$ must be an integer, $n$ must be an even number.
Also, $n_3 = n - n_1 = n - n/2 = n/2$.
So, for a graph of this type to exist, $n$ must be even, and it must have exactly $n/2$ 1-valent vertices and $n/2$ 3-valent vertices.

A 1-loop graph consists of a single cycle with trees rooted at its vertices. Since all vertices are 1-valent or 3-valent:
*   A 1-valent vertex must be a leaf (it cannot be part of a cycle, as cycle vertices have degree at least 2).
*   A vertex on the cycle must have degree at least 2. If it has degree 2, it's not allowed. So all vertices on the cycle must be 3-valent.

Let $k$ be the length of the cycle. The $k$ vertices on the cycle are 3-valent. Each uses 2 edges to be part of the cycle, leaving 1 "stub" for each. These $k$ stubs must connect to the $n_1 = n/2$ 1-valent vertices. Since each 1-valent vertex needs one connection, there must be $k$ 1-valent vertices, and each is connected to a distinct cycle vertex.
So, the structure of such a graph is a cycle $C_k$ where each vertex of the cycle has a single pendant edge (a leaf) attached to it.
The total number of vertices is $n = k \text{ (cycle vertices)} + k \text{ (leaf vertices)} = 2k$. This confirms $n$ must be even.

Now we enumerate these labeled graphs for $n=2k$:

1.  **Case $n$ is odd**: $N_n = 0$.

2.  **Case $n=2$**: Here $k=1$. This corresponds to a cycle of length 1 (a self-loop).
    The graph has 1 3-valent vertex (with a self-loop and a leaf) and 1 1-valent vertex (the leaf).
    *   Choose 1 vertex out of $n=2$ to have the self-loop (and be 3-valent): $\binom{2}{1}$ ways.
    *   The other vertex is the 1-valent leaf.
    *   Attach the leaf to the 3-valent vertex: $1!$ way.
    *   A self-loop on a labeled vertex is unique.
    Number of graphs: $\binom{2}{1} \times 1 \times 1 = 2$.

3.  **Case $n=4$**: Here $k=2$. This corresponds to a cycle of length 2 (a double edge).
    The graph has 2 3-valent vertices (forming a double edge, each with a leaf) and 2 1-valent vertices (the leaves).
    *   Choose 2 vertices out of $n=4$ to form the double edge (and be 3-valent): $\binom{4}{2}$ ways.
    *   The remaining 2 vertices are the 1-valent leaves.
    *   A double edge between 2 labeled vertices is unique.
    *   Attach the 2 leaves to the 2 cycle vertices: $2!$ ways.
    Number of graphs: $\binom{4}{2} \times 1 \times 2! = 6 \times 2 = 12$.

4.  **Case $n \ge 6$ and $n$ is even**: Here $k \ge 3$. This corresponds to a standard cycle $C_k$.
    *   Choose $k=n/2$ vertices out of $n$ to form the cycle: $\binom{n}{k}$ ways.
    *   The number of ways to form a labeled cycle of length $k$ from these $k$ chosen vertices is $(k-1)!$. (e.g., for 3 vertices $\{1,2,3\}$, $1-2-3-1$ is one cycle, $1-3-2-1$ is another distinct labeled cycle).
    *   The remaining $k=n/2$ vertices are the leaves.
    *   Attach these $k$ leaves to the $k$ cycle vertices (each leaf to a distinct cycle vertex): $k!$ ways.
    Number of graphs: $\binom{n}{k} \times (k-1)! \times k! = \frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!} (k-1)! k! = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!} (k-1)!$.
    Since $n=2k$, we have $n-k = k$.
    So, $N_n = \frac{n!}{k!} (k-1)! = \frac{n!}{k}$.
    Substituting $k=n/2$, we get $N_n = \frac{n!}{n/2}$.

**Summary for Part 2:**
The number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only, denoted $N_n$, is:
*   $N_n = 0$ if $n$ is odd.
*   $N_2 = 2$.
*   $N_4 = 12$.
*   $N_n = \frac{n!}{n/2}$ if $n \ge 6$ and $n$ is even.

**Check for small numbers of vertices:**
*   $n=1,3,5$: $N_n=0$, as expected (must be even).
*   $n=2$: Formula gives $2$.
    The two graphs are: (1-2 with self-loop on 1), and (1-2 with self-loop on 2).
*   $n=4$: Formula gives $12$.
    The graph structure is a double edge between two vertices, each having a leaf. e.g., $\{1,2\}$ form the double edge, $\{3,4\}$ are leaves.
    Possible attachments: $3$ to $1$, $4$ to $2$; or $3$ to $2$, $4$ to $1$.
    Number of ways to choose $\{1,2\}$ from $\{1,2,3,4\}$ is $\binom{4}{2}=6$. For each choice, e.g. $\{1,2\}$, the leaves are $\{3,4\}$.
    The double edge is between $1$ and $2$.
    Leaves can be attached as $(1-3, 2-4)$ or $(1-4, 2-3)$. So $6 \times 2 = 12$.
*   $n=6$: Formula gives $N_6 = \frac{6!}{6/2} = \frac{720}{3} = 240$.
    This corresponds to choosing 3 vertices for the cycle, forming a labeled cycle, and attaching 3 leaves.
    $\binom{6}{3}$ ways to choose cycle vertices.
    $(3-1)! = 2$ ways to form a labeled cycle on these 3 vertices.
    $3! = 6$ ways to attach the 3 leaves to the 3 cycle vertices.
    $\binom{6}{3} \times (3-1)! \times 3! = 20 \times 2 \times 6 = 240$. The formula holds.

The final answer is $\boxed{ \begin{aligned} \text{1-loop contribution to } \log(Z/Z_0) &= -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g) \\ \text{Number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only (N_n)} &= \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \\ 2 & \text{if } n=2 \\ 12 & \text{if } n=4 \\ n!/(n/2) & \text{if } n \ge 6 \text{ and } n \text{ is even} \end{cases} \end{aligned} }$
