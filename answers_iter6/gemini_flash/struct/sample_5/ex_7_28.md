The problem asks us to calculate the leading term of the 1-point function and the connected 2-point function up to $O(g^2)$ for a cubic anharmonic oscillator in Euclidean quantum mechanics.

**Relevant Examples and Differences:**

*   **Example 1** is highly relevant. It demonstrates the calculation of 1-particle irreducible (1PI) functions and connected correlation functions using Feynman diagrams in quantum field theory. It establishes the Euclidean action, free propagator, and uses Dyson's equation, which are all crucial for this problem.
*   **Example 2 and 3** are not relevant as they deal with different areas of mathematics (differential topology and combinatorics, respectively).

**Specific Differences from Example 1:**

1.  **Interaction Term:** Example 1 deals with a quartic interaction ($U(q) \propto q^4$), while our problem involves a cubic interaction ($U(q) \propto q^3$). This means the Feynman rules for vertices and the resulting diagrams will be different.
    *   Example 1: Action $S_{int} = \int dt \frac{g}{24} q^4$. Vertex factor $-g$ (assuming $g$ includes $1/24$).
    *   Main Problem: Potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$. This means the interaction part of the action is $S_{int} = \int dt (-\frac{g}{6} q^3)$. The Feynman vertex factor for a $q^3$ vertex is $-(-g/6) = g/6$.
2.  **Quantities to Calculate:** Example 1 calculates the 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma$. Our problem asks for the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ and the *connected* 2-point function $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$. The connected 2-point function is the full propagator $G(t_1, t_2)$, which can be expressed in terms of $\Sigma$ via Dyson's equation.
3.  **Order of Perturbation:** We need the leading term for the 1-point function (lowest non-zero order in $g$) and up to $O(g^2)$ for the connected 2-point function.

---

**Solution:**

First, we establish the basic Feynman rules:
*   **Euclidean Action:** $S[q] = \int dt \left(\frac12 \dot q^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3\right)$.
*   **Free Propagator:** $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.
*   **Vertex Factor:** Each $q^3$ vertex at time $t_v$ carries a factor of $g/6$.

---

**(i) Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function is the sum of all connected diagrams with one external leg. In the free theory ($g=0$), $\langle q(t) \rangle = 0$. Thus, the leading term must be at least $O(g)$.

At $O(g^1)$, we have a single $q^3$ vertex. For a connected diagram with one external leg, the other two legs must form a closed loop.
*   **Diagram:** A single $q^3$ vertex at $t_v$. One leg connects to the external point $t$. The other two legs form a loop, connecting back to $t_v$.
*   **Vertex factor:** $g/6$.
*   **Propagators:** $G_0(t, t_v)$ for the external leg, and $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ for the internal loop.
*   **Combinatorial factor:** In the path integral expansion, we have $\int Dq \, q(t) \, e^{-S_0} \int dt' (g/6) q(t')^3$. We need to contract $q(t)$ with one $q(t')$ (3 choices) and the remaining two $q(t')$ with each other (1 choice). So, the factor is 3.

The contribution is:
$G_1(t)^{(1)} = \int dt_v \left( \frac{g}{6} \right) \cdot 3 \cdot G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$
Using $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v - t_v|} = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$G_1(t)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t - t_v|} \right) \left( \frac{1}{2m} \right)$
$G_1(t)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{8m^2} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt_v e^{-m|t - t_v|}$
The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-m|x|} dx = 2 \int_0^{\infty} e^{-mx} dx = 2 \left[ -\frac{e^{-mx}}{m} \right]_0^{\infty} = \frac{2}{m}$.
$G_1(t)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{8m^2} \left( \frac{2}{m} \right) = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.
This is a constant, as expected for a time-translationally invariant system.

---

**(ii) Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ is the full propagator $G(t_1, t_2)$, which can be expanded perturbatively.

*   **$O(g^0)$ term:** This is the free propagator.
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

*   **$O(g^1)$ term:** We look for connected diagrams with two external legs and one $q^3$ vertex.
    *   **Diagram:** A single $q^3$ vertex at $t_v$. One leg connects to $t_1$, another to $t_2$. The third leg forms a loop back to $t_v$.
    *   **Vertex factor:** $g/6$.
    *   **Propagators:** $G_0(t_1, t_v)$, $G_0(t_2, t_v)$, $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    *   **Combinatorial factor:** There are 3 ways to connect to $t_1$, 2 ways to connect to $t_2$, and 1 way for the remaining leg to form a loop. So, $3 \times 2 \times 1 = 6$.

The contribution is:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \int dt_v \left( \frac{g}{6} \right) \cdot 6 \cdot G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = g \int dt_v \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_v|} \right) \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_2 - t_v|} \right) \left( \frac{1}{2m} \right)$
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{8m^3} \int dt_v e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|)}$.
Let $I(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_v e^{-m(|t_1 - t_v| + |t_2 - t_v|)}$. Assuming $t_1 < t_2$:
$I(t_1, t_2) = \int_{-\infty}^{t_1} e^{-m(t_1 - t_v + t_2 - t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_1}^{t_2} e^{-m(t_v - t_1 + t_2 - t_v)} dt_v + \int_{t_2}^{\infty} e^{-m(t_v - t_1 + t_v - t_2)} dt_v$
$I(t_1, t_2) = \frac{e^{-m(t_2 - t_1)}}{2m} + (t_2 - t_1) e^{-m(t_2 - t_1)} + \frac{e^{-m(t_2 - t_1)}}{2m}$
$I(t_1, t_2) = \left( \frac{1}{m} + |t_1 - t_2| \right) e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$ (for general $t_1, t_2$).
So, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{8m^3} \left( \frac{1}{m} + |t_1 - t_2| \right) e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} = \frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g|t_1 - t_2|}{8m^3} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

*   **$O(g^2)$ term:** This involves diagrams with two $q^3$ vertices. The full propagator $G(t_1, t_2)$ is related to the 1PI self-energy $\Sigma(t_1, t_2)$ by Dyson's equation: $G = G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 \Sigma G_0 + \dots$.
    We need $\Sigma$ up to $O(g^2)$.
    *   $\Sigma^{(0)} = 0$.
    *   $\Sigma^{(1)}$: A single $q^3$ vertex with two external amputated legs. The third leg cannot form a loop (not 1PI if a single cut splits it). It cannot connect to another external leg. Thus, there are no 1PI diagrams at $O(g^1)$ for $\phi^3$ theory. So, $\Sigma^{(1)} = 0$.
    *   $\Sigma^{(2)}$: We need 1PI diagrams with two external amputated legs and two $q^3$ vertices.
        *   **Diagram:** Two $q^3$ vertices at $t_a, t_b$. One leg from $t_a$ is amputated (to $t_1$), one leg from $t_b$ is amputated (to $t_2$). The remaining two legs from $t_a$ connect to the remaining two legs from $t_b$, forming two propagators between $t_a$ and $t_b$. This is a 1PI diagram.
        *   **Vertex factors:** $(g/6)^2$.
        *   **Propagators:** $[G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2$.
        *   **Combinatorial factor:** For $V_a$: 3 ways to choose the external leg. For $V_b$: 3 ways to choose the external leg. The remaining 2 legs from $V_a$ connect to the remaining 2 legs from $V_b$ in $2 \times 1 = 2$ ways. Total $3 \times 3 \times 2 = 18$. Since the two vertices are identical, divide by $2!$. So, $18/2 = 9$.
        *   $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) = 9 \cdot (g/6)^2 [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2 = 9 \frac{g^2}{36} [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2 = \frac{g^2}{4} [G_0(t_1, t_2)]^2$.
        *   Substituting $G_0$: $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{4} \left( \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} \right)^2 = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} e^{-2m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

    Now, we use Dyson's equation to get the $O(g^2)$ contribution to $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$:
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
    This is a convolution. It's easier to calculate in frequency space ($\omega$-space).
    The Fourier transform of $G_0(t)$ is $G_0(\omega) = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2}$.
    The Fourier transform of $\Sigma^{(2)}(t)$ is $\Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int d\tau e^{i\omega\tau} e^{-2m|\tau|}$.
    The integral $\int d\tau e^{i\omega\tau} e^{-2m|\tau|} = \frac{4m}{\omega^2 + (2m)^2}$.
    So, $\Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \frac{4m}{\omega^2 + 4m^2} = \frac{g^2}{4m(\omega^2 + 4m^2)}$.
    In frequency space, $G_2^c(\omega)^{(2)} = G_0(\omega) \Sigma^{(2)}(\omega) G_0(\omega)$:
    $G_2^c(\omega)^{(2)} = \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2} \frac{g^2}{4m(\omega^2 + 4m^2)} \frac{1}{\omega^2 + m^2} = \frac{g^2}{4m} \frac{1}{(\omega^2 + m^2)^2 (\omega^2 + 4m^2)}$.
    We perform partial fraction decomposition for $\frac{1}{(x+m^2)^2(x+4m^2)}$ where $x=\omega^2$:
    $\frac{1}{(x+m^2)^2(x+4m^2)} = \frac{-1}{9m^4(x+m^2)} + \frac{1}{3m^2(x+m^2)^2} + \frac{1}{9m^4(x+4m^2)}$.
    We use the inverse Fourier transforms:
    $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{\omega^2+M^2} \right] = \frac{1}{2M} e^{-M|t|}$.
    $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(\omega^2+M^2)^2} \right] = \frac{1}{4M^3} e^{-M|t|} + \frac{|t|}{2M^2} e^{-M|t|}$.
    Applying these:
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \frac{g^2}{4m} \left[ \frac{-1}{9m^4} \left(\frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}\right) + \frac{1}{3m^2} \left(\frac{1}{4m^3} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{|t_1-t_2|}{2m^2} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}\right) + \frac{1}{9m^4} \left(\frac{1}{2(2m)} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}\right) \right]$
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \frac{g^2}{4m} \left[ \left(\frac{-1}{18m^5} + \frac{1}{12m^5}\right) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{|t_1-t_2|}{6m^4} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{1}{36m^5} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right]$
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \frac{g^2}{4m} \left[ \frac{1}{36m^5} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{6|t_1-t_2|}{36m^5} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{1}{36m^5} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right]$
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} = \frac{g^2}{144m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2|t_1-t_2|}{24m^5} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{144m^6} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Combining all terms for $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $O(g^2)$:**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} + G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2)} + O(g^3)$
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \left( \frac{g}{8m^4} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g|t_1 - t_2|}{8m^3} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} \right) + \left( \frac{g^2}{144m^6} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2|t_1-t_2|}{24m^5} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{144m^6} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} \right) + O(g^3)$
Collecting terms by their exponential factors:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \left( \frac{1}{2m} + \frac{g}{8m^4} + \frac{g^2}{144m^6} \right) e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \left( \frac{g}{8m^3} + \frac{g^2}{24m^5} \right) |t_1 - t_2| e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{144m^6} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3)$.

The final answer is $\boxed{\begin{aligned} (i) &\; \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2) \\ (ii) &\; \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c = \left( \frac{1}{2m} + \frac{g}{8m^4} + \frac{g^2}{144m^6} \right) e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \left( \frac{g}{8m^3} + \frac{g^2}{24m^5} \right) |t_1 - t_2| e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{144m^6} e^{-2m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3) \end{aligned}}$
