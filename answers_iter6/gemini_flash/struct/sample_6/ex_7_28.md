The problem asks us to calculate the leading term of the 1-point function and the connected 2-point function up to $O(g^2)$ for a Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator.

**Relevant Examples and Differences:**

*   **Example 1 (Quartic Anharmonic Oscillator):** This example is highly relevant. It demonstrates the calculation of Feynman diagrams in Euclidean QFT, including setting up the action, identifying the free propagator, defining Feynman rules for vertices, and calculating contributions to correlation functions (specifically, the 1PI 2-point function).
    *   **Difference 1: Interaction Term:** Example 1 uses a $q^4$ interaction, while our problem uses a $q^3$ interaction. This changes the Feynman vertex rule and the topology of diagrams. For $q^4$, the vertex factor is $-g$. For $q^3$, the potential is $U(q) = \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$, so the interaction action is $S_{\text{int}}[q] = \int dt (-\frac{g}{6} q^3)$. The Feynman rule for the $q^3$ vertex is $(-1) \times (-\frac{g}{6}) \times 3! = g$.
    *   **Difference 2: Quantity Calculated:** Example 1 calculates the 1PI 2-point function $\Sigma$. Our problem asks for the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ and the *connected* 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$. While related, $G_2^c$ is the sum of all connected diagrams, which can be expressed in terms of the 1PI self-energy $\Sigma$ via Dyson's equation ($G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 \Sigma G_0 + \dots$).
    *   **Difference 3: 1-point function:** Example 1 does not cover the calculation of the 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$. This requires drawing and evaluating Feynman diagrams with one external leg.

*   **Examples 2 and 3:** These examples are not relevant as they deal with different mathematical and combinatorial problems unrelated to quantum field theory.

---

**Solution:**

**General Setup:**

The Euclidean action is given by $S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} (\partial_t q)^2 + U(q) \right)$.
With $U(q) = \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$, the action is:
$S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} (\partial_t q)^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.

The action is split into a free part $S_0[q]$ and an interaction part $S_{\text{int}}[q]$:
$S_0[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} (\partial_t q)^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 \right)$
$S_{\text{int}}[q] = \int dt \left( - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$

The **free propagator** (Green's function of the free theory) is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

The **Feynman rule for the vertex** (from $S_{\text{int}}$):
The interaction term is $- \frac{g}{6} \int dt \, q(t)^3$. The vertex factor is $g$. Each vertex is integrated over time $t_v$.

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$.**

The 1-point function $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is the sum of all connected Feynman diagrams with one external leg at time $t$.

*   **Order $g^0$:** No vertices. A single external leg is not a connected diagram contributing to $\langle q(t) \rangle$. So, $G_1^{(0)}(t) = 0$.

*   **Order $g^1$ (Leading Term):** One vertex. The vertex has three legs. One leg connects to the external point $t$. The remaining two legs must connect to each other to form a loop, making it a connected diagram.
    *   **Diagram:** A single vertex at $t_v$ with one external leg at $t$ and one internal loop.
    *   **Contribution:** The diagram represents the term $\langle q(t) S_{\text{int}} \rangle_0 / \langle 1 \rangle_0$.
        Using Wick's theorem for $\langle q(t) (-\frac{g}{6}) \int dt_v q(t_v)^3 \rangle_0$:
        $q(t)$ contracts with one $q(t_v)$ (3 ways).
        The remaining two $q(t_v)$'s contract with each other (1 way).
        Total $3 \times 1 = 3$ contractions.
        The prefactor for the diagram is $3 \times (-\frac{g}{6}) = -\frac{g}{2}$.
    *   **Expression:** $-\frac{g}{2} \int dt_v \, G_0(t, t_v) \, G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    *   **Evaluate $G_0(t_v, t_v)$:** $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v-t_v|} = \frac{1}{2m}$.
    *   **Evaluate the integral:** $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) = \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} e^{i\omega t} G_0(\omega) \int dt_v e^{-i\omega t_v} = G_0(\omega=0) = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
        (This is the zero-momentum free propagator, representing a constant shift.)
    *   **Result:** $G_1^{(1)}(t) = -\frac{g}{2} \times \frac{1}{2m} \times \frac{1}{m^2} = -\frac{g}{4m^3}$.
    This is the leading (lowest order in $g$) non-zero term for $G_1(t)$.

**Answer for Part (i):**
The leading term of the 1-point function is $\boxed{G_1(t) = -\frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2)}$.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function is the sum of all connected diagrams with two external legs at $t_1$ and $t_2$. We use the Dyson series expansion: $G_2^c = G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 + G_0 \Sigma G_0 \Sigma G_0 + \dots$, where $\Sigma$ is the 1PI self-energy.

*   **Order $g^0$:**
    This is the free propagator, with no vertices.
    $G_2^{c,(0)}(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$.

*   **Order $g^1$:**
    This term comes from $G_0 \Sigma^{(1)} G_0$. We first need $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b)$.
    *   **$\Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b)$:** The 1PI self-energy at $O(g)$. This is a single vertex where two legs are 'external' (connected to $t_a, t_b$) and the third leg forms a loop.
        The vertex factor is $g$. The internal loop contributes $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$.
        There is no symmetry factor for this diagram (the external legs are distinguishable, and the loop is unique). The $\delta$-function expresses locality.
        $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) = g \times G_0(t_a, t_a) \times \delta(t_a - t_b) = \frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_a - t_b)$.
    *   **Contribution to $G_2^c$:**
        $G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, \Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) \, G_0(t_b, t_2)$
        $= \int dt_a dt_b \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, \frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_a - t_b) \, G_0(t_b, t_2)$
        $= \frac{g}{2m} \int dt_a \, G_0(t_1, t_a) \, G_0(t_a, t_2)$.
    *   **Evaluation of the integral:** The integral $\int dt_a G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2)$ is the inverse Fourier transform of $G_0(\omega)^2 = \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2}$.
        This integral evaluates to $\frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
    *   **Result:** $G_2^{c,(1)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g}{2m} \times \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
        $= \frac{g}{8m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

*   **Order $g^2$:**
    This order includes two types of contributions from the Dyson series: $G_0 \Sigma^{(2)} G_0$ and $G_0 \Sigma^{(1)} G_0 \Sigma^{(1)} G_0$.

    1.  **Contribution from $\Sigma^{(2)}$ (the "bubble" diagram):**
        *   **$\Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b)$:** The lowest order 1PI diagram with two vertices and two external legs. This is a "bubble" diagram where two vertices ($V_A, V_B$) are connected by two internal propagators, and the external legs connect to $V_A$ and $V_B$.
            The expansion term is $\frac{1}{2!} \left(-\frac{g}{6}\right)^2 \int dt_A dt_B q(t_A)^3 q(t_B)^3$.
            The coefficient for this diagram from Wick's theorem: $q(t_a)$ contracts with one $q(t_A)$ (3 ways), $q(t_b)$ contracts with one $q(t_B)$ (3 ways). The remaining two $q(t_A)$'s contract with the remaining two $q(t_B)$'s ($2! = 2$ ways). Total contractions: $3 \times 3 \times 2 = 18$.
            The overall factor is $18 \times \frac{1}{2} \frac{g^2}{36} = \frac{g^2}{4}$.
        *   **Expression:** $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b) = \frac{g^2}{4} [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^2$.
        *   **Contribution to $G_2^c$:**
            $G_2^{c,(2,a)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_A dt_B \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, \left( \frac{g^2}{4} [G_0(t_A, t_B)]^2 \right) \, G_0(t_B, t_2)$.
            This integral is $\frac{g^2}{4} \int dt_A dt_B \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, [G_0(t_A, t_B)]^2 \, G_0(t_B, t_2)$.
            In momentum space, this corresponds to $G_0(\omega)^4$.
            The inverse Fourier transform of $1/(\omega^2+m^2)^4$ is $\frac{1}{48m^7} (1+m|t| + \frac{m^2 t^2}{3} + \frac{m^3 |t|^3}{15}) e^{-m|t|}$.
        *   **Result:** $G_2^{c,(2,a)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{4} \times \frac{1}{48m^7} (1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3} + \frac{m^3|t_1-t_2|^3}{15}) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
            $= \frac{g^2}{192m^7} (1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3} + \frac{m^3|t_1-t_2|^3}{15}) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

    2.  **Contribution from $G_0 \Sigma^{(1)} G_0 \Sigma^{(1)} G_0$ (two tadpole insertions):**
        This represents two insertions of the $O(g)$ self-energy into the free propagator chain.
        $G_2^{c,(2,b)}(t_1, t_2) = \int dt_A dt_B dt_C dt_D \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, \Sigma^{(1)}(t_A, t_B) \, G_0(t_B, t_C) \, \Sigma^{(1)}(t_C, t_D) \, G_0(t_D, t_2)$.
        Using $\Sigma^{(1)}(t_a, t_b) = \frac{g}{2m} \delta(t_a - t_b)$:
        $= \left(\frac{g}{2m}\right)^2 \int dt_A dt_C \, G_0(t_1, t_A) \, G_0(t_A, t_C) \, G_0(t_C, t_2)$.
        In momentum space, this corresponds to $G_0(\omega)^3$.
        The inverse Fourier transform of $1/(\omega^2+m^2)^3$ is $\frac{1}{16m^5} (1+m|t| + \frac{m^2 t^2}{3}) e^{-m|t|}$.
        *   **Result:** $G_2^{c,(2,b)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{4m^2} \times \frac{1}{16m^5} (1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3}) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
            $= \frac{g^2}{64m^7} (1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3}) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

    3.  **Total $O(g^2)$ contribution:** Summing the two parts:
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \left[ \frac{1}{192m^7} \left(1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3} + \frac{m^3|t_1-t_2|^3}{15}\right) \right.$
        $\left. + \frac{1}{64m^7} \left(1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3}\right) \right] g^2 e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        Combine coefficients: $\frac{1}{192} + \frac{3}{192} = \frac{4}{192} = \frac{1}{48}$.
        $G_2^{c,(2)}(t_1, t_2) = \frac{g^2}{192m^7} \left[ (1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3} + \frac{m^3|t_1-t_2|^3}{15}) + 3(1+m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3}) \right] e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
        $= \frac{g^2}{192m^7} \left[ 4 + 4m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{4}{3}m^2(t_1-t_2)^2 + \frac{1}{15}m^3|t_1-t_2|^3 \right] e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$
        $= \frac{g^2}{48m^7} \left[ 1 + m|t_1-t_2| + \frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3} + \frac{m^3|t_1-t_2|^3}{60} \right] e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

**Answer for Part (ii):**
The connected 2-point function up to $O(g^2)$ is:
$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m}e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g}{8m^4}(1+m|t_1-t_2|)e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g^2}{48m^7}\left(1+m|t_1-t_2|+\frac{m^2(t_1-t_2)^2}{3}+\frac{m^3|t_1-t_2|^3}{60}\right)e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + O(g^3)}$
