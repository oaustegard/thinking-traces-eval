This problem combines a quantum field theory calculation (1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ in 0D) with a graph-theoretic counting problem for labeled graphs.

**Relevant Examples and Differences:**

1.  **Example 2: 1-Loop Effective Action** is highly relevant.
    *   **Similarity:** It deals with a 0D QFT action of the form $S(x) = x^2/2 - S_{\text{int}}(x)$ and computes a 1-loop quantity. The method relies on the second derivative of the action, $S''(x)$.
    *   **Difference:** Example 2 calculates the 1-loop effective action $S_1(x_c)$, while the main problem asks for the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$. The interaction term is also different: Example 2 has $S_{\text{int}}(x) = gx^3/6$, while the main problem has $S_{\text{int}}(x) = g(x^2 + x^3/6)$.

2.  **Example 1: EGF for Restricted-Valence Labeled Trees** is partially relevant for the combinatorial part.
    *   **Similarity:** It involves counting labeled graphs with valence restrictions.
    *   **Difference:** Example 1 counts trees, while the main problem counts 1-loop graphs. It uses EGFs and implicit equations, which are not directly applied in the counting method for the main problem, but the general idea of combinatorial enumeration is shared.

**Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

The partition function is $Z = \int dx e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$. The free partition function is $Z_0 = \int dx e^{-S_0(x)/\hbar}$ where $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ for a 0D QFT is given by the formula:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -\frac{1}{2} \log \left( \frac{S''(x_c)}{S_0''(x_0)} \right)$, where $x_c$ is the classical saddle point of $S(x)$ and $x_0$ is the classical saddle point of $S_0(x)$. We typically expand around $g=0$, so $x_c \approx x_0 = 0$.

First, let's find the derivatives of the action $S(x) = x^2/2 - g(x^2 + x^3/6)$:
$S(x) = \frac{1-2g}{2} x^2 - \frac{g}{6} x^3$.

The free action is $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
So, $S_0'(x) = x$, and $S_0''(x) = 1$. The saddle point for $S_0(x)$ is $x_0=0$.

For the full action $S(x)$:
$S'(x) = (1-2g)x - \frac{g}{2}x^2$.
The saddle points are solutions to $S'(x)=0$, which are $x=0$ or $x = \frac{2(1-2g)}{g}$.
For small $g$, the relevant saddle point for the perturbation expansion around the free theory is $x_c=0$.

Now, compute the second derivative of $S(x)$:
$S''(x) = (1-2g) - gx$.
At the saddle point $x_c=0$, we have $S''(0) = 1-2g$.

Using the 1-loop formula for $\log(Z/Z_0)$:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -\frac{1}{2} \log \left( \frac{S''(0)}{S_0''(0)} \right) = -\frac{1}{2} \log \left( \frac{1-2g}{1} \right) = -\frac{1}{2} \log(1-2g)$.

This can be expanded as a series for small $g$:
$-\frac{1}{2} \log(1-2g) = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{k+1}(-2g)^k}{k} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(2g)^k}{k} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{-(2g)^k}{2k}$.
The first term is $-g$.

**Answer for Part 1:** $\boxed{-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)}$

**Part 2: Number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only (be careful with double edges and self-loops).**

A 1-loop graph is a connected graph with $n$ vertices and $n$ edges.
Let $n_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $n_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
The total number of vertices is $n = n_1 + n_3$.
The sum of degrees is $1 \cdot n_1 + 3 \cdot n_3 = 2E = 2n$.
Substitute $n_1 = n - n_3$:
$(n - n_3) + 3n_3 = 2n$
$n + 2n_3 = 2n$
$2n_3 = n$.
This implies that $n$ must be an even number. If $n$ is odd, there are no such graphs.
If $n$ is even, then $n_3 = n/2$, and $n_1 = n - n/2 = n/2$.
So, any such graph must have $n/2$ 1-valent vertices and $n/2$ 3-valent vertices.

A 1-loop graph consists of a unique cycle with trees attached to its vertices. Since all vertices are either 1-valent or 3-valent, and there are no 2-valent vertices, the cycle itself must be formed by 3-valent vertices. Each 3-valent vertex in the cycle uses two of its edges to participate in the cycle. The remaining one edge must connect to a 1-valent vertex, as 1-valent vertices are the only other type available and must be leaves.
Therefore, the graph structure must be a cycle of $k = n/2$ 3-valent vertices, with each 3-valent vertex having a unique 1-valent vertex attached to it.

Let $k = n/2$. The number of labeled graphs $N(n)$ is determined as follows:

1.  **Choose the $k$ vertices that will be 3-valent (and thus form the cycle) and the $k$ vertices that will be 1-valent (leaves).**
    There are $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{2k}{k}$ ways to do this.

2.  **Connect each of the $k$ 1-valent vertices to one of the $k$ 3-valent vertices.**
    There are $k!$ ways to pair up the 1-valent vertices with the 3-valent vertices.

3.  **Form the "cycle" using the remaining $k$ degrees of the $k$ 3-valent vertices.**
    This step depends on the value of $k$:
    *   **Case $k=1$ ($n=2$):** One 3-valent vertex $v$ and one 1-valent vertex $u$. The edge $(u,v)$ uses one degree of $v$. The remaining two degrees of $v$ must form a self-loop on $v$ (a double edge between $v$ and itself). There is only **1** way to form such a "cycle" (a double self-loop).
    *   **Case $k=2$ ($n=4$):** Two 3-valent vertices $v_1, v_2$ and two 1-valent vertices $u_1, u_2$. After connecting $u_1$ to $v_1$ and $u_2$ to $v_2$ (or $u_1$ to $v_2$ and $u_2$ to $v_1$), each $v_i$ has one degree remaining. These must form a double edge between $v_1$ and $v_2$. There is only **1** way to form such a "cycle" (a double edge between $v_1$ and $v_2$).
    *   **Case $k \ge 3$ ($n \ge 6$):** The $k$ 3-valent vertices must form a simple cycle using their remaining degrees. The number of ways to form a simple cycle on $k$ distinct labeled vertices is $(k-1)!/2$.

Combining these steps:

*   **For $n=2$ ($k=1$):**
    $N(2) = \binom{2}{1} \times 1! \times 1 = 2 \times 1 \times 1 = 2$.
    (Example: Vertices 1, 2. If 1 is 3-valent, 2 is 1-valent: $2-1\circlearrowleft$. If 2 is 3-valent, 1 is 1-valent: $1-2\circlearrowleft$.)

*   **For $n=4$ ($k=2$):**
    $N(4) = \binom{4}{2} \times 2! \times 1 = 6 \times 2 \times 1 = 12$.
    (Example: Vertices 1,2,3,4. Choose {1,2} as 3-valent, {3,4} as 1-valent. Pair (1,3), (2,4). Connect 1 and 2 with a double edge. This is one graph. Permute pairings: $2!$. Choose 3-valent vertices: $\binom{4}{2}=6$. Total $6 \times 2 = 12$.)

*   **For $n \ge 6$ ($k \ge 3$):**
    $N(n) = \binom{n}{n/2} \times (n/2)! \times \frac{(n/2 - 1)!}{2}$.
    Substituting $k=n/2$:
    $N(2k) = \binom{2k}{k} k! \frac{(k-1)!}{2} = \frac{(2k)!}{k! k!} k! \frac{(k-1)!}{2} = \frac{(2k)! (k-1)!}{2k!}$.

The number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs is:
*   $N(n) = 0$ if $n$ is odd.
*   $N(2) = 2$
*   $N(4) = 12$
*   $N(n) = \frac{n! ((n/2)-1)!}{2(n/2)!}$ for $n \ge 6$ (where $n$ is even).

**Answer for Part 2:**
$\boxed{N(n) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \\ 2 & \text{if } n=2 \\ 12 & \text{if } n=4 \\ \frac{n! ((n/2)-1)!}{2(n/2)!} & \text{if } n \text{ is even and } n \ge 6 \end{cases}}$
