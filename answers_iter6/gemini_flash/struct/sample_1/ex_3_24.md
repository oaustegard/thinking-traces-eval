**Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

(a) **Relevant examples:**
*   **Example 2: 1-Loop Effective Action** is highly relevant. It demonstrates how to compute the 1-loop contribution to the effective action, which is directly related to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ in the context of quantum mechanics (single variable integral). The method involves analyzing the quadratic part of the action at the classical minimum.

(b) **Differences from Example 2:**
*   The action in the main problem is $S(x) = x^2/2 - g(x^2 + x^3/6)$, which includes an additional $-gx^2$ term compared to $S(x) = x^2/2 - gx^3/6$ in Example 2. This term modifies the quadratic (mass) part of the action.
*   The goal is specifically $\log(Z/Z_0)$, where $Z = \int dx \, e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$ and $Z_0$ is the free partition function ($g=0$). This quantity is often interpreted as the 1-loop correction to the vacuum energy or free energy.

**Reasoning for Part 1:**
The partition function is given by $Z = \int dx \, e^{-S(x)/\hbar}$. The free partition function $Z_0$ corresponds to $g=0$, so $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
For a single-variable integral in quantum mechanics, the 1-loop contribution to $\log Z$ is obtained by expanding the action around its classical minimum $x_c$ (where $S'(x_c)=0$) and performing a Gaussian integral.
The 1-loop expression for $\log Z$ is approximately $-\frac{1}{\hbar}S(x_c) - \frac{1}{2}\log S''(x_c) + \text{const}$.

First, let's rewrite the given action:
$S(x) = \frac{x^2}{2} - g x^2 - \frac{g x^3}{6} = \frac{(1-2g)x^2}{2} - \frac{g x^3}{6}$.

Next, we find the classical minimum $x_c$ by setting the first derivative to zero:
$S'(x) = (1-2g)x - \frac{g x^2}{2}$.
$S'(x_c) = x_c \left( (1-2g) - \frac{g x_c}{2} \right) = 0$.
This gives two possible classical solutions: $x_c = 0$ or $x_c = \frac{2(1-2g)}{g}$.
For an expansion around $g=0$, the relevant saddle point is $x_c=0$.

Now, we compute the second derivative of the action:
$S''(x) = (1-2g) - g x$.
At the classical minimum $x_c=0$, we have $S''(0) = 1-2g$.

For the free theory ($g=0$), the action is $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.
Its classical minimum is $x_{c,0}=0$.
Its second derivative is $S_0''(x) = 1$, so $S_0''(0) = 1$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is given by:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = \left( -\frac{1}{\hbar}S(0) - \frac{1}{2}\log S''(0) \right) - \left( -\frac{1}{\hbar}S_0(0) - \frac{1}{2}\log S_0''(0) \right)$.
Since $S(0)=0$ and $S_0(0)=0$, and $S_0''(0)=1$ (so $\log S_0''(0) = 0$), this simplifies to:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -\frac{1}{2}\log S''(0) = -\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)$.

This is the 1-loop contribution. For small $g$, we can expand $\log(1-2g) = -2g - \frac{(-2g)^2}{2} - O(g^3)$:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -\frac{1}{2}(-2g - 2g^2 - O(g^3)) = g + g^2 + O(g^3)$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is $\boxed{-\frac{1}{2}\log(1-2g)}$.

**Part 2: Number of labeled n-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only (be careful with double edges and self-loops).**

(a) **Relevant examples:**
*   **Example 1: EGF for Restricted-Valence Labeled Trees** is relevant due to its focus on labeled graphs and restricted vertex valencies, and the use of combinatorial techniques for enumeration.

(b) **Differences from Example 1:**
*   The problem specifies "1-loop graphs" (containing exactly one cycle), which are topologically distinct from trees (acyclic).
*   The allowed valencies are 1 and 3, instead of 1 and 4.
*   The problem asks for the direct number of graphs for a given $n$, not an Exponential Generating Function (EGF). The explicit mention of "double edges and self-loops" implies that cycles of length 1 ($k=1$) and 2 ($k=2$) should be considered.

**Reasoning for Part 2:**
A 1-loop graph is a connected graph with exactly one cycle. All vertices in the graph must have degree 1 or 3.
Let's analyze the structure of such a graph:
1.  **Vertices not on the cycle:** Any vertex not on the cycle must be part of a tree attached to a cycle vertex. If its degree in the graph is 1, it must be a leaf. If its degree is 3, it must be an internal node of degree 3.
2.  **Vertices on the cycle:** Let $v_c$ be a vertex on the cycle. It contributes 2 to its degree from the cycle edges. Its total degree in the graph is $2 + (\text{degree of } v_c \text{ as a root of an attached tree})$. Since the total degree must be 1 or 3, and it's already $\ge 2$, it must be 3. This means $v_c$ must have exactly one additional edge connecting to an attached tree (or a single leaf).
This implies that every vertex on the cycle must have degree 3.
Furthermore, if a cycle vertex $v_c$ has an attached tree $T_c$, then $v_c$ must have degree 1 *within* $T_c$ (as its root). For a tree where all internal vertices are 3-valent and all leaves are 1-valent, if the root has degree 1, it must be a leaf itself. This means $T_c$ must consist only of the vertex $v_c$ (as a leaf) and one edge connecting it to another vertex. This "other vertex" must be a 1-valent vertex in the overall graph.

Therefore, the only possible structure for such a graph is a cycle of $k$ vertices, where each of these $k$ cycle vertices is connected to exactly one additional 1-valent vertex (a leaf).
The total number of vertices $n$ in such a graph is $k$ (cycle vertices) + $k$ (leaf vertices) $= 2k$.
This implies that $n$ must be an even number. If $n$ is odd, the number of such graphs is 0.

Let $N_n$ be the number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices.

*   **Case 1: $n$ is odd.**
    $N_n = 0$.

*   **Case 2: $n$ is even.** Let $n=2k$.
    The construction process for these labeled graphs is:
    1.  Choose $k$ vertices out of $n$ to form the cycle. There are $\binom{n}{k}$ ways.
    2.  Form a labeled cycle on these $k$ chosen vertices. The number of ways depends on $k$:
        *   If $k=1$: A single vertex with a self-loop. There is 1 way.
        *   If $k=2$: Two vertices connected by a double edge. There is 1 way.
        *   If $k \ge 3$: A simple cycle of length $k$. There are $\frac{(k-1)!}{2}$ ways for labeled vertices.
    3.  The remaining $k$ vertices are the leaves. Each must be attached to a unique cycle vertex. There are $k!$ ways to assign these $k$ leaves to the $k$ cycle vertices.

    Combining these steps:
    $N_{2k} = \binom{2k}{k} \times (\text{cycle formation ways for } k \text{ vertices}) \times k!$
    $N_{2k} = \frac{(2k)!}{k!k!} \times (\text{cycle formation ways for } k \text{ vertices}) \times k!$
    $N_{2k} = \frac{(2k)!}{k!} \times (\text{cycle formation ways for } k \text{ vertices})$.

    Applying the specific cycle formation ways:
    *   **If $k=1$ (i.e., $n=2$):**
        $N_2 = \frac{2!}{1!} \times 1 = 2$.
        (E.g., for vertices $\{1,2\}$: Graph 1 has vertex 1 with a self-loop and an edge to vertex 2. Graph 2 has vertex 2 with a self-loop and an edge to vertex 1. Both are valid.)
    *   **If $k=2$ (i.e., $n=4$):**
        $N_4 = \frac{4!}{2!} \times 1 = \frac{24}{2} = 12$.
        (E.g., for vertices $\{1,2,3,4\}$: Choose 2 for the double edge (e.g., $\{1,2\}$) in $\binom{4}{2}=6$ ways. The remaining 2 (e.g., $\{3,4\}$) are leaves. Attach $3$ to $1$ and $4$ to $2$, or $3$ to $2$ and $4$ to $1$. $6 \times 2 = 12$ ways.)
    *   **If $k \ge 3$ (i.e., $n \ge 6$ and even):**
        $N_{2k} = \frac{(2k)!}{k!} \times \frac{(k-1)!}{2} = \frac{(2k)!}{2k}$.
        (E.g., for $n=6, k=3$: $N_6 = \frac{6!}{2 \times 3} = \frac{720}{6} = 120$. This matches $\binom{6}{3} \times \frac{(3-1)!}{2} \times 3! = 20 \times 1 \times 6 = 120$.)

**Answer:**
The number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only, denoted by $N_n$, is:
$\boxed{
N_n =
\begin{cases}
    0 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd} \\
    2 & \text{if } n=2 \\
    12 & \text{if } n=4 \\
    \frac{n!}{n/2} = \frac{n!}{k} & \text{if } n \ge 6 \text{ is even (where } k=n/2 \text{)}
\end{cases}
}$
