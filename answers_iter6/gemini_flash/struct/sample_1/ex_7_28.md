The problem asks for calculations in Euclidean quantum mechanics for a cubic anharmonic oscillator. We will use Feynman diagrams to compute the 1-point and connected 2-point functions.

**Relevant Examples and Differences:**

*   **Example 1 (Quantum Particle with $q^4$ potential):** This example is highly relevant as it demonstrates the calculation of Feynman diagrams for correlation functions in Euclidean quantum mechanics. It sets up the Euclidean action, free propagator, and Feynman rules, and proceeds to calculate 1PI diagrams at different orders.
*   **Differences from Example 1:**
    1.  **Interaction Term:** Example 1 has a $q^4$ interaction, leading to a 4-point vertex. Our problem has a $q^3$ interaction, which yields a 3-point vertex. This changes the Feynman rules for the vertex factor and the topology of diagrams.
    2.  **Calculated Function:** Example 1 calculates the 1-particle irreducible (1PI) 2-point function $\Sigma(t_1,t_2)$. Our problem asks for the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ and the *connected* 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$. The $q^3$ interaction leads to a non-zero $G_1(t)$ (tadpole diagrams), which is not present in $q^4$ theory (without a source term). Also, $G_2^c$ includes all connected diagrams, not just 1PI ones.
    3.  **Order of Calculation:** For $G_1(t)$, we need the leading term ($O(g^1)$). For $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$, we need terms up to $O(g^2)$.

**Step 1: Establish Euclidean Action and Feynman Rules**

The Euclidean action for the given potential $U(q) = \tfrac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \tfrac{g q^3}{6}$ is:
$S[q] = \int dt \left( \tfrac12 \dot q^2 + \tfrac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \tfrac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.

*   **Free Propagator:** $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1 - t_2|}$. In momentum space, $\tilde G_0(p) = \frac{1}{p^2+m^2}$.
*   **Interaction Term:** $S_I = -\frac{g}{6} \int dt q^3(t)$.
*   **Feynman Vertex Rule:** For a $q^k$ interaction term $c_k \int dt q^k(t)$, the vertex factor is $-k! \times c_k$. Here, $k=3$ and $c_3 = -g/6$. So, the vertex factor for a $q^3$ vertex is $-3! \times (-g/6) = -6 \times (-g/6) = g$. Each 3-point vertex carries a factor $g$.

We will use the following standard Fourier transforms for integrals:
*   $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) = \frac{1}{m^2}$ (this is the zero-momentum component, $\tilde G_0(0)$).
*   $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip\tau}}{(p^2+M^2)^2} = \frac{1}{4M^3} (1+M|\tau|)e^{-M|\tau|}$.
*   $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip\tau}}{(p^2+M^2)^3} = \frac{1}{32M^5} (3+3M|\tau|+M^2\tau^2)e^{-M|\tau|}$.
*   $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip\tau}}{(p^2+M_1^2)^2(p^2+M_2^2)} = \frac{1}{(M_2^2-M_1^2)^2} \left[ \frac{1}{2M_2} e^{-M_2|\tau|} - \frac{1}{2M_1} e^{-M_1|\tau|} (1 - \frac{M_2^2-M_1^2}{2M_1^2}(1+M_1|\tau|)) \right]$.
    More simply, for $M_1=m, M_2=3m$:
    $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(p^2+m^2)^2(p^2+9m^2)} \right] = \frac{3+4m|\tau|}{128m^5} e^{-m|\tau|} + \frac{1}{384m^5} e^{-3m|\tau|}$.

**Part (i): Calculate $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ to leading order in $g$.**

The leading order term for $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is $O(g^1)$. This corresponds to the simplest connected diagram with one external leg.
*   **Diagram:** A single 3-point vertex at time $t_v$, with one external leg at $t$ and the other two internal legs forming a loop connecting back to the same vertex. This is a tadpole diagram.
*   **Feynman Rules Application:**
    *   Vertex factor: $g$.
    *   Propagators: $G_0(t, t_v)$ for the external leg, $G_0(t_v, t_v)$ for each of the two internal legs forming the loop.
    *   Integration over internal vertex time: $\int dt_v$.
    *   Symmetry Factor: The vertex has 3 legs. One connects to the external line. The remaining two form a loop. There are 3 ways to choose which leg connects to the external line. The two internal legs forming a loop are indistinguishable, so we divide by 2. Thus, the symmetry factor for the diagram is $1/2$.
*   **Calculation:**
    $G_1(t)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$
    First, $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_v-t_v|} = \frac{1}{2m}$.
    Next, $\int dt_v G_0(t, t_v) = \int dt_v \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip(t-t_v)}}{p^2+m^2} = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ipt}}{p^2+m^2} (2\pi \delta(p)) = \frac{1}{0^2+m^2} = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
    This is the zero-momentum component, indicating that $\langle q(t) \rangle$ is a constant in time due to translational invariance.
    So, $G_1(t)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2m} \cdot \frac{1}{m^2} = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.

**Answer for (i):** $\boxed{\langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2)}$

**Part (ii): Calculate $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

This requires summing all connected diagrams with two external legs at $t_1$ and $t_2$, up to $O(g^2)$. Let $\tau = |t_1-t_2|$.

**1. $O(g^0)$ Term:**
*   This is the free propagator:
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(0)} = G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m\tau}$.

**2. $O(g^1)$ Term:**
*   **Diagram:** A single 3-point vertex at $t_v$, with two external legs at $t_1, t_2$ and one internal leg forming a loop.
*   **Feynman Rules Application:**
    *   Vertex factor: $g$.
    *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_v)$, $G_0(t_2, t_v)$, $G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    *   Integration over $t_v$: $\int dt_v$.
    *   Symmetry Factor: The vertex has 3 legs. Two connect to external lines $t_1, t_2$. The remaining one forms a loop. There are 3 ways to choose which leg forms the loop. The loop itself has a symmetry factor of 2. So the diagram has a factor of $1/2$.
*   **Calculation:**
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{2} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) G_0(t_v, t_v)$.
    Using $G_0(t_v, t_v) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
    $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v)$.
    The integral $\int dt_v G_0(t_1, t_v) G_0(t_2, t_v) = \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip(t_1-t_2)}}{(p^2+m^2)^2} = \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m\tau)e^{-m\tau}$.
    So, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(1)} = \frac{g}{4m} \cdot \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m\tau)e^{-m\tau} = \frac{g}{16m^4} (1+m\tau)e^{-m\tau}$.

**3. $O(g^2)$ Terms:**
There are three distinct connected diagrams with two vertices:

*   **Diagram 1: "Double Tadpole"**
    *   **Topology:** Two vertices $V_A$ (at $t_a$) and $V_B$ (at $t_b$) connected by a single propagator $G_0(t_a, t_b)$. $V_A$ also connects to $t_1$ and forms a loop. $V_B$ also connects to $t_2$ and forms a loop.
    *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
        *   Vertex factors: $g \times g = g^2$.
        *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_a)$, $G_0(t_a, t_b)$, $G_0(t_b, t_2)$, $G_0(t_a, t_a)$ (loop at $V_A$), $G_0(t_b, t_b)$ (loop at $V_B$).
        *   Integrations: $\int dt_a dt_b$.
        *   Symmetry Factor: Each loop $G_0(t_a, t_a)$ and $G_0(t_b, t_b)$ contributes a factor of $1/2$. There are no further symmetries (vertices are distinguished by external legs). Total symmetry factor $1/4$.
    *   **Calculation:**
        $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,1)} = \frac{g^2}{4} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2) G_0(t_a, t_a) G_0(t_b, t_b)$.
        Using $G_0(t_a, t_a) = G_0(t_b, t_b) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
        $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,1)} = \frac{g^2}{4} \left(\frac{1}{2m}\right)^2 \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        The integral is $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip\tau}}{(p^2+m^2)^3} = \frac{1}{32m^5} (3+3m\tau+m^2\tau^2)e^{-m\tau}$.
        So, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,1)} = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \frac{1}{32m^5} (3+3m\tau+m^2\tau^2)e^{-m\tau} = \frac{g^2}{512m^7} (3+3m\tau+m^2\tau^2)e^{-m\tau}$.

*   **Diagram 2: "Tadpole Insertion"**
    *   **Topology:** A propagator $G_0(t_1, t_2)$ with an $O(g^1)$ tadpole loop inserted into it. This means one external leg of $V_A$ connects to $t_1$, another to $t_2$, and the third leg connects to $V_B$. $V_B$ then forms a tadpole (one leg connects to $V_A$, two legs form a loop).
    *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
        *   Vertex factors: $g \times g = g^2$.
        *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_a)$, $G_0(t_a, t_2)$, $G_0(t_a, t_b)$, $G_0(t_b, t_b)$ (loop at $V_B$).
        *   Integrations: $\int dt_a dt_b$.
        *   Symmetry Factor: The loop at $V_B$ implies a factor of $1/2$. No other symmetries. Total $1/2$.
    *   **Calculation:**
        $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,2)} = \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) G_0(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_b)$.
        Using $G_0(t_b, t_b) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
        $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,2)} = \frac{g^2}{4m} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2) G_0(t_a, t_b)$.
        The integral $\int dt_b G_0(t_a, t_b) = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
        So, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,2)} = \frac{g^2}{4m^3} \int dt_a G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_2)$.
        The integral is $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ip\tau}}{(p^2+m^2)^2} = \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m\tau)e^{-m\tau}$.
        So, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,2)} = \frac{g^2}{4m^3} \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m\tau)e^{-m\tau} = \frac{g^2}{16m^6} (1+m\tau)e^{-m\tau}$.

*   **Diagram 3: "Sunset" (1PI Self-Energy Insertion)**
    *   **Topology:** Two vertices $V_A$ (at $t_a$) and $V_B$ (at $t_b$) connected by *three* propagators. External legs connect $t_1$ to $V_A$ and $V_B$ to $t_2$. This is a 1PI self-energy diagram inserted into a free propagator.
    *   **Feynman Rules Application:**
        *   Vertex factors: $g \times g = g^2$.
        *   Propagators: $G_0(t_1, t_a)$, $G_0(t_b, t_2)$, and three $G_0(t_a, t_b)$'s.
        *   Integrations: $\int dt_a dt_b$.
        *   Symmetry Factor: The three internal propagators connecting $V_A$ and $V_B$ are indistinguishable, contributing a factor of $1/3! = 1/6$. Total $1/6$.
    *   **Calculation:**
        $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,3)} = \frac{g^2}{6} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^3 G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        The term $\int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) [G_0(t_a, t_b)]^3 G_0(t_b, t_2)$ is the Fourier transform of $\frac{1}{(p^2+m^2)^2} \frac{1}{p^2+(3m)^2}$.
        Using the partial fraction result derived in thought process:
        $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(p^2+m^2)^2(p^2+9m^2)} \right] = \frac{3+4m\tau}{128m^5} e^{-m\tau} + \frac{1}{384m^5} e^{-3m\tau}$.
        So, $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)^{(2,3)} = \frac{g^2}{6} \left( \frac{3+4m\tau}{128m^5} e^{-m\tau} + \frac{1}{384m^5} e^{-3m\tau} \right)$.
        $= \frac{g^2(3+4m\tau)}{768m^5} e^{-m\tau} + \frac{g^2}{2304m^5} e^{-3m\tau}$.
        Wait, I made a mistake in the full previous partial fraction calculation. The previous was for $M_1=m, M_2=3m$.
        The $\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2)$ for $q^4$ in Example 1 was $\frac{g^2}{48 m^3} e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|}$. Here, for $q^3$, the $\Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_1, t_2)$ is $\frac{g^2}{6} [G_0(t_1,t_2)]^3 = \frac{g^2}{48m^3} e^{-3m|t_1-t_2|}$.
        So the full $G_2^c$ term is $\int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) \Sigma^{(2)}_{\text{sunset}}(t_a, t_b) G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{48m^3} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) e^{-3m|t_a-t_b|} G_0(t_b, t_2)$.
        This is $\frac{g^2}{48m^3} \mathcal{F}^{-1}\left[ \frac{1}{(p^2+m^2)(p^2+9m^2)(p^2+m^2)} \right]$.
        $= \frac{g^2}{48m^3} \left( \frac{3+4m\tau}{128m^5} e^{-m\tau} + \frac{1}{384m^5} e^{-3m\tau} \right)$.
