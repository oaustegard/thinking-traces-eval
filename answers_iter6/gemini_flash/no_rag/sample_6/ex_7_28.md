The problem asks for two quantities related to the Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator, defined by the potential $U(q) = \frac{1}{2} m^2 q^2 - \frac{1}{6} g q^3$. The action is $S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + U(q) \right)$. We will use perturbation theory in $g$. The free action (for $g=0$) is $S_0[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{1}{2} m^2 q^2 \right)$, and the interaction action is $S_{int}[q] = - \int dt \frac{1}{6} g q^3$.

The free propagator in Euclidean time, $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0$, is given by:
$G_0(t_1, t_2) = \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{i\omega(t_1-t_2)}}{\omega^2+m^2} = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
A useful special case is $G_0(t, t) = \frac{1}{2m}$.

**Part (i): Calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) := <q(t)>$ with respect to $g$.**

The 1-point function is given by $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle = \frac{\langle q(t) e^{-S_{int}[q]} \rangle_0}{\langle e^{-S_{int}[q]} \rangle_0}$.
We expand $e^{-S_{int}[q]} = 1 - S_{int}[q] + \frac{1}{2} (S_{int}[q])^2 - \dots$.

The numerator is $\langle q(t) (1 - S_{int}[q] + \dots) \rangle_0$.
The leading term is $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ (due to $q \to -q$ symmetry of the free theory).
The next term is $- \langle q(t) S_{int}[q] \rangle_0 = - \langle q(t) (-\frac{g}{6} \int dt' q(t')^3) \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.
Using Wick's theorem for Gaussian integrals, $\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$:
We must contract $q(t)$ with one $q(t')$, and the remaining two $q(t')$s with each other. There are 3 ways to choose which $q(t')$ connects to $q(t)$.
So, $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
The numerator's leading non-zero term is thus $\frac{g}{6} \int dt' (3 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.

The denominator is $\langle e^{-S_{int}[q]} \rangle_0 = 1 - \langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_0 + \dots$.
$\langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_0 = - \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 0$ (again by symmetry, an odd number of fields).
So the denominator is $1 + O(g^2)$.

Therefore, the leading term of $G_1(t)$ is:
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
Since $G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{2m}$ (a constant), we have:
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{2} \frac{1}{2m} \int dt' G_0(t, t')$.
The integral $\int dt' G_0(t, t')$ is the zero-momentum free propagator:
$\int dt' G_0(t, t') = \int dt' \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{i\omega(t-t')}}{\omega^2+m^2} = \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{i\omega t}}{\omega^2+m^2} (2\pi \delta(\omega)) = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
Thus, $G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} \frac{1}{m^2} = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.
As expected for a translationally invariant vacuum, $G_1(t)$ is a constant.

**Part (ii): Calculate the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := <q(t_1) q(t_2)>_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function can be obtained from the generating functional for connected correlation functions, $\ln \mathcal{Z}[J]$, by taking two functional derivatives with respect to the source $J(t)$:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{\delta^2}{\delta J(t_1) \delta J(t_2)} \ln \mathcal{Z}[J] \bigg|_{J=0}$.
Here, $\mathcal{Z}[J] = \mathcal{Z}_0[J] \left\langle e^{-S_{int}[q]} \right\rangle_{0,J}$, where $\langle \dots \rangle_{0,J}$ denotes averaging with the free action and the source term $\int Jq$.
Expanding the logarithm:
$\ln \mathcal{Z}[J] = \ln \mathcal{Z}_0[J] + \ln \left( 1 - \langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J} + \frac{1}{2} \langle S_{int}[q]^2 \rangle_{0,J} - \dots \right)$
$\ln \mathcal{Z}[J] = \ln \mathcal{Z}_0[J] - \langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J} + \frac{1}{2} \langle S_{int}[q]^2 \rangle_{0,J} - \frac{1}{2} (\langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J})^2 + O(g^3)$.

Let's evaluate the derivatives for each term:

1.  **Zeroth order in $g$**: From $\ln \mathcal{Z}_0[J] = \frac{1}{2} \int dt dt' J(t) G_0(t, t') J(t')$.
    $\frac{\delta^2}{\delta J(t_1) \delta J(t_2)} \left( \frac{1}{2} \int dt dt' J(t) G_0(t, t') J(t') \right) \bigg|_{J=0} = G_0(t_1, t_2)$.
    This is the free propagator.

2.  **First order in $g$**: From $- \langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J}$.
    $- \langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt_a \langle q(t_a)^3 \rangle_{0,J}$.
    We need $\frac{\delta^2}{\delta J(t_1) \delta J(t_2)} \left( \frac{g}{6} \int dt_a \langle q(t_a)^3 \rangle_{0,J} \right) \bigg|_{J=0}$.
    Using $\frac{\delta}{\delta J(t_1)} \langle O[q] \rangle_{0,J} = \langle O[q] q(t_1) \rangle_{0,J}$ and setting $J=0$:
    $\frac{g}{6} \int dt_a \langle q(t_a)^3 q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0$.
    By Wick's theorem, the connected contractions are those where $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$ connect to $q(t_a)$, and the remaining $q(t_a)$'s connect to each other.
    The number of ways to connect $q(t_1)$ to one $q(t_a)$, $q(t_2)$ to another $q(t_a)$, and the remaining $q(t_a)$ to itself is $3 \times 2 = 6$.
    So, $\langle q(t_a)^3 q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = 6 G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_a)$.
    The $O(g)$ contribution is $g \int dt_a G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_a) G_0(t_a, t_a)$.
    This can be expressed using $G_0(t_a,t_a) = \frac{1}{2m}$ and $\int dt_a G_0(t_1,t_a)G_0(t_2,t_a) = \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{e^{i\omega(t_1-t_2)}}{(\omega^2+m^2)^2} = -\frac{\partial}{\partial m^2} G_0(t_1,t_2)$.
    So, $g \frac{1}{2m} \left(-\frac{\partial}{\partial m^2} G_0(t_1,t_2) \right) = \frac{g}{2m} \left( \frac{1}{4m^3} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} \right) = \frac{g}{8m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

3.  **Second order in $g$**: From $\frac{1}{2} \langle S_{int}[q]^2 \rangle_{0,J}$ and $- \frac{1}{2} (\langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J})^2$.
    The $\ln \mathcal{Z}[J]$ formalism automatically sums only connected diagrams.
    The term $- \frac{1}{2} (\langle S_{int}[q] \rangle_{0,J})^2$ generates disconnected diagrams (products of 1-point functions), so it should not contribute to $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ (as $\langle q(t_1) \rangle \langle q(t_2) \rangle$ is explicitly subtracted in the definition of connected functions). Indeed, evaluating the derivatives at $J=0$ for this term yields $- \frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{\delta}{\delta J(t_1)} \langle S_{int} \rangle_{0,J} \right) \left( \frac{\delta}{\delta J(t_2)} \langle S_{int} \rangle_{0,J} \right) |_{J=0} = - \frac{1}{2} G_1(t_1) G_1(t_2)$, which is $O(g^2)$ but disconnected.

    The only $O(g^2)$ connected contribution comes from $\frac{1}{2} \langle S_{int}[q]^2 \rangle_{0,J}$.
    $\frac{1}{2} (\frac{g}{6})^2 \int dt_a dt_b \langle q(t_a)^3 q(t_b)^3 \rangle_{0,J}$.
    Taking the derivatives and setting $J=0$:
    $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt_a dt_b \langle q(t_a)^3 q(t_b)^3 q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0$.
    We need to find the connected contractions for this 8-point function. The relevant diagram is the one-loop self-energy correction (often called "figure-eight" or "sunset" in 0+1D).
    It connects $q(t_1)$ to one $q(t_a)$, $q(t_2)$ to one $q(t_b)$, and the remaining two $q(t_a)$'s connect to the remaining two $q(t_b)$'s, forming two loops between $t_a$ and $t_b$.
    Number of ways: $3$ ways for $q(t_1)$ to $q(t_a)$, $3$ ways for $q(t_2)$ to $q(t_b)$. Then $2$ ways for one remaining $q(t_a)$ to $q(t_b)$, and $1$ way for the other remaining $q(t_a)$ to the other $q(t_b)$. This means $3 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1 = 18$ ways. No, this means $3 \times 3 \times (2 \times 2) = 36$ ways.
    The diagram is $G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_b) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2$.
    So the $O(g^2)$ contribution is $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt_a dt_b \left( 36 G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_2, t_b) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2 \right)$.
    $= \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_a dt_b G_0(t_1, t_a) G_0(t_b, t_2) G_0(t_a, t_b)^2$.
    This term represents the self-energy insertion $\Sigma^{(2)}(t_a, t_b) = \frac{g^2}{2} G_0(t_a, t_b)^2$.
    In momentum space, this is $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} e^{ip(t_1-t_2)} G_0(p) \Sigma^{(2)}(p) G_0(p)$, where $G_0(p) = \frac{1}{p^2+m^2}$.
    $\Sigma^{(2)}(p) = \frac{g^2}{2} \int \frac{d\omega}{2\pi} \frac{1}{(\omega^2+m^2)((p-\omega)^2+m^2)}$.
    Using Feynman parametrization and integrating over $\omega$:
    $\Sigma^{(2)}(p) = \frac{g^2}{8} \int_0^1 dx \frac{1}{(m^2+p^2 x(1-x))^{3/2}}$.
    The $O(g^2)$ term in position space is then:
    $\int \frac{dp}{2\pi} e^{ip(t_1-t_2)} \frac{1}{(p^2+m^2)^2} \left( \frac{g^2}{8} \int_0^1 dx \frac{1}{(m^2+p^2 x(1-x))^{3/2}} \right)$.

Combining these terms, the connected 2-point function up to $O(g^2)$ is:

$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g}{8m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} e^{ip(t_1-t_2)} \frac{g^2}{8(p^2+m^2)^2} \int_0^1 dx \frac{1}{(m^2+p^2 x(1-x))^{3/2}}$.

The final answer is:
(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is:
$\boxed{G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^3}}$

(ii) The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$ is:
$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \frac{g}{8m^4} (1+m|t_1-t_2|) e^{-m|t_1-t_2|} + \int \frac{dp}{2\pi} e^{ip(t_1-t_2)} \frac{g^2}{8(p^2+m^2)^2} \int_0^1 dx \frac{1}{(m^2+p^2 x(1-x))^{3/2}}}$
