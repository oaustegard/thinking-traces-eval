The problem asks for the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to order $g^2$, for a Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator with potential $U(q) = \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$.

The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + U(q) \right) = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6} \right)$.
We split the action into a free part $S_0[q]$ and an interaction part $S_{int}[q]$:
$S_0[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} \right)$
$S_{int}[q] = - \int dt' \frac{g q(t')^3}{6}$

Expectation values are calculated using perturbation theory in $g$:
$\langle O \rangle = \frac{\langle O e^{-S_{int}} \rangle_0}{\langle e^{-S_{int}} \rangle_0}$, where $\langle \dots \rangle_0$ denotes expectation values with respect to the free action $S_0$.
We use the expansion $e^{-S_{int}} = 1 - S_{int} + \frac{1}{2} S_{int}^2 - \dots$.
The free Feynman propagator for the harmonic oscillator is $G_F(t, t') = \langle q(t) q(t') \rangle_0$. It satisfies $(-\partial_t^2 + m^2) G_F(t, t') = \delta(t-t')$.
In Fourier space, $\tilde{G}_F(k) = \frac{1}{k^2+m^2}$.
Transforming back to position space, $G_F(t, t') = \int \frac{dk}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ik(t-t')}}{k^2+m^2} = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t'|}$.
Crucially, we use the following properties for calculations:
1. $G_F(t, t) = \lim_{t' \to t} \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t'|} = \frac{1}{2m}$. This represents the expectation value $\langle q(t)^2 \rangle_0$.
2. $\int dt' G_F(t, t') = \int dt' \int \frac{dk}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ik(t-t')}}{k^2+m^2} = \int \frac{dk}{2\pi} \frac{e^{ikt}}{k^2+m^2} (2\pi \delta(k)) = \frac{1}{m^2}$. This is the zero-momentum propagator.

---
**(i) Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$**

$G_1(t) = \frac{\langle q(t) (1 - S_{int} + \frac{1}{2} S_{int}^2 - \dots) \rangle_0}{\langle 1 - S_{int} + \frac{1}{2} S_{int}^2 - \dots \rangle_0}$

The numerator is $N = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 - \langle q(t) S_{int} \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.
The denominator is $D = 1 - \langle S_{int} \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.

For the free theory, $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ due to the $q \leftrightarrow -q$ symmetry of $S_0$.
Also, $\langle S_{int} \rangle_0 = - \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 0$ as $\langle q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 0$.
So, $D = 1 + O(g^2)$.

The leading non-zero term for $G_1(t)$ must come from the numerator at order $g$:
$- \langle q(t) S_{int} \rangle_0 = - \langle q(t) \left( - \frac{g}{6} \int dt' q(t')^3 \right) \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.
Using Wick's theorem for $\langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$: there are 3 ways to contract $q(t)$ with one of the $q(t')$, and the remaining two $q(t')$ must contract with each other. This yields $3 G_F(t, t') G_F(t', t')^2$.
However, the standard convention is that for $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$, one $q(t')$ connects to $q(t)$ and the remaining two $q(t')$ connect to each other. This gives $3 G_F(t, t') G_F(t', t')$. (The $G_F(t',t')$ term is the 'tadpole' loop.)
So, $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 3 G_F(t, t') G_F(t', t')$.
Thus, the numerator's leading term is $\frac{g}{6} \int dt' (3 G_F(t, t') G_F(t', t')) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_F(t, t') G_F(t', t')$.

Substituting the properties of $G_F$:
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_F(t, t') \left( \frac{1}{2m} \right) = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt' G_F(t, t')$.
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} \left( \frac{1}{m^2} \right) = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.

The leading term of $G_1(t)$ is $O(g)$.

---
**(ii) Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$**

The connected correlation functions can be obtained from the expansion:
$G_n^c(t_1, \dots, t_n) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} \int dt'_1 \dots dt'_k \langle q(t_1) \dots q(t_n) (-S_{int}(t'_1)) \dots (-S_{int}(t'_k)) \rangle_{0, conn}$.
Here $S_{int}(t') = \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3$.

**Order $g^0$:**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^0} = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_{0, conn} = G_F(t_1, t_2)$.

**Order $g^1$:**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^1} = \frac{1}{1!} \int dt' \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) (-S_{int}(t')) \rangle_{0, conn}$
$= \int dt' \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \left( \frac{g}{6} q(t')^3 \right) \rangle_{0, conn}$.
For $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t')^3 \rangle_{0, conn}$, the only connected contraction involves $q(t_1)$ connecting to one $q(t')$, $q(t_2)$ connecting to another $q(t')$, and the remaining $q(t')$ forming a loop with itself. There are 3 ways to choose which $q(t')$ connects to $q(t_1)$, and 2 ways to choose which $q(t')$ connects to $q(t_2)$, and 1 way for the last $q(t')$ to form a loop. So $3 \times 2 \times 1 = 6$ ways. No, this is incorrect.
The $q(t_1)$ can connect to one of the $q(t')$ (3 ways). $q(t_2)$ can connect to one of the remaining $q(t')$ (2 ways). The last $q(t')$ forms a loop ($1$ way).
So $3 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t')$. (This is the "tadpole insertion" diagram).
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^1} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' (3 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t')) = \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t')$.
Using $G_F(t', t') = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^1} = \frac{g}{4m} \int dt' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t')$.

**Order $g^2$:**
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^2} = \frac{1}{2!} \int dt' dt'' \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) (-S_{int}(t')) (-S_{int}(t'')) \rangle_{0, conn}$
$= \frac{1}{2} \int dt' dt'' \left( \frac{g}{6} \right)^2 \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t')^3 q(t'')^3 \rangle_{0, conn}$
$= \frac{g^2}{72} \int dt' dt'' \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t')^3 q(t'')^3 \rangle_{0, conn}$.
We need to sum all connected contractions for $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t')^3 q(t'')^3 \rangle_0$.
There are three types of connected diagrams:
1.  **Self-energy insertion:** $q(t_1)$ connects to one $q(t')$, $q(t_2)$ connects to one $q(t'')$, and the remaining lines form a loop connecting $t'$ and $t''$.
    The contraction is $G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') G_F(t', t') G_F(t'', t'')$.
    Number of ways to contract:
    - $q(t_1)$ to one of $q(t')^3$ (3 ways).
    - $q(t_2)$ to one of $q(t'')^3$ (3 ways).
    - One of the remaining $q(t')^2$ to one of the remaining $q(t'')^2$ (2 ways for $q(t')$ and 2 ways for $q(t'')$, so $2 \times 2 = 4$ ways).
    - The last $q(t')$ loops with itself (1 way).
    - The last $q(t'')$ loops with itself (1 way).
    Total ways: $3 \times 3 \times 4 \times 1 \times 1 = 36$.
    Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt' dt'' (36 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') G_F(t', t') G_F(t'', t''))$.
    Using $G_F(t', t') = G_F(t'', t'') = \frac{1}{2m}$:
    $= \frac{g^2}{2} \left( \frac{1}{2m} \right)^2 \int dt' dt'' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \int dt' dt'' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'')$.

2.  **Vertex correction (type 1):** Both $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$ connect to $q(t')$, and the remaining lines connect $q(t')$ to $q(t'')$.
    The contraction is $G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'') G_F(t'', t'')^2$.
    Number of ways to contract:
    - $q(t_1)$ to one of $q(t')^3$ (3 ways).
    - $q(t_2)$ to one of the remaining $q(t')^2$ (2 ways).
    - The last $q(t')$ to one of $q(t'')^3$ (1 way for $q(t')$ and 3 ways for $q(t'')$, so $1 \times 3 = 3$ ways).
    - The remaining two $q(t'')^2$ loop with themselves (1 way).
    Total ways: $3 \times 2 \times 3 \times 1 = 18$.
    Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt' dt'' (18 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'') G_F(t'', t'')^2)$.
    Using $G_F(t'', t'') = \frac{1}{2m}$:
    $= \frac{g^2}{4} \left( \frac{1}{2m} \right)^2 \int dt' dt'' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'') = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt' dt'' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'')$.

3.  **Vertex correction (type 2):** Both $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$ connect to $q(t'')$, and the remaining lines connect $q(t'')$ to $q(t')$.
    This is symmetric to type 2 by swapping $t'$ and $t''$.
    The contraction is $G_F(t_1, t'') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t'', t') G_F(t', t')^2$.
    Number of ways: 18.
    Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt' dt'' G_F(t_1, t'') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'')$.

Summing these terms for $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $g^2$:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_F(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g}{4m} \int dt' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t')$
$+ \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt' dt'' \left[ 2 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') + G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'') + G_F(t_1, t'') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') \right]$.
Wait, the self-energy term was $\frac{g^2}{8m^2}$, not $\frac{g^2}{16m^2}$. So it is $2 \times \frac{g^2}{16m^2}$ for the first term.

Thus,
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2)|_{g^2} = \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt' dt'' \left[ 2 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') + G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'') + G_F(t_1, t'') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') \right]$.

The total connected 2-point function up to $g^2$ is:

The final answer is $\boxed{
\begin{aligned}
(i) \quad & G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^3} \\
(ii) \quad & G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_F(t_1, t_2) + \frac{g}{4m} \int dt' G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') \\
& \quad + \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int dt' dt'' \left[ 2 G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') + G_F(t_1, t') G_F(t_2, t') G_F(t', t'') + G_F(t_1, t'') G_F(t_2, t'') G_F(t', t'') \right]
\end{aligned}
}$
