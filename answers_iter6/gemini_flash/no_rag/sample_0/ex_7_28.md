The problem asks us to calculate the leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$, and the connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_c$ up to and including order $g^2$. The system is a Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator with potential $U(q) = m^2 q^2 / 2 - g q^3 / 6$.

We will use the path integral formulation. The Euclidean action is $S_E[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + U(q) \right)$.
Substituting $U(q)$, we get $S_E[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 - \frac{g}{6} q^3 \right)$.
We split the action into a free part $S_0[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} \dot{q}^2 + \frac{m^2}{2} q^2 \right)$ and an interaction part $S_{int}[q] = -\frac{g}{6} \int dt q^3(t)$.
The path integral averages are defined as $\langle O[q] \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q O[q] \exp(-S_E[q])}{\int \mathcal{D}q \exp(-S_E[q])}$.
We expand $\exp(-S_E[q]) = \exp(-S_0[q]) \exp(-S_{int}[q]) = \exp(-S_0[q]) (1 - S_{int}[q] + \frac{1}{2} S_{int}[q]^2 - \dots)$.
Averages with respect to the free action $S_0[q]$ are denoted by $\langle \cdot \rangle_0$. For the free harmonic oscillator, $\langle q(t)^n \rangle_0 = 0$ for odd $n$, due to the $q \to -q$ symmetry.

The free propagator (2-point function) is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0$. In Euclidean time, this is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$. An important special case is $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m}$.

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle$ with respect to $g$.**

$G_1(t) = \frac{\langle q(t) (1 - S_{int} + \dots) \rangle_0}{\langle 1 - S_{int} + \dots \rangle_0}$.
Since $\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$ and $\langle S_{int} \rangle_0 = \langle -\frac{g}{6} \int ds q(s)^3 \rangle_0 = 0$, the expansion simplifies:
$G_1(t) = \frac{\langle q(t) \rangle_0 - \langle q(t) S_{int} \rangle_0 + O(g^2)}{1 - \langle S_{int} \rangle_0 + O(g^2)} = \frac{0 - \langle q(t) S_{int} \rangle_0 + O(g^2)}{1 - 0 + O(g^2)}$.
$G_1(t) = - \langle q(t) S_{int} \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.
Substituting $S_{int} = -\frac{g}{6} \int ds q(s)^3$:
$G_1(t) = - \langle q(t) \left( -\frac{g}{6} \int ds q(s)^3 \right) \rangle_0 + O(g^2) = \frac{g}{6} \int ds \langle q(t) q(s)^3 \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.
Using Wick's theorem for the free 4-point function $\langle q(t) q(s) q(s) q(s) \rangle_0$:
There are $3!/1! = 3$ ways to pair $q(t)$ with one $q(s)$, and the remaining two $q(s)$'s pair with each other.
$\langle q(t) q(s)^3 \rangle_0 = 3 G_0(t,s) G_0(s,s)$.
So, $G_1(t) = \frac{g}{6} \int ds \left( 3 G_0(t,s) G_0(s,s) \right) + O(g^2) = \frac{g}{2} \int ds G_0(t,s) G_0(s,s) + O(g^2)$.
Substituting $G_0(s,s) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{2} \frac{1}{2m} \int ds G_0(t,s) + O(g^2) = \frac{g}{4m} \int ds \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-s|} + O(g^2)$.
The integral $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} ds e^{-m|t-s|} = \frac{2}{m}$.
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m} \frac{1}{2m} \frac{2}{m} + O(g^2) = \frac{g}{4m^3} + O(g^2)$.
The leading term for $G_1(t)$ is constant, as expected for a translationally invariant system in its ground state.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function is related to the generating functional $W[J] = \ln Z[J]$, where $Z[J] = \int \mathcal{D}q \exp(-S_E[q] + \int dt J(t)q(t))$.
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \frac{\delta^2 W[J]}{\delta J(t_1) \delta J(t_2)} \Big|_{J=0}$.
$W[J] = \ln \left( \langle \exp(-S_{int} + \int Jq) \rangle_0 \right)$.
Let $A = \langle \exp(-S_{int} + \int Jq) \rangle_0$. We expand $A$ in powers of $g$ and $J$:
$\exp(-S_{int}) = 1 - S_{int} + \frac{1}{2} S_{int}^2 - \dots$
$\exp(\int Jq) = 1 + \int Jq + \frac{1}{2} (\int Jq)^2 + \frac{1}{6} (\int Jq)^3 + \frac{1}{24} (\int Jq)^4 + \dots$
Since $\langle q^n \rangle_0 = 0$ for odd $n$, many terms vanish. For $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$, we need terms in $A$ that are $O(J^2)$.
$A = 1 + \frac{1}{2} \langle (\int Jq)^2 \rangle_0 + \frac{1}{24} \langle (\int Jq)^4 \rangle_0 + \dots$ (from $\exp(\int Jq)$)
$+ \langle -S_{int} \int Jq \rangle_0 + \langle -\frac{1}{6} S_{int} (\int Jq)^3 \rangle_0 + \dots$ (from $-S_{int} \exp(\int Jq)$)
$+ \frac{1}{2} \langle S_{int}^2 \rangle_0 + \frac{1}{4} \langle S_{int}^2 (\int Jq)^2 \rangle_0 + \dots$ (from $\frac{1}{2} S_{int}^2 \exp(\int Jq)$)
Terms like $\langle S_{int} \rangle_0$, $\langle S_{int}^3 \rangle_0$, $\langle (\int Jq)^3 \rangle_0$, $\langle S_{int} (\int Jq)^n \rangle_0$ for even $n$, etc., are zero due to odd number of $q$'s.
$W[J] = \ln A = (A-1) - \frac{1}{2}(A-1)^2 + \frac{1}{3}(A-1)^3 - \dots$

Let's find the coefficient of $\frac{1}{2} J(t_1) J(t_2)$ in $W[J]$ up to $O(g^2)$.

**Order $g^0$**:
From $\frac{1}{2} \langle (\int Jq)^2 \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2} \int ds_1 ds_2 J(s_1) J(s_2) G_0(s_1,s_2)$.
The coefficient of $\frac{1}{2} J(t_1) J(t_2)$ is $G_0(t_1,t_2)$.

**Order $g^1$**:
The only term in $A-1$ with one $g$ and two $J$'s is $\langle -S_{int} (\int Jq) \rangle_0$. No, this term has only one $J$.
$W[J]$ does not have $J^2$ terms at $O(g)$ from $A-1$.
Let's consider the full expression for $G_2^c(t_1,t_2) = \langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \rangle - \langle q(t_1) \rangle \langle q(t_2) \rangle$.
$\langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \rangle = \frac{\langle q(t_1)q(t_2) (1 - S_{int} + \frac{1}{2}S_{int}^2) \rangle_0}{1 + \frac{1}{2}\langle S_{int}^2 \rangle_0} + O(g^3)$.
$\langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \rangle_0 = G_0(t_1,t_2)$.
$\langle q(t_1)q(t_2) (-S_{int}) \rangle_0 = \langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \frac{g}{6} \int ds q(s)^3 \rangle_0$.
Using Wick's theorem: $q(t_1)$ pairs with $q(s)$, $q(t_2)$ pairs with $q(s)$, and $q(s)$ pairs with $q(s)$. There are $3 \times 2 \times 1 = 6$ ways.
So this term is $\frac{g}{6} \int ds (6 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,s)) = g \int ds G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,s)$.
This is the $O(g^1)$ contribution to $\langle q(t_1)q(t_2) \rangle$.
So $G_2^c(t_1,t_2)$ at $O(g^1)$ is $g \int ds G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,s) = \frac{g}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)$.

**Order $g^2$**:
Terms contributing to $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ at $O(g^2)$:
1. From $A-1$: $\frac{1}{4} \langle S_{int}^2 (\int Jq)^2 \rangle_0 = \frac{g^2}{144} \int ds du dt_a dt_b J(t_a) J(t_b) \langle q(s)^3 q(u)^3 q(t_a) q(t_b) \rangle_0$.
   The coefficient of $\frac{1}{2}J(t_1)J(t_2)$ is $\frac{g^2}{72} \int ds du \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(s)^3 q(u)^3 \rangle_0$.
   We need to contract $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(s_1) q(s_2) q(s_3) q(u_1) q(u_2) q(u_3) \rangle_0$.
   The relevant connected diagrams are:
   a. $t_1 \leftrightarrow s$, $t_2 \leftrightarrow u$, with $s \leftrightarrow u$ and two tadpoles $s \leftrightarrow s$, $u \leftrightarrow u$.
      $9 \times G_0(t_1,s) G_0(s,u) G_0(u,t_2) G_0(s,s) G_0(u,u)$.
   b. $t_1 \leftrightarrow s$, $t_2 \leftrightarrow s$, with $s \leftrightarrow u$ and two tadpoles $u \leftrightarrow u$.
      $18 \times G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,s) G_0(s,u) G_0(u,u)^2$.
   c. Symmetric to b: $t_1 \leftrightarrow u$, $t_2 \leftrightarrow u$, with $u \leftrightarrow s$ and two tadpoles $s \leftrightarrow s$.
      $18 \times G_0(t_1,u) G_0(t_2,u) G_0(u,s) G_0(s,s)^2$.
   d. $t_1 \leftrightarrow s$, $t_2 \leftrightarrow u$, with two propagators $s \leftrightarrow u$.
      $18 \times G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,u) G_0(s,u)^2$.
   So this part is $\frac{g^2}{72} \int ds du \left( 9 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(s,u)G_0(u,t_2)G_0(s,s)G_0(u,u) \right.$
   $\left. + 18 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,u)G_0(u,u)^2 + 18 G_0(t_1,u)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(u,s)G_0(s,s)^2 \right.$
   $\left. + 18 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(s,u)^2 \right)$.

2. From $-\frac{1}{2}(A-1)^2$: we need terms with $J^2$. The only relevant term is $-\frac{1}{2} (\langle -S_{int} \int Jq \rangle_0)^2$.
   $\langle -S_{int} \int Jq \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{2} \int ds dt' J(t') G_0(s,t') G_0(s,s)$.
   So, $-\frac{1}{2} \left( \frac{g}{2} \int ds_1 dt_1 J(t_1) G_0(s_1,t_1) G_0(s_1,s_1) \right) \left( \frac{g}{2} \int ds_2 dt_2 J(t_2) G_0(s_2,t_2) G_0(s_2,s_2) \right)$.
   The coefficient of $\frac{1}{2} J(t_1) J(t_2)$ is $-\frac{g^2}{4} \int ds_1 ds_2 G_0(s_1,t_1) G_0(s_1,s_1) G_0(s_2,t_2) G_0(s_2,s_2)$.
   This term corresponds to $-\langle q(t_1) \rangle_g \langle q(t_2) \rangle_g$, where $\langle q(t) \rangle_g = \frac{g}{2} \int ds G_0(t,s) G_0(s,s)$.

Combining these results and substituting $G_0(s,s) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$G_2^c(t_1,t_2) = G_0(t_1,t_2)$
$+ \frac{g}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,s)$
$+ \frac{g^2}{72} \int ds du \left( 9 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(s,u)G_0(u,t_2) (\frac{1}{2m})^2 \right.$
$\left. + 18 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,u) (\frac{1}{2m})^2 + 18 G_0(t_1,u)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(u,s) (\frac{1}{2m})^2 \right.$
$\left. + 18 G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(s,u)^2 \right)$
$- \frac{g^2}{4} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s) (\frac{1}{2m}) G_0(t_2,u) (\frac{1}{2m})$.

Simplifying the $O(g^2)$ terms:
$G_2^c(t_1,t_2) = G_0(t_1,t_2) - \frac{g}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,s)$
$+ \frac{g^2}{288m^2} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s)G_0(s,u)G_0(u,t_2)$
$+ \frac{g^2}{144m^2} \int ds du (G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,u) + G_0(t_1,u)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(u,s))$
$+ \frac{g^2}{4} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(s,u)^2$
$- \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,u)$.

The term $\frac{g}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)$ has a positive sign because the interaction term in the action is $-g/6 q^3$, so the vertex factor is $g/6$. For the self-energy, it's $-g/6 \times 3! G_0(s,s) = -g G_0(s,s)$. So the first correction to propagator is $G_0 \Sigma G_0 = G_0 (-g G_0(s,s)) G_0$. This is $-g \frac{1}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,s)$. My previous calculation had a sign error.

Final answers:
(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ with respect to $g$:
$G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^3}$.

(ii) The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$:
$G_2^c(t_1,t_2) = G_0(t_1,t_2) - \frac{g}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,s)$
$+ \frac{g^2}{288m^2} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s)G_0(s,u)G_0(u,t_2)$
$+ \frac{g^2}{72m^2} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,s)G_0(s,u)$
$+ \frac{g^2}{4} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s)G_0(t_2,u)G_0(s,u)^2$
$- \frac{g^2}{16m^2} \int ds du G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,u)$.
where $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.

The final answer is $\boxed{
(i) G_1(t) = \frac{g}{4m^3}
(ii) G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1,t_2) - \frac{g}{2m} \int ds G_0(t_1,s) G_0(t_2,s)
