The problem asks for two quantities related to a Euclidean cubic anharmonic oscillator using perturbation theory in the coupling constant $g$. The potential is $U(q) = \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6}$.
The Euclidean action is $S[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} (\frac{dq}{dt})^2 + U(q) \right) = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} (\frac{dq}{dt})^2 + \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} - \frac{g q^3}{6} \right)$.
We split the action into a free part $S_0[q]$ and an interaction part $S_I[q]$:
$S_0[q] = \int dt \left( \frac{1}{2} (\frac{dq}{dt})^2 + \frac{m^2 q^2}{2} \right)$
$S_I[q] = - \int dt \frac{g q^3}{6} = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' q(t')^3$.

Expectation values are given by $\langle O \rangle = \frac{\int \mathcal{D}q \, O[q] \, e^{-S_0[q]} e^{-S_I[q]}}{\int \mathcal{D}q \, e^{-S_0[q]} e^{-S_I[q]}} = \frac{\langle O e^{-S_I} \rangle_0}{\langle e^{-S_I} \rangle_0}$, where $\langle \cdot \rangle_0$ denotes expectation values with respect to the free action $S_0$.
We use the expansion $e^{-S_I} = 1 - S_I + \frac{1}{2} S_I^2 - \dots$.
The free propagator is $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
A key property is $G_0(t,t) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t|} = \frac{1}{2m}$.

**Part (i): Leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t) = <q(t)>$ with respect to g.**

$G_1(t) = \frac{\langle q(t) (1 - S_I + \frac{1}{2} S_I^2 - \dots) \rangle_0}{\langle 1 - S_I + \frac{1}{2} S_I^2 - \dots \rangle_0}$.
First, evaluate the denominator $Z = \langle e^{-S_I} \rangle_0$:
$\langle S_I \rangle_0 = \langle \frac{g}{6} \int dt' q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.
By Wick's theorem, $\langle q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = 0$ as it involves an odd number of fields.
So $Z = 1 + O(g^2)$. To leading order in $g$, $Z=1$.

Next, evaluate the numerator $N(t) = \langle q(t) e^{-S_I} \rangle_0$:
$N(t) = \langle q(t) \rangle_0 - \langle q(t) S_I \rangle_0 + O(g^2)$.
$\langle q(t) \rangle_0 = 0$.
The leading contribution is from the $S_I$ term:
$- \langle q(t) S_I \rangle_0 = - \langle q(t) \frac{g}{6} \int dt' q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = - \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0$.
Using Wick's theorem for $\langle q(t) q(t')^3 \rangle_0 = \langle q(t) q(t') q(t') q(t') \rangle_0$:
There are $3$ ways to contract $q(t)$ with one of the $q(t')$ fields, and the remaining two $q(t')$ fields must contract with each other. This yields $3 \times G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
So, $- \frac{g}{6} \int dt' \, 3 G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t') = - \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_0(t, t') G_0(t', t')$.
Substituting $G_0(t', t') = \frac{1}{2m}$:
$G_1(t) = - \frac{g}{2} \int dt' G_0(t, t') \frac{1}{2m} = - \frac{g}{4m} \int dt' G_0(t, t')$.
The integral $\int dt' G_0(t, t')$ for an infinite time range is:
$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} dt' \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t-t'|} = \frac{1}{2m} \left( \int_{-\infty}^t e^{m(t'-t)} dt' + \int_t^\infty e^{-m(t'-t)} dt' \right)$
$= \frac{1}{2m} \left( \frac{1}{m} e^{m(t'-t)} \Big|_{-\infty}^t - \frac{1}{m} e^{-m(t'-t)} \Big|_t^\infty \right) = \frac{1}{2m} \left( \frac{1}{m} (1-0) - \frac{1}{m} (0-1) \right) = \frac{1}{2m} \left( \frac{2}{m} \right) = \frac{1}{m^2}$.
Thus, the leading term of $G_1(t)$ is:
$G_1(t) = - \frac{g}{4m} \frac{1}{m^2} = - \frac{g}{4m^3}$.

**Part (ii): Connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) := <q(t_1) q(t_2)>_c$ modulo $g^3$.**

The connected 2-point function is defined as $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle - \langle q(t_1) \rangle \langle q(t_2) \rangle$.
We need to calculate $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ up to $O(g^2)$.
$\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle = \frac{\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) (1 - S_I + \frac{1}{2} S_I^2) \rangle_0}{1 + \frac{1}{2} \langle S_I^2 \rangle_0} + O(g^3)$.
Using $(1+x)^{-1} \approx 1-x$ for small $x$:
$\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle = \left( \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 - \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) S_I \rangle_0 + \frac{1}{2} \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) S_I^2 \rangle_0 \right) \left( 1 - \frac{1}{2} \langle S_I^2 \rangle_0 \right) + O(g^3)$.
$\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 - \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) S_I \rangle_0 + \frac{1}{2} \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) S_I^2 \rangle_0 - \frac{1}{2} \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 \langle S_I^2 \rangle_0 + O(g^3)$.

Let's evaluate the terms:
1.  **$O(g^0)$ term:** $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 = G_0(t_1, t_2)$.

2.  **$O(g^1)$ term:** $- \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) S_I \rangle_0 = - \frac{g}{6} \int dt_3 \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_3)^3 \rangle_0$.
    By Wick's theorem, we contract $q(t_1), q(t_2)$ with two of $q(t_3)$ and the remaining $q(t_3)$ with itself. There are $3$ ways to choose which $q(t_3)$ self-contracts.
    So, $3 \times G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_3)$.
    This term is $- \frac{g}{6} \int dt_3 \, 3 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_3) = - \frac{g}{2} \int dt_3 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_3)$.
    Substituting $G_0(t_3, t_3) = \frac{1}{2m}$:
    $- \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_3 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3)$.

3.  **$O(g^2)$ terms:**
    The connected part of $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle$ at $O(g^2)$ comes from:
    $\frac{1}{2} \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) S_I^2 \rangle_0 - \frac{1}{2} \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle_0 \langle S_I^2 \rangle_0$.
    This combination subtracts all disconnected vacuum bubble diagrams. The remaining terms are the connected diagrams.
    $S_I^2 = (\frac{g}{6})^2 \int dt_3 dt_4 q(t_3)^3 q(t_4)^3 = \frac{g^2}{36} \int dt_3 dt_4 q(t_3)^3 q(t_4)^3$.
    We need to identify the connected contractions of $\langle q(t_1) q(t_2) q(t_3)^3 q(t_4)^3 \rangle_0$. There are $2+3+3=8$ fields.
    Diagrammatically, these are:
    a)  $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$ connect to the same vertex (say $t_3$), the remaining leg of $t_3$ connects to $t_4$, and the remaining two legs of $t_4$ form a loop.
        There are $3 \times 2$ ways to connect $q(t_1)$ and $q(t_2)$ to $q(t_3)^3$.
        There are $3$ ways for the remaining $q(t_3)$ to connect to $q(t_4)^3$.
        There are $2 \times 1$ ways for the remaining two $q(t_4)$ to form a loop.
        Total ways: $6 \times 3 \times 2 = 36$.
        This gives $36 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_4) G_0(t_4, t_4)$.
        Due to symmetry ($t_3 \leftrightarrow t_4$), we have an identical term $36 G_0(t_1, t_4) G_0(t_2, t_4) G_0(t_4, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_3)$.
        Sum: $72 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_4) G_0(t_4, t_4)$.
        Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt_3 dt_4 \left( 72 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_4) G_0(t_4, t_4) \right)$
        $= g^2 \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_4) \frac{1}{2m}$.

    b)  $q(t_1)$ connects to $t_3$, $q(t_2)$ connects to $t_4$. The remaining two legs of $t_3$ connect to the remaining two legs of $t_4$ (i.e., a box diagram).
        There are $3 \times 3$ ways for $q(t_1)$ to $q(t_3)$ and $q(t_2)$ to $q(t_4)$.
        There are $2 \times 2$ ways for the remaining $q(t_3)^2$ and $q(t_4)^2$ to connect.
        Total ways: $9 \times 4 = 36$.
        Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt_3 dt_4 \left( 36 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4) G_0(t_3, t_4)^2 \right)$
        $= \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4) G_0(t_3, t_4)^2$.

    c)  $q(t_1)$ connects to $t_3$, $q(t_2)$ connects to $t_4$. The remaining two legs of $t_3$ form a loop, and the remaining two legs of $t_4$ form a loop.
        There are $3 \times 3$ ways for $q(t_1)$ to $q(t_3)$ and $q(t_2)$ to $q(t_4)$.
        There are $2 \times 1$ ways for $q(t_3)^2$ to form a loop, and $2 \times 1$ ways for $q(t_4)^2$ to form a loop.
        Total ways: $9 \times 2 \times 2 = 36$.
        Contribution: $\frac{g^2}{72} \int dt_3 dt_4 \left( 36 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4) G_0(t_3, t_3) G_0(t_4, t_4) \right)$
        $= \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4) \frac{1}{2m} \frac{1}{2m} = \frac{g^2}{8m^2} \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4)$.
        Since $\int dt' G_0(t,t') = 1/m^2$, the integral is $(1/m^2)^2 = 1/m^4$.
        So this term is $\frac{g^2}{8m^2} \frac{1}{m^4} = \frac{g^2}{8m^6}$.

Now, we combine these terms to get $G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = \langle q(t_1) q(t_2) \rangle - G_1(t_1) G_1(t_2)$.
$G_1(t_1) G_1(t_2) = (- \frac{g}{4m^3})^2 = \frac{g^2}{16m^6}$. This term is of $O(g^2)$.

Collecting all terms for $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ up to $O(g^2)$:
$G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2)$
$- \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_3 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3)$
$+ g^2 \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_4) \frac{1}{2m}$
$+ \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4) G_0(t_3, t_4)^2$
$+ \frac{g^2}{8m^6} - \frac{g^2}{16m^6}$.

The last two terms combine: $\frac{g^2}{8m^6} - \frac{g^2}{16m^6} = \frac{2g^2 - g^2}{16m^6} = \frac{g^2}{16m^6}$.

Final result:

(i) The leading term of the 1-point function $G_1(t)$ is:
$\boxed{G_1(t) = - \frac{g}{4m^3}}$

(ii) The connected 2-point function $G_2^c(t_1, t_2)$ modulo $g^3$ is:
$\boxed{G_2^c(t_1, t_2) = G_0(t_1, t_2) - \frac{g}{4m} \int dt_3 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) + \frac{g^2}{2m} \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_3) G_0(t_3, t_4) + \frac{g^2}{2} \int dt_3 dt_4 G_0(t_1, t_3) G_0(t_2, t_4) G_0(t_3, t_4)^2 + \frac{g^2}{16m^6}}$
where $G_0(t_1, t_2) = \frac{1}{2m} e^{-m|t_1-t_2|}$.
