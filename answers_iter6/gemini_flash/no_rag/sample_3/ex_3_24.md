**Part 1: 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$**

The action is $S(x) = x^2/2 - g(x^2 + x^3/6)$.
The partition function is $Z = \int e^{-S(x)/\hbar} dx$.
The free partition function is $Z_0 = \int e^{-S_0(x)/\hbar} dx$, where $S_0(x) = x^2/2$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ is typically obtained by expanding the action around its classical minimum and performing a Gaussian integral. For small $g$, the classical minimum $x_c$ is at $x=0$.
$S'(x) = x - g(2x + x^2/2)$. Setting $S'(x)=0$ yields $x(1 - 2g - gx/2) = 0$. For small $g$, $x_c=0$ is the relevant minimum.
The action expanded around $x_c=0$ is:
$S(x) = S(0) + S'(0)x + S''(0)x^2/2 + S'''(0)x^3/6 + \dots$
$S(0)=0$.
$S'(0)=0$.
$S''(x) = 1 - 2g - gx$, so $S''(0) = 1-2g$.
$S'''(x) = -g$, so $S'''(0) = -g$.
Thus, $S(x) = (1-2g)x^2/2 - g x^3/6$.

The 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ in quantum mechanics corresponds to the leading order in $\hbar$ (i.e., $\hbar^0$) in the expansion of $\log(Z/Z_0)$.
In a path integral, this is usually given by $\log(Z/Z_0) \approx -S(x_c)/\hbar - 1/2 \log(S''(x_c)/\hbar) + 1/2 \log(S_0''(0)/\hbar)$.
For $x_c=0$:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -1/2 \log(S''(0)) + 1/2 \log(S_0''(0))$.
Since $S_0''(0)=1$ (from $S_0(x)=x^2/2$), and $S''(0) = 1-2g$:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -1/2 \log(1-2g)$.

Expanding this for small $g$:
$\log(Z/Z_0) = -1/2 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} (-2g)^n = -1/2 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} (-1)^n (2g)^n = -1/2 \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{-1}{n} (2g)^n = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{2^n}{n} g^n$.
This is the 1-loop contribution to $\log(Z/Z_0)$ as a series in $g$. Let $A_n = 2^n/n$ be the coefficient of $g^n$.

**Part 2: Number of labeled $n$-vertex 1-loop graphs with 1-valent and 3-valent vertices only**

For a connected graph, the number of loops is given by $L = E - V + 1$. For a 1-loop graph, $L=1$, so $E-V+1=1$, which implies $E=V$.
Let $n_1$ be the number of 1-valent vertices and $n_3$ be the number of 3-valent vertices.
The total number of vertices is $V = n_1 + n_3$.
The sum of degrees of all vertices is $1 \cdot n_1 + 3 \cdot n_3 = n_1 + 3n_3$.
By the handshaking lemma, the sum of degrees is $2E$.
So, $n_1 + 3n_3 = 2E$.
Since $E=V$, we have $n_1 + 3n_3 = 2(n_1 + n_3)$.
This simplifies to $n_1 + 3n_3 = 2n_1 + 2n_3$, which implies $n_3 = n_1$.
Therefore, for such graphs to exist, $n_1$ must equal $n_3$, and the total number of vertices $n = n_1+n_3 = 2n_1$ must be an even number. If $n$ is odd, $C_n=0$.
